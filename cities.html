Indianapolis, IN

Government & Policy

A simmering dispute over how to fund Indianapolis road repairs has broken into open conflict between Mayor Joe Hogsett and a majority bloc on the City-County Council. A dozen council Democrats are sponsoring a vehicle tax increase to generate roughly $50 million annually for road work — a proposal Hogsett has firmly opposed, arguing the city can meet its funding obligations without raising taxes on residents. His alternative relies on redirecting $20 million already in the budget, tapping $5 million in stormwater fees, and drawing an additional $25 million from supplemental property tax receipts the city receives from the state each spring. With council committee hearings on the measure scheduled through mid-June, the outcome will determine how the city finances one of its most politically contentious infrastructure needs heading into the second half of the year.

Economics & Public Safety

The Indianapolis City-County Council advanced a $19.49 million supplemental fiscal package directing income tax revenue toward a broad range of city priorities, including road repairs, housing stability programs, and community services. Hendricks County Prosecutor Loren Delp escalated that debate by releasing decades of tracking data arguing that crime from Marion County is actively spreading into surrounding suburban communities — a claim that has added pressure on city leaders to address public safety more aggressively as the region competes for business investment.

Nashville, TN

Government & Budget

Mayor Freddie O'Connell celebrated the passage of Nashville's Fiscal Year 2027 operating budget in mid-June, calling it the most ambitious affordability package in the city's history. The budget makes the largest-ever local investment in affordable housing and includes a 22% grocery tax cut championed by O'Connell — a policy that will put money directly back in residents' pockets. The passage came after notable friction between the mayor and Metro Council, with Budget Chair Delishia Porterfield filing a competing substitute budget before a final agreement was reached. With revenues running flat, the budget also tightens city spending and bolsters financial reserves to buffer against broader economic uncertainty.

Boston, MA

Government & Politics

Boston's fiscal year 2027 budget process came to a contentious head this month as the City Council passed $11.8 million in amendments to Mayor Michelle Wu's $4.9 billion spending plan — the smallest year-over-year increase since the Great Recession. Wu agreed to most of the council's changes but rejected $1.4 million tied to programs for seniors, immigrants, and the arts, arguing those reallocations would force the transportation department to lay off parking enforcement officers, sign installers, and traffic signal repair crews. The standoff underscores a broader tension between the mayor and councilors over priorities in a year when Boston faces stagnating property tax revenue, a slowdown in new construction, and soaring employee health insurance costs. The council must act before the fiscal year begins July 1.

Harvard University and developer Tishman Speyer celebrated the opening of the Enterprise Research Campus (ERC) in Allston on June 23, marking a milestone in Harvard's long-planned expansion across the Charles River. The opening comes against a softer backdrop for Boston's life-sciences market: lab rents have fallen roughly 20% from their pandemic-era peak as an oversupply of newly built space outpaces leasing demand, a trend local real estate observers say could take several years to fully absorb.

St. Louis, MO

Infrastructure

A massive sinkhole that opened beneath Interstate 44 on June 13 near North Broadway and Biddle Street — roughly a block from the Dome at America's Center — has left a stretch of downtown St. Louis effectively cut off for over a week. The collapse, measuring approximately 35 feet long, 20 feet wide, and 15 feet deep, was triggered by a broken 20-inch water main, with additional smaller pipes failing in a cascade after the initial rupture. Workers have been forced to rethink their repair approach after discovering that bridge support columns were exposed by the hole, complicating the original stabilization timeline. As of June 19, I-44 between the Stan Musial Veterans Memorial Bridge and the Poplar Street Bridge remains closed through at least next weekend, with city officials urging commuters to use Jefferson Avenue and Parnell Street as alternate routes.

Baltimore, MD

Education

Maryland's only all-boys public charter school is racing against the clock as Baltimore Collegiate School for Boys faces a June 30 charter expiration after the Baltimore City school board voted 6–4 in January not to renew it. District officials cited declining student achievement, underfunded special-education services, and financial instability, but school CEO Edwin Avent has filed an appeal and is seeking a court hearing scheduled for August 7. The school first opened in 2015 and reports that nearly 90% of its graduates earn high school diplomas, with half going on to two- or four-year colleges. The fight has galvanized families and community advocates who argue the closure disproportionately harms young Black male students who have few comparable options in the district. Meanwhile, in a separate but related development, Baltimore City Public Schools reached an $8.5 million settlement with charter operators over a yearslong funding dispute, a deal that could signal a new chapter in the contentious relationship between the district and its charter sector.

Read more: Baltimore Brew CBS Baltimore Baltimore Sun

Government & Policy

Baltimore's City Council is pursuing what would be the most sweeping overhaul of the city's charter since 1994, with a package of proposed amendments that would reshape how the city manages its budget, oversight, and infrastructure. A key provision championed by Councilwoman Odette Ramos would move the mayor's budget submission deadline from May 15 to April 15, giving the council 30 additional days to scrutinize departmental spending and hold agency hearings before the fiscal year begins. Separately, city leaders have proposed a charter amendment that would spin off Baltimore's water and wastewater operations into a standalone regional authority, separating them from the Department of Public Works — a structural change aimed at improving accountability and long-term infrastructure investment. All proposed amendments would be placed on the November 2026 ballot for voter ratification.

Charlotte, NC

Government & Politics

Charlotte finds itself in a rare moment of civic transition as the City Council interviewed five finalists on June 18 to replace Mayor Vi Lyles, who is set to resign on June 30. The five candidates — Mayor Pro Tem James Mitchell, former council member Harold Cogdell, state Senator Caleb Theodros, attorney Robert Harrington, and former Federal Reserve vice president Carrie Cook — each made their case to the 11-member council during a public interview session. Orlando, FL

Economics & Business

Orlando's identity as a defense and simulation powerhouse was on vivid display this month as the Orlando Economic Partnership's June Tech Connect report catalogued a flurry of investment activity at and around Central Florida Research Park, home to Boeing, BAE Systems, L3Harris, and Northrop Grumman — part of a modeling, simulation, and training sector that generates roughly $7 billion a year in contracts. VirTra purchased its two-building Research Park campus outright rather than leasing, signaling long-term commitment, while M-tron Industries landed a $6.8 million follow-on production contract for frequency-control components inside counter-drone radar systems. On the finance side, Climate First Bank — a profitable Orlando-based community lender near $2 billion in assets — closed a $67 million institutional funding round led by Wellington Management and is now exploring a potential IPO. The OEP report frames the theme uniting all of it: Orlando has essentially settled the question of whether it can build advanced systems, and the open challenge now is whether its talent pipeline — anchored by UCF's Institute for Simulation and Training and Full Sail's ranked game-design programs — can keep pace with simultaneous demand from defense contractors, two growing theme-park empires, and a maturing finance sector.

Read more: Orlando Economic Partnership — Tech Connect June 18 Orlando Business Journal — Climate First Bank IPO GlobeNewswire — VirTra campus purchase

Government & Housing

Orlando's city council received a progress update in April on Accelerate Orlando, a $58 million initiative that has redirected general revenue funds toward affordable housing and homelessness services. The program's most visible success is Palm Gardens, a former downtown hotel that was converted into 149 updated apartments renting for $1,050 per month including utilities — a project that city officials say cut crime at the site from roughly 400 incidents to 100 since the conversion. Two additional projects are in the pipeline: The Residences at Blossom Trail (140 affordable units, sale to developer pending) and the Villas at Clear Lake (about 100 affordable senior apartments in partnership with Lift Orlando). The city is also contributing $10 million toward a new six-story women's and children's shelter for the Coalition for the Homeless, and is funding a comprehensive day services center at the Christian Service Center — set to open this September — that will offer medical exams, mental health counseling, meals, laundry, mailboxes, and job training to the 350–400 homeless individuals the center serves daily.

Denver, CO

Government & Politics

Denver is deep in the running to host the 2028 Democratic National Convention, having survived a competitive first cut that left five finalists — Atlanta, Boston, Chicago, Philadelphia, and Denver. Party officials conducted a three-day site visit May 5–7, touring City Hall and Ball Arena while Mayor Mike Johnston and Colorado Democrats pitched the city's hospitality credentials and progressive track record. Johnston has estimated the event could inject close to half a billion dollars into the local economy, a figure that carries extra weight as Denver navigates one of its tightest budget cycles in years. The DNC is expected to announce its host city in the first quarter of 2027, and the last time Denver hosted was in 2008, when Barack Obama accepted the nomination before more than 80,000 people at Mile High Stadium.

Read more: Denverite Colorado Newsline CBS Colorado

Labor & City Government

Staff aides to the Denver City Council voted to unionize in early June, joining a wave of municipal workers organizing across the city. The move comes as Denver confronts significant budget pressures — the city has been working through what officials describe as some of the steepest spending cuts in years, with layoffs already affecting several departments. Council aides argue that collective bargaining will give them more stability and a formal voice as the city weighs further belt-tightening measures heading into the next fiscal year.
