Nashville, TN

Government & Politics

Metro Council voted unanimously on July 21 to approve Nashville's first zoning regulations for data centers, capping the giant server farms at 500,000 square feet in Davidson County and requiring buffer zones around schools, hospitals and the Nashville Zoo. The vote followed months of neighborhood pushback over a proposed facility near the zoo and another planned on the campus of Fisk University. Alongside the zoning bill, the council also extended a temporary moratorium on new data center permits through December 1, giving the city more time to finalize rules on power use, water consumption and noise. The package requires operators to file emergency response and power-resiliency plans and to publicly disclose annual compliance reports, a response to fears that rapid data-center growth could strain the city's power grid and water supply. Mayor Freddie O'Connell's administration backed the measures, which supporters say give Nashville leverage it lacked as tech companies scouted sites across Middle Tennessee.

Boston, MA

Government & Public Safety

Boston is grappling with a summer surge in violence after a spate of shootings during the Fourth of July weekend sparked renewed calls for change. Mayor Michelle Wu responded by expanding her summer jobs program, offering paid positions to Boston Public Schools students as part of a broader safety plan meant to keep teens engaged during the peak crime months of summer. The response has drawn a mixed reception on the City Council: Councilor Erin Murphy is requesting a hearing on the plan's effectiveness, and she has joined Councilor Ed Flynn in calling for a citywide public safety summit. Boston Police Department's midyear crime statistics show overall crime roughly flat versus last year, with homicides, sexual assaults, and robberies all trending below average, though non-domestic aggravated assaults have climbed more than 50 incidents to 753. Separately, the Council approved a regional grant supporting gang-violence prevention and adopted a resolution pushing Boston Public Schools to enroll all 103 eligible schools in the state's Safe Routes program.

Economics & Business

Boston's life-sciences sector is showing early signs of stabilizing after a brutal stretch of layoffs. Massachusetts biotech firms cut roughly 346 jobs across seven companies in the second quarter of 2026, the lowest quarterly total in more than two years, though the sector has still shed over 9,500 jobs statewide in the first half of the year amid clinical trial failures and merger-driven consolidation. Commercial real estate is sending a similarly uneven signal: a Texas-based buyer recently purchased a Route 128 office park with redevelopment plans, while one of the Seaport's newest office towers sits largely empty despite its prime waterfront location, underscoring the gap between the region's biotech recovery and its office market. On the small-business side, the city's Office of Small Business celebrated the first graduating class of its BizUnlocked Boston Business Access Program, which connects fourteen local entrepreneurs with resources to grow their companies.

St. Louis, MO

Government

Mayor Cara Spencer used a July 7 announcement to lay out five strategic priorities and 25 underlying initiatives meant to set the city's direction for the next several years, spanning city services, neighborhoods, economic growth, city culture, and government modernization. Among the concrete steps: a new paving and sidewalk pilot program with dedicated funding, formalized "dig once" coordination with utilities to cut repeat street work, and a revamped model for aligning economic-development efforts across city agencies. The administration says it will track the initiatives weekly through its CityStat program, which was just expanded to include the Health Department, Human Services, and the Towing Division. The plan is being built in partnership with the Board of Aldermen and the Comptroller's Office, signaling an effort to keep City Hall's various offices moving in the same direction after a rocky stretch for city governance.

Read more: City of St. Louis — Strategic Priorities announcement First Alert 4 — Mayor unveils priorities

Crime & Justice

St. Louis closed out the first half of 2026 with its lowest homicide total in 12 years, according to St. Louis Public Radio's review of police data, with 63 killings recorded through June 30 compared to 71 over the same span last year. City police say the decline tracks a broader regional drop in violent crime that has held through 2025 and into this year. Even so, officials and residents note that felony thefts have ticked up in 2026, and public perception of safety hasn't caught up with the statistical improvement, a gap the police department has flagged as its next communications challenge. The numbers arrive as the Spencer administration touts public safety as one pillar of its new strategic priorities.

Baltimore, MD

Crime & Justice

Baltimore posted its fewest homicides ever recorded through the first half of a calendar year, Mayor Brandon M. Scott announced July 1, with 50 killings logged from January through June 2026, down 23% from the 65 recorded over the same stretch in 2025. Non-fatal shootings also declined, and the police department's Warrant Apprehension Task Force made 1,006 arrests in the first six months of the year, well above last year's pace. City officials tied part of the improvement to the Community Action Lab, a neighborhood leadership program Scott expanded this month into the Southeastern District after launching it in the Northern District in April. The citywide numbers aren't uniformly positive, though: several individual crime categories have ticked up compared with the same point last year, underscoring how the overall trend can mask uneven experiences from one neighborhood to the next.

Economics & Business

Baltimore's parking-reform-minded City Council granted developer David Bramble's MCB Real Estate a special exception for 65 off-street parking spaces on July 9, nearly double the 36 normally allowed, clearing the way for a Streets Market grocery store on West North Avenue. The store anchors The Shoppes at Reservoir Square, part of MCB's roughly $170 million Reservoir Square development, and many neighbors welcomed the prospect of a full-service supermarket in a stretch of the city that has long gone without one. But the vote frustrated residents of the adjoining Historic Mount Royal Terrace Association and advocates such as Bikemore, who argued the suburban-style lot and a plan to convert a bus lane into metered parking would worsen congestion on the busy corridor. Councilman Ryan Dorsey, an outspoken champion of eliminating parking minimums citywide, voted for the exception anyway, telling colleagues he was "happy to flout the law" to secure the grocery store. Construction on the retail center is expected to finish in 2027.

Charlotte, NC

Government & Politics

Charlotte City Council voted 9-2 in early June to adopt a nearly $4.5 billion Fiscal Year 2027 budget that took effect July 1, with council members Ed Driggs and LaWana Mayfield casting the dissenting votes. The plan raises the property tax rate by 1.89 cents, adding roughly $5.71 a month to the average homeowner's bill, in order to fund 10 percent raises for police and firefighters, 4 percent raises for other city employees, and a citywide minimum wage of $25 an hour. It also authorizes the city's largest-ever affordable housing bond, a $125 million commitment aimed at expanding housing supply as Charlotte continues to grow. City officials say the full budget and capital investment plan are now posted for public review.

Courts & Justice

A former Charlotte FC director has sued the team's parent company, Tepper Sports and Entertainment, alleging he was fired for being kind while female colleagues faced few consequences for far more serious misconduct. Dustin Swinehart, who was terminated in August 2024 after a supervisory lapse at a Charlotte FC youth camp, claims in the suit that women at the organization "got away with drunken behavior, racist comments and dishonest public statements," and that a "powerful female triumvirate" of executives — including owner Nicole Tepper, CEO Kristi Coleman and HR chief Kisha Smith — applied a double standard that was quick to terminate men and lenient toward women. The lawsuit alleges Title VII sex discrimination and wrongful discharge under North Carolina public policy, and seeks a jury trial, back pay and punitive damages. Tepper Sports and Entertainment also owns the Carolina Panthers.

Orlando, FL

Government & Politics

Orange County officials are bracing for steep budget cuts as voters prepare to decide on Florida's Amendment 3, a property tax measure that could strip roughly $165 million from county coffers, including about $30 million from the Sheriff's Office alone. Sheriff John Mina initially requested a $508 million budget before trimming the ask to $470 million amid the uncertainty, while the county's corrections chief warned that staffing reductions could increase risk to public safety and jeopardize jail accreditation. The amendment vote looms over nearly every county department, with fire officials separately cautioning it could slow emergency response times. Commissioners have already passed a tentative $8.9 billion budget, leaving public safety funding in limbo until the amendment's fate is decided at the ballot box.

Denver, CO

Crime & Justice

Denver's overall crime numbers are trending in a reassuring direction, but a sharp rise in killings is complicating the picture. Through July 1, the city recorded a marginal 0.4% dip in total crime, with steeper declines in auto theft, violent gun crime, and robberies, yet homicides have jumped nearly 40% year over year, with 26 killings logged by the same point that saw 19 a year earlier. Denver Police Chief Ron Thomas struck a mixed tone, saying the department is encouraged by the broader decline but "remain[s] concerned with individuals' propensity towards violence and the uptick in homicides committed this year." The divergence has put pressure on city leaders to explain why violent crime overall is easing even as the most serious offense category worsens.

Read more: KDVR: Crime in Denver down except homicides Denver7 Crime Coverage

Economics & Business

City Hall is betting big on downtown's comeback. Denver officials rolled out the Denver Jobs Agenda, a $100 million public-private initiative that includes a $40 million incentive fund aimed at drawing employers back into downtown office space, part of a three-year push to generate 10,000 jobs citywide. The effort comes as downtown's commercial real estate market remains under real strain, with some of the highest office vacancy rates in the country and multiple buildings in or facing foreclosure, according to reporting from The Colorado Sun. Separately, the city approved $4.5 million to help CU Denver build an innovation hub at Independence Plaza, a bet on the same "deep tech" and quantum-computing tenants that brokers say are starting to offset weakness in the traditional office market. Foot traffic downtown, boosted by the completed 16th Street makeover, has recovered to about 95% of 2019 levels even as office demand lags well behind.

Tampa–St. Petersburg, FL

Crime & Justice

A tense hostage-and-ambush case gripped Hillsborough County Thursday after a man who had held his ex-girlfriend captive overnight in Ruskin — binding her with zip ties and threatening to kill her — opened fire on deputies who tracked him down. Sheriff Chad Chronister said deputies used a surveillance drone to locate 34-year-old suspect Chris Dmuchowski before he ambushed them from inside a parked van, shooting deputies Sopharmony Muon and Colton Daniels; a third deputy, Sgt. Harrison Bashner, then shot and killed Dmuchowski. Both injured deputies were airlifted to Tampa General Hospital and remain in treatment. The shooting has renewed attention on officer safety protocols in the county and comes as local agencies continue to lean on drone surveillance during high-risk calls.

Read more: FOX 13 Tampa Bay MySuncoast/ABC 7 Bay News 9

Economics & Development

Tampa's nearly $60 million West Riverwalk Extension hit a visible milestone this month as crews finished drilling roughly 70 support shafts — some nearly 50 feet deep — into the Hillsborough River bed to anchor three new overwater structures: the Platt Street and Brorein Street pedestrian bridges and the Tony Jannus Park observation overlook. The project, now about 20% complete, is meant to stitch together more than a dozen west-side neighborhoods with a continuous 12.2-mile waterfront trail into downtown, with the bulk of the work expected to wrap by mid-2027. City officials framed the engineering milestone as proof the long-planned West River BUILD corridor, which also includes street and sidewalk upgrades along Platt Street and Rome Avenue, is moving from planning into visible construction.
