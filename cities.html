Denver, CO

Crime & Justice

Denver's overall crime numbers are trending in a reassuring direction, but a sharp rise in killings is complicating the picture. Through July 1, the city recorded a marginal 0.4% dip in total crime, with steeper declines in auto theft, violent gun crime, and robberies, yet homicides have jumped nearly 40% year over year, with 26 killings logged by the same point that saw 19 a year earlier. Denver Police Chief Ron Thomas struck a mixed tone, saying the department is encouraged by the broader decline but "remain[s] concerned with individuals' propensity towards violence and the uptick in homicides committed this year." The divergence has put pressure on city leaders to explain why violent crime overall is easing even as the most serious offense category worsens.

Read more: KDVR: Crime in Denver down except homicides Denver7 Crime Coverage

Economics & Business

City Hall is betting big on downtown's comeback. Denver officials rolled out the Denver Jobs Agenda, a $100 million public-private initiative that includes a $40 million incentive fund aimed at drawing employers back into downtown office space, part of a three-year push to generate 10,000 jobs citywide. The effort comes as downtown's commercial real estate market remains under real strain, with some of the highest office vacancy rates in the country and multiple buildings in or facing foreclosure, according to reporting from The Colorado Sun. Separately, the city approved $4.5 million to help CU Denver build an innovation hub at Independence Plaza, a bet on the same "deep tech" and quantum-computing tenants that brokers say are starting to offset weakness in the traditional office market. Foot traffic downtown, boosted by the completed 16th Street makeover, has recovered to about 95% of 2019 levels even as office demand lags well behind.

Tampa–St. Petersburg, FL

Crime & Justice

A tense hostage-and-ambush case gripped Hillsborough County Thursday after a man who had held his ex-girlfriend captive overnight in Ruskin — binding her with zip ties and threatening to kill her — opened fire on deputies who tracked him down. Sheriff Chad Chronister said deputies used a surveillance drone to locate 34-year-old suspect Chris Dmuchowski before he ambushed them from inside a parked van, shooting deputies Sopharmony Muon and Colton Daniels; a third deputy, Sgt. Harrison Bashner, then shot and killed Dmuchowski. Both injured deputies were airlifted to Tampa General Hospital and remain in treatment. The shooting has renewed attention on officer safety protocols in the county and comes as local agencies continue to lean on drone surveillance during high-risk calls.

Read more: FOX 13 Tampa Bay MySuncoast/ABC 7 Bay News 9

Economics & Development

Tampa's nearly $60 million West Riverwalk Extension hit a visible milestone this month as crews finished drilling roughly 70 support shafts — some nearly 50 feet deep — into the Hillsborough River bed to anchor three new overwater structures: the Platt Street and Brorein Street pedestrian bridges and the Tony Jannus Park observation overlook. The project, now about 20% complete, is meant to stitch together more than a dozen west-side neighborhoods with a continuous 12.2-mile waterfront trail into downtown, with the bulk of the work expected to wrap by mid-2027. City officials framed the engineering milestone as proof the long-planned West River BUILD corridor, which also includes street and sidewalk upgrades along Platt Street and Rome Avenue, is moving from planning into visible construction.

San Diego, CA

Government & Politics

San Diego's phased minimum wage increase for hospitality workers took effect July 1, raising base pay at hotels with at least 150 rooms, amusement parks, and event centers above the city's standard $17.25 hourly floor on a path toward $25 over the coming years. The City Council approved the ordinance as part of a broader summer workload that also included certifying the results of the June 2 election and appointing nine new commissioners — among them Jesus Martin Gallegos-Munoz, representing District 8 — to the Commission on Police Practices, the civilian body that reviews San Diego police conduct. Council members are separately weighing whether to call a special election to fill seats representing Districts 2, 4, and 6. Taken together, the actions show a council working through economic policy and police-oversight structures in the same stretch of meetings.

Read more: CBS 8: Hospitality wage increase City Council press releases

Economics & Business

After more than a year of supervised testing across neighborhoods from Pacific Beach to Southcrest, Waymo announced this month that its robotaxis will begin fully driverless operation in San Diego, part of a four-city expansion that also includes Las Vegas, Tampa, and Denver. The rollout starts narrowly, limited to company employees before opening to the public later this year, but it puts San Diego alongside Los Angeles, Phoenix, and other major markets already served by autonomous rideshare. Not everyone is on board: Councilmember Sean Elo-Rivera pushed the regional transit board to formally oppose the driverless expansion without stronger oversight, and the board in turn asked state lawmakers and regulators to intervene before the service scales up. The clash sets up a summer standoff between a fast-moving tech rollout and local officials seeking more guardrails around autonomous vehicles.

Minneapolis–St. Paul, MN

Government & Politics

A Minneapolis City Council committee hearing on July 8 drew an overflow crowd of residents opposing a proposed "drones as first responders" pilot program that would let police dispatch drones to 911 calls ahead of officers in the Fourth Precinct on the city's north side. More than 40 speakers said they don't trust how the department would use the technology, with several raising alarm that the vendor, Skydio, has also supplied drones to the Israeli government. The committee nonetheless advanced the proposal, sending it to the full council for a vote on July 16 — a session that, notably, won't include another public comment period. If approved, the trial could begin as soon as July 20, making it one of the more closely watched surveillance-policy fights at City Hall this summer.

Read more: MPR News CBS Minnesota

Crime & Justice

Minnesota Attorney General Keith Ellison has shut down the state's Conviction Review Unit, the office that investigated potential wrongful convictions statewide, after the Trump administration declined to renew the federal grant that funded it. Created in 2020, the unit had reviewed more than 1,000 applications and helped overturn three wrongful convictions over nearly five years of operation; roughly 160 people currently in prison who were awaiting review are now being notified the unit is closing. With the statewide office suspended, Hennepin County's Conviction Integrity Unit becomes the only independent avenue in Minnesota for reviewing such claims, a gap that defense attorneys and advocates say will be felt hardest by defendants outside the Twin Cities' largest county.

Detroit, MI

Government

Detroit's city council narrowly backed Mayor Mary Sheffield's agenda this month, first by approving a nine-month extension of the ShotSpotter gunshot-detection contract in a heated 5-4 vote, with members split over whether the technology justifies its cost. That decision followed through on the council's earlier approval of a $3 billion budget for the fiscal year that began July 1, which funds several of Sheffield's signature priorities. Among them: a $21.45-an-hour living wage floor for full-time city employees is now showing up in paychecks, with the raise reflected starting in the July 13 pay period for roughly 900 workers, most of whom live in Detroit and work in departments like Public Works and Building Safety.

San Francisco–Oakland, CA

Government & Politics

San Francisco's Fourth of July fireworks show over the Golden Gate Bridge turned into a logistical mess this year, as fog obscured the pyrotechnics and gridlock near the bridge stranded Waymo robotaxis and delayed Muni buses for hours. Supervisors Stephen Sherrill and Bilal Mahmood have opened inquiries into what went wrong, and City Hall insiders point to a specific gap: the retirement last year of Martha Cohen, the city's longtime special-events coordinator, who had run high-profile gatherings from Dianne Feinstein's funeral to APEC and Warriors championship parades since starting at City Hall in the 1990s. Sources describe Cohen's institutional knowledge of event logistics as effectively irreplaceable, with one former colleague noting nobody has matched her ability to anticipate what could go wrong. The episode has become a fresh flashpoint for scrutiny of Mayor Daniel Lurie's City Hall operations, even as his allies notched decisive wins in June's Board of Supervisors elections.

Read more: SF Standard: The event czar's absence SF Standard: How the meltdown unfolded

Economics & Business

San Francisco's housing market has entered what agents are calling a "bidmaxxing" era, with homes selling for at least $1 million over asking price surging 1,700% in the first half of 2026 versus the same period last year. Brokerage Compass counted 144 such sales through June, including 44 in June alone, as buyers — many in tech and finance — race to close deals ahead of IPO-driven stock lockup expirations from companies like OpenAI, Anthropic, and SpaceX. Citywide home prices are up 22.2% year-over-year, the steepest increase of any Bay Area county, with luxury enclaves like Sea Cliff and Pacific Heights posting the sharpest gains as active listings fell to just 225 in May. The frenzy is spilling into the rental market too, where rents are breaking records, pushing some priced-out workers to reconsider buying instead of renting.

Seattle, WA

Economics & Business

Boeing officially launched a new $1 billion 737 MAX assembly line at its Everett factory this month, the first line dedicated to the jet built outside the company's original Renton plant since production consolidated there in 1970. The North Line held its ribbon-cutting on July 10 and is expected to lift monthly 737 MAX output from 42 to 47 planes during an initial phase, climbing toward 52 by early 2027, while adding nearly 1,000 jobs in Everett. The investment comes as Boeing works through a backlog of more than 4,300 unfilled 737 MAX orders and looks to rebuild trust after years of production troubles. For Everett and the broader Seattle metro, the expansion signals renewed confidence in the region's aerospace manufacturing base at a moment when downtown Seattle is grappling with the nation's highest office vacancy rate.

Crime & Justice

Small businesses in Seattle's Little Saigon and other vulnerable neighborhoods say they've reached a breaking point over open-air drug use and organized retail theft, with owners describing losses that outstrip whatever bump they saw from summer tourism, including this year's FIFA World Cup crowds. King County prosecutors say they're now charging organized theft rings on a near-daily basis but need far more staff and funding to keep pace, pointing to the closure of a Fred Meyer in the Lake City neighborhood as a warning of what happens when repeat theft drives grocery access out of a community. Mayor Katie Wilson's administration launched a targeted response last month, adding police patrols, $1.1 million for overdose treatment and outreach, and diversion programs, and the effort has already produced more than 100 arrests. The pressure comes even as citywide data shows overall crime down nearly 2% and shootings down more than 20%, a split picture that has merchants weighing recovery against ongoing losses.

Cleveland, OH

Economics & Business

Mayor Justin Bibb has released a formal request for qualifications inviting developers to reimagine roughly 50 acres of downtown lakefront between West Third and East Ninth streets, the latest step in his long-running Shore-to-Core-to-Shore vision to reconnect the city with Lake Erie. The RFQ follows more than three years of master planning and is financed in part through a downtown tax-increment financing district that officials project could generate as much as $7.5 billion over 42 years. Separately, the city was awarded nearly $60 million in federal funding for the North Coast Connector, a pedestrian bridge intended to link downtown to the waterfront; construction is expected to begin in 2027, backed by $130 million in federal money and $20 million from the state. Together, the moves mark one of the most ambitious pushes yet to redevelop Cleveland's waterfront and strengthen its urban core.

Read more: Downtown Lakefront RFQ North Coast Connector grant

Crime & Justice

Cleveland police have launched STANCE, a new Street and Nuisance Crime Enforcement initiative aimed at cracking down on vehicle break-ins and other quality-of-life offenses across the city. In its opening weeks, officers arrested 23 people, most of them juveniles, suspected of committing car break-ins, part of a broader public-safety push under Mayor Bibb's RISE initiative that officials credit with a 36% drop in homicides. Separately, the FBI is asking Northeast Ohio residents to come forward as potential victims in an alleged real estate Ponzi scheme run by Gregory and Danielle Parker, who face federal wire fraud charges. Investigators say the couple promised high returns on real estate development projects in Cleveland and East Cleveland between 2017 and 2023 before the scheme collapsed.
