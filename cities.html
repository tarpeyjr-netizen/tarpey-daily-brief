Austin, TX

Economics & Development

Austin City Council has approved the annexation of more than 2,600 acres of land east of downtown known as "Dog's Head," a nearly four-square-mile stretch along the Colorado River that city leaders say could reshape Austin's growth for decades. The deal involves a 45-year development agreement with Endeavor Real Estate Group, requiring 20% affordable housing, 266 acres of parks and trails, and new infrastructure — all funded in part through a tax increment financing zone that is now drawing scrutiny from Travis County leaders. City documents estimate the project could generate $3.5 billion in new property tax revenue over 30 years, though environmental advocates raised concerns during council debate about flood risk given the property's proximity to the river. Travis County commissioners are now weighing whether to participate in the tax financing plan, a vote that could determine how quickly the project moves forward.

Media & Culture

The University of Texas at Austin fired KUT Public Media General Manager Debbie Hiott on June 15, ending a tenure she began in 2019 after nearly three decades as a journalist and editor-in-chief at the Austin American-Statesman. The dismissal came weeks after a public dispute over KUT's inaugural community festival, which UT officials abruptly asked the station to relocate just two days before it was scheduled to begin — a decision Hiott publicly pushed back on. Records obtained by KERA News subsequently challenged the university's stated justifications for the firing, raising questions about academic independence and the relationship between UT leadership and the NPR affiliate it controls. Gerald Johnson, a UT Austin finance veteran, has been named interim general manager as the station navigates the fallout.

Richmond, VA

Government & Politics

Virginia's landmark assault weapons ban, signed by Governor Spanberger in May, was dealt a last-minute blow when a state judge granted a preliminary injunction blocking the law from taking effect on its July 1 start date. The injunction, valid through the end of the year or until a final ruling, came after the NRA and several Virginia gun-rights groups filed suit challenging the law's constitutionality. The law would have banned the sale and transfer of certain semi-automatic firearms and magazines holding more than 15 rounds — though it would not have required existing owners to surrender their weapons. The ruling sent shockwaves through Richmond-area gun shops, with at least one local dealer saying it would voluntarily stop selling affected firearms regardless of the injunction. Attorney General Jay Jones said the Commonwealth will appeal, arguing the ban is both constitutional and necessary for public safety. The development comes after dozens of Virginia prosecutors and sheriffs had already declared they would not enforce the law even if it had taken effect.

Economics & Community

Richmond City Council received the results of its community livability survey this month, a broad assessment of how residents feel about life in the city. The findings pointed to a genuine split: residents expressed satisfaction with cultural amenities and recreational opportunities, but flagged worsening perceptions of public safety and the economy compared to 2021 levels. Affordability and public utilities also emerged as persistent concerns. Separately, the city's newly adopted budget reflects ongoing efforts to address those gaps, with allocations including an $80,000 grant to the Richmond Behavioral Health Foundation for the Trauma Healing Response Network — a program targeting residents affected by community violence. The council also authorized a tree improvement agreement with Capital Trees at Byrd Park, a small but symbolic investment in the city's green infrastructure.

San Antonio, TX

Economics & Development

San Antonio's Project Marvel — the city's $4 billion downtown sports and entertainment district anchored by a new Spurs arena — continued to advance this month as the City Council approved two contracts totaling $6.3 million, including hiring an executive program manager. The city is nearing an agreement to acquire the former Institute of Texan Cultures site at Hemisfair for the arena footprint, and community mobility workshops were held June 25 and 27 to gather public input on transportation impacts. Financing for the project is split among the Spurs ($500 million), Bexar County ($311 million in venue tax allocations), and the city itself, which will contribute up to $489 million through bonds repaid by Spurs lease payments, state hotel revenues, and Tax Increment Reinvestment Zone funds. The arena is projected to open by summer 2032.

Read more: San Antonio Report — Project Marvel update KENS5 — Council approves $6.3M contracts

Government & Politics

San Antonio faces a $158 million budget shortfall over the next two years, driven largely by $80 million in new public safety costs tied to collective bargaining agreements with police and fire departments. City Manager Erik Walsh proposed closing the gap with a 6% property tax increase — the maximum allowed without a public vote — but a majority of the City Council pushed back, calling the plan a nonstarter. The standoff leaves council members under pressure to identify specific cuts if they reject the tax hike, with a full draft budget due in August and a final vote on spending and tax rates expected in September. Mayor Gina Ortiz Jones has called for transparency, noting the city must balance fiscal restraint with the costs already locked in by existing labor contracts.

Indianapolis, IN

Government & Policy

A simmering dispute over how to fund Indianapolis road repairs has broken into open conflict between Mayor Joe Hogsett and a majority bloc on the City-County Council. A dozen council Democrats are sponsoring a vehicle tax increase to generate roughly $50 million annually for road work — a proposal Hogsett has firmly opposed, arguing the city can meet its funding obligations without raising taxes on residents. His alternative relies on redirecting $20 million already in the budget, tapping $5 million in stormwater fees, and drawing an additional $25 million from supplemental property tax receipts the city receives from the state each spring. With council committee hearings on the measure scheduled through mid-June, the outcome will determine how the city finances one of its most politically contentious infrastructure needs heading into the second half of the year.

Government & Budget

Mayor Freddie O'Connell celebrated the passage of Nashville's Fiscal Year 2027 operating budget in mid-June, calling it the most ambitious affordability package in the city's history. The budget makes the largest-ever local investment in affordable housing and includes a 22% grocery tax cut championed by O'Connell — a policy that will put money directly back in residents' pockets. The passage came after notable friction between the mayor and Metro Council, with Budget Chair Delishia Porterfield filing a competing substitute budget before a final agreement was reached. With revenues running flat, the budget also tightens city spending and bolsters financial reserves to buffer against broader economic uncertainty.

Boston, MA

Government & Politics

Boston's fiscal year 2027 budget process came to a contentious head this month as the City Council passed $11.8 million in amendments to Mayor Michelle Wu's $4.9 billion spending plan — the smallest year-over-year increase since the Great Recession. Wu agreed to most of the council's changes but rejected $1.4 million tied to programs for seniors, immigrants, and the arts, arguing those reallocations would force the transportation department to lay off parking enforcement officers, sign installers, and traffic signal repair crews. The standoff underscores a broader tension between the mayor and councilors over priorities in a year when Boston faces stagnating property tax revenue, a slowdown in new construction, and soaring employee health insurance costs. The council must act before the fiscal year begins July 1.

St. Louis, MO

Infrastructure

A massive sinkhole that opened beneath Interstate 44 on June 13 near North Broadway and Biddle Street — roughly a block from the Dome at America's Center — has left a stretch of downtown St. Louis effectively cut off for over a week. The collapse, measuring approximately 35 feet long, 20 feet wide, and 15 feet deep, was triggered by a broken 20-inch water main, with additional smaller pipes failing in a cascade after the initial rupture. Workers have been forced to rethink their repair approach after discovering that bridge support columns were exposed by the hole, complicating the original stabilization timeline. As of June 19, I-44 between the Stan Musial Veterans Memorial Bridge and the Poplar Street Bridge remains closed through at least next weekend, with city officials urging commuters to use Jefferson Avenue and Parnell Street as alternate routes.

Government & Public Safety

Mayor Cara Spencer signed Executive Order 97 on June 18, imposing an overnight youth curfew in The Grove neighborhood — formally Forest Park Southeast — for the coming Pride weekend. The curfew runs from 8 p.m. to 5 a.m. on the nights of June 27 and June 28, covering the stretch of Manchester Avenue between Boyle and Vandeventer, which the Street Department will barricade. Spencer cited safety concerns stemming from chaotic street-takeover incidents in previous years and framed the measures as a way to allow residents and visitors to celebrate without risk of the disorder that has disrupted the area in recent Pride seasons.

Baltimore, MD

Education

Maryland's only all-boys public charter school is racing against the clock as Baltimore Collegiate School for Boys faces a June 30 charter expiration after the Baltimore City school board voted 6–4 in January not to renew it. District officials cited declining student achievement, underfunded special-education services, and financial instability, but school CEO Edwin Avent has filed an appeal and is seeking a court hearing scheduled for August 7. The school first opened in 2015 and reports that nearly 90% of its graduates earn high school diplomas, with half going on to two- or four-year colleges. The fight has galvanized families and community advocates who argue the closure disproportionately harms young Black male students who have few comparable options in the district. Meanwhile, in a separate but related development, Baltimore City Public Schools reached an $8.5 million settlement with charter operators over a yearslong funding dispute, a deal that could signal a new chapter in the contentious relationship between the district and its charter sector.

Read more: Baltimore Brew CBS Baltimore Baltimore Sun

Government & Policy

Baltimore's City Council is pursuing what would be the most sweeping overhaul of the city's charter since 1994, with a package of proposed amendments that would reshape how the city manages its budget, oversight, and infrastructure. A key provision championed by Councilwoman Odette Ramos would move the mayor's budget submission deadline from May 15 to April 15, giving the council 30 additional days to scrutinize departmental spending and hold agency hearings before the fiscal year begins. Separately, city leaders have proposed a charter amendment that would spin off Baltimore's water and wastewater operations into a standalone regional authority, separating them from the Department of Public Works — a structural change aimed at improving accountability and long-term infrastructure investment. All proposed amendments would be placed on the November 2026 ballot for voter ratification.
