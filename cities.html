Miami–Fort Lauderdale, FL

Crime & Justice

Fort Lauderdale police are investigating a fatal shooting that erupted before dawn on July 5 inside a parking garage at the Broward County Governmental Center, in the heart of downtown's entertainment district. The victim, 43-year-old James Cosey, was rushed to Broward Health Medical Center, where he was later pronounced dead. Officers recovered a firearm at the scene and detained a person of interest for questioning, though the case remains open as detectives work to establish what led to the confrontation. The shooting adds to a string of late-night violence along the Las Olas corridor, a stretch of bars and clubs that has drawn repeated police response over the past year, and has renewed calls from residents for stepped-up safety measures in the district. Investigators are still asking anyone with information to come forward.

Read more: WPLG Local 10 NBC 6 South Florida

Economics & Business

Miami's business climate is drawing fresh attention this year as companies weigh the impact of Florida's elimination of the state sales tax on commercial rent, a change enacted through House Bill 7031 that is now reshaping site-selection decisions across the city. Firms signing new Miami office leases in 2026 see the savings flow directly to their operating line, reinforcing a broader pattern of companies expanding in the city rather than relocating elsewhere. The momentum builds on events like eMerge Americas 2026, the tech and innovation conference held at the Miami Beach Convention Center in April that drew thousands of entrepreneurs, investors and corporate leaders eyeing the region's growth in finance, climate technology and health care. City and county economic development officials say the combination of low taxes, global connections and an expanding talent pool is positioning Miami-Dade as one of the country's most fully recovered post-pandemic economies.

Atlanta, GA

Government & Politics

Atlanta's City Council has filled the vacant District 2 seat left open when Amir Farokhi stepped down to become head of school at the Galloway School, appointing disability advocate Carden Wyckoff to serve out the remainder of the term. Wyckoff, who chaired the city's Accessibility and Inclusion Task Force and sits on the MARTA Riders Advisory Committee, was recommended for the post by Farokhi himself and took her oath of office in a ceremony overseen by a municipal court judge. She inherits Farokhi's seats on the Transportation and Zoning committees and the Committee on Council, giving her an immediate hand in decisions affecting Midtown and surrounding neighborhoods. Notably, her arrival coincided with a council-backed moratorium on new event and vending permits tied to the FIFA activation zone around Mercedes-Benz Stadium, a restriction now in effect through July 19 as the city manages crowd and logistics pressure from World Cup-related programming. Wyckoff is not seeking a full term in November's municipal elections, meaning District 2 will see another transition later this year.

Crime & Justice

A routine traffic stop south of Atlanta turned deadly on June 29 when a driver pulled over on I-85 near mile marker 34 in Coweta County produced a hidden firearm during a struggle with deputies and opened fire. According to CBS Atlanta's account of the shooting, deputies had removed the man from his vehicle and were attempting to detain him, including using a Taser, when he broke free, climbed back into the driver's seat, and fired multiple rounds at officers before being fatally shot in the return exchange. No deputies were injured. The Georgia Bureau of Investigation has taken over the case, as is standard for officer-involved shootings in the state, and Fox 5 Atlanta reported the GBI is reviewing dashcam and body-camera footage to determine whether the deputies' use of force was justified. The man's identity has not yet been released pending notification of family.

Washington, DC

Government & Development

The DC Office of Planning released an 89-page draft master plan for the 180-acre RFK campus, laying out a vision for the site anchored by the Washington Commanders' new $3.7 billion stadium. The framework divides the property into six districts and proposes 5,500 to 6,500 new housing units, at least 30% of them affordable, alongside up to 8 million square feet of mixed-use development, a new SportsPlex recreation complex, and expanded parkland along the Anacostia River. Some neighbors have pushed back, with community members telling city officials the plan reflects "nothing that we've asked for" on issues like traffic and density. The public comment period runs 45 days, closing August 14, giving residents a final window to shape the redevelopment before it moves toward construction.

Read more: Mayor Bowser's office WTOP WJLA

Government & Public Safety

Mayor Muriel Bowser's temporary emergency curfew orders became permanent this month after the Youth Curfew Amendment Act of 2026 took effect on July 2, formally authorizing the mayor or police chief to designate Extended Youth Curfew Zones across the District. Under the law, anyone under 18 gathering in a group of nine or more within a designated zone must disperse unless engaged in an exempted activity. The measure grew out of a series of emergency orders imposed around Navy Yard, the Southwest Waterfront, U Street, and Banneker Park after large teen gatherings and violent incidents, including a fatal shooting of a 15-year-old girl in a Southeast apartment in June. City leaders frame the permanent authority as a tool to curb youth violence, while civil liberties advocates continue to question how broadly the zones will be applied.

Houston, TX

Government & Politics

Houston's new fiscal year kicked off July 1 under Mayor John Whitmire's FY2027 budget, which the mayor's office says avoids a property tax increase and steers clear of one-time transfers or deferred infrastructure obligations to close the gap. The plan follows a rockier path a year earlier, when the $7.5 billion FY2026 budget passed amid protests at City Hall and pointed warnings from City Controller Chris Hollins about long-term financial sustainability. Residents are already starting to feel the new fiscal year's changes: a newly authorized fee begins appearing on water bills in August, part of the administration's push to fund core services like public safety, parks, and the municipal courts without raising the tax rate. The budget rollout comes as the city juggles record short-term costs tied to hosting the FIFA World Cup alongside longer-term fiscal discipline promises.

Read more: City of Houston: FY2027 Budget ABC13: FY2026 budget protests

Economics & Business

With the FIFA World Cup bringing an estimated 500,000 visitors to Houston's fan fest over its 39-day run, the city has rushed to finish infrastructure meant to outlast the tournament. Downtown Houston is midway through a major cooling retrofit on Main Street designed to cut sidewalk temperatures by as much as 10 degrees, a response to the brutal Gulf Coast summer heat that organizers worried would deter foot traffic during the matches. Alongside the cooling project, officials rolled out "Via Fútbol," a new wayfinding and navigation system built to help both World Cup visitors and everyday commuters move through downtown more easily. Houston hosted seven matches total at NRG Stadium, including two knockout-round games, with the city's final match — Canada vs. Morocco — played on July 4; city planners are framing the cooling and navigation upgrades as permanent civic investments rather than one-time tournament spending.

Dallas, TX

Government & Politics

Dallas city leaders took a major step this summer toward moving on from I.M. Pei's aging City Hall, voting in mid-June to reject repair plans for the 1970s-era building and instead direct City Manager Kimberly Bizor Tolbert to explore relocation. The vote caps months of debate over whether to patch up the deteriorating municipal building or start over at a new site, and Tolbert now has up to $3 million to conduct due diligence on prospective locations, including negotiating pre-development agreements. She's expected to bring a shortlist of options back to the council by the end of August, when members will vote on next steps. The decision, detailed by CBS Texas, sets up what could become one of the largest municipal real estate moves in the city's recent history, with ripple effects for downtown redevelopment and the fate of the landmark Pei-designed tower.

Read more: WFAA: Council votes no on repairs CBS Texas: City Hall relocation vote

Economics & Business

In a bid to lure Wall Street to North Texas, the Dallas City Council voted unanimously in late June to approve an $18.5 million tax incentive package aimed at bringing Morgan Stanley to the Uptown area. The investment bank is weighing Dallas as the site for a new large-scale U.S. operations hub that could bring more than $780 million in capital investment and create up to 4,800 high-paying jobs. If finalized, the project would rank among the city's most significant corporate relocations in years, adding to a string of financial-sector expansions in North Texas and reinforcing Uptown's growth as an office and residential hub. City officials are framing the incentive as a down payment on future tax revenue and job growth, though the deal still hinges on Morgan Stanley's final site selection.

Chicago, IL

Government & Public Safety

Chicago Police Superintendent Larry Snelling announced his retirement effective July 15, capping a tenure marked by a 32% drop in murders and a 41% reduction in shootings — culminating in 2025's lowest homicide count in 60 years. Snelling, a son of the Englewood neighborhood who spent three decades with the department, leaves amid reported tensions with City Hall that sources say made him determined to exit before the upcoming mayoral election. Mayor Brandon Johnson named police veteran Fred Waller as acting superintendent while a permanent replacement search gets underway, a process further complicated by the looming electoral landscape that could soon change who controls the selection. The transition comes at a delicate moment: even as the city celebrates Snelling's crime-reduction legacy, mid-year homicide data through June shows a roughly 6% uptick compared to the same period in 2025.

Los Angeles, CA

Crime & Justice

Federal authorities in Los Angeles unsealed a sweeping 65-count gang RICO indictment in what prosecutors are calling "Operation Broken Blade" — the first human trafficking gang RICO case ever brought in the Central District of California. Twenty-five individuals have been charged, with at least 51 victims identified across adult and child sex trafficking networks. Among those charged is Mukeshkumar Rambhai Ahir, the on-site manager of the Stadium Inn & Spas motel, who allegedly deposited more than $64,000 in proceeds he knew derived from a street gang's trafficking operation. Federal agents conducted large-scale arrest operations across the Los Angeles area to execute the charges, signaling a major escalation in the federal crackdown on organized trafficking rings in Southern California.

Read more: CBS Los Angeles MyNewsLA Crime

Government & Economics

Mayor Karen Bass proposed a $14.9 billion city budget for fiscal year 2026–27 that avoids layoffs, maintains current firefighter staffing, and funds 510 new LAPD hires — a marked departure from the city's recent fiscal struggles. The budget benefits from rising business, sales, and property tax revenues, and directs significant resources toward homelessness services, housing production, and infrastructure repair. The proposal comes as LA continues its uneven recovery from the January 2025 Palisades and Eaton wildfires: a June 2026 economic update from the county found that while debris clearance has progressed, fewer than a thousand structures are actually under construction on the more than 16,000 lots destroyed, with many survivors still displaced and awaiting insurance settlements. The budget must be approved by the City Council ahead of the July 1 fiscal deadline.

Government & Politics

Mayor Zohran Mamdani's progressive coalition scored a sweeping victory in the June 23 congressional primaries, with all three Mamdani-backed candidates winning their races — including two who defeated Democratic incumbents. Community organizer Darializa Avila Chevalier ousted five-term Rep. The results signal a significant leftward shift in New York's congressional delegation and a consolidation of Mamdani's influence over city and national politics just months into his mayoral tenure.
