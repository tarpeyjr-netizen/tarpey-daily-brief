Microsoft Copilot

This Week in Microsoft Copilot

If you use Windows, Word, or Outlook, you've probably bumped into Copilot — Microsoft's AI assistant that's been popping up in basically every corner of its software. This week it had one of those "spring cleaning meets big renovation" moments, with Microsoft admitting some things haven't worked while announcing an ambitious plan for what comes next.

The headline news: Microsoft is planning to merge every version of Copilot — the one in Windows, the one in Office apps, the consumer app, the works — into a single unified app by August, alongside a new paid tier of "AutoPilot" agents. These are always-on AI helpers that work in the background without you asking, quietly checking your email and calendar and taking action on their own, sometimes making 10 to 20 behind-the-scenes AI calls just to handle one task. Think of it less like a chatbot you talk to and more like a assistant who shows up to work while you're asleep and leaves a note about what got done. At the same time, Microsoft is cutting the fat: Copilot Podcasts and Copilot Labs are both getting shut down after failing to catch on. That honesty matters, because it came alongside a pretty striking admission — fewer than 4.5% of Microsoft's 450 million business customers actually pay for Copilot, and even among those who do, only 20 to 30% use it weekly. On the smaller-but-useful side, Copilot now adds watermarks to AI-generated videos and audio so people can tell what's real, and long-running agent tasks now show live progress icons right in the Windows taskbar, so you're not left wondering if the AI is still working or just stuck.

The big picture here is Microsoft betting big on autonomous agents while quietly acknowledging that most people still aren't sold on paying for AI features yet. The taskbar progress indicators and watermarks are small trust-building moves — they're Microsoft's way of making an increasingly autonomous assistant feel less like a black box. Whether the August merger actually gets more people to stick around, or just consolidates confusion into one app instead of five, is the thing to watch next.

🔍 Tool Spotlight

MolmoMotion (Ai2)

MolmoMotion is a new open-source AI model from the Allen Institute for AI (Ai2) and the University of Washington that does something wonderfully specific: you show it a few seconds of video plus a plain-English instruction, and it predicts exactly where objects will move next in 3D space. Think of it like a physics-savvy fortune teller for pixels — tell it "the cup will slide left" and it plots out the actual trajectory, point by point, in real-world coordinates. Built on Ai2's Molmo 2 vision-language model, it comes in two flavors: one that predicts motion step-by-step like writing out coordinates, and one that handles uncertainty better by blending possible futures together.

Why it matters: this is exactly the kind of skill robots need to grab objects reliably, and it already boosted robot manipulation success rates by over 20 percentage points in testing, plus made AI-generated video look more physically believable. Researchers, robotics teams, and video-generation startups are the obvious audience, but the surprising part is that Ai2 released the whole thing openly — model weights, training code, and training data — at a time when most labs keep their best tricks locked up.

ChatGPT

This Week in ChatGPT

ChatGPT is OpenAI's flagship AI assistant — the one that kicked off the modern AI boom back in late 2022 and now has hundreds of millions of users worldwide. This week was a big one, with a new model dropping, memory getting smarter, and a long-awaited coding tool landing for everyone.

The headline story is GPT-5.6, which OpenAI quietly launched on June 26 in a restricted preview. Meanwhile, Codex Remote went generally available on all plans, meaning you can now kick off coding tasks from your phone and have ChatGPT work on your actual Mac or Windows machine in the background, checking in with you when it needs approval.

Rounding things out, OpenAI rolled out a much-improved dictation model with better accuracy across languages and accents — particularly strong gains for Japanese, Korean, Vietnamese, and multilingual speakers who mix languages mid-sentence. And GPT-4.5 officially retired this week, with conversations seamlessly continuing on GPT-5.5. With GPT-5.6 on the horizon and free users finally getting memory, the platform is pushing hard to make the free tier feel genuinely powerful, not just a teaser for paid plans.

The shift from Llama to Muse Spark is one of the more significant strategic pivots in AI this year.
