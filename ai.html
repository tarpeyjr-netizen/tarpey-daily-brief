Microsoft Copilot

This Week in Microsoft Copilot

Microsoft Copilot is the AI assistant baked into Word, Excel, Outlook, Teams, and just about everything else in the Microsoft 365 universe — it's basically like having a very fast, very tireless intern sitting inside every app you already use. This week was a busy one, bouncing between a dramatic outage and a string of genuinely exciting new features.

The week kicked off with a hiccup: a misconfigured authentication update triggered a widespread Copilot outage that briefly knocked out AI features across the entire Microsoft 365 suite. Word, Excel, Outlook, Teams — all went dark for Copilot users until Microsoft patched the culprit microservice and restored service. It's the kind of thing that makes you realize how much people have come to depend on these tools. On the brighter side, the June feature rollout came loaded with upgrades. Copilot Notebooks can now pull in Teams meeting transcripts, notes, and shared content as knowledge sources — so if you missed a meeting, you can literally ask Copilot to catch you up using the actual recording. Outlook got a notable quality-of-life boost too: you can now drop full email threads directly into your chat prompt, letting Copilot reason over an entire conversation in one shot. And the underlying models got an upgrade, with GPT-5.5 Instant and Thinking now available inside the platform for more complex, multi-step tasks.

The direction here is clear: Microsoft is turning Copilot from a nice add-on into something closer to a second brain that lives inside your workflow. The meeting integration alone could change how teams communicate — why take detailed notes when your AI can reconstruct the whole conversation on demand?

Artlist

This Week in Artlist

Artlist started as a music licensing site for video creators — the kind of place you'd go to find a royalty-free song for your YouTube video without worrying about copyright strikes. But in 2026, it's transformed into something much more ambitious: a full AI video production platform that wants to be the one-stop shop for creators who make professional content.

This week, Artlist made a big splash in Hollywood. The company hosted what they're calling "Hollywood Week," where they live-demoed their AI Studio platform to actual filmmakers, studios, Google, and NVIDIA, plus a room full of Emmy-winning directors. The centerpiece was the announcement of "Terrarium," an AI-hybrid feature film being produced using Artlist's own tools — a bold move that signals they're serious about competing at the highest levels of filmmaking, not just helping YouTubers find background music. The timing came right on the heels of a major June 10th feature update, which revealed that Artlist Studio now integrates Sora, Veo 3, Flux, and ElevenLabs all under one roof. They also introduced proprietary models including Artist Original 1.0 for cinema-grade image generation and Hailuo 2.3 for advanced video creation — giving creators access to a toolkit that would have cost tens of thousands of dollars in studio software just a few years ago.

Artlist reaching $300M ARR on the back of 600% new-user growth in early 2026 tells you something important: there's a massive appetite for AI-powered creative tools that don't require a film school degree to operate. The Hollywood bet is a smart signal — if Artlist can credibly claim its tools helped produce a real feature film, every indie creator in the world will want a piece of that.

SAP Joule

This Week in SAP Joule

SAP Joule is the AI assistant built into SAP's massive suite of business software — think of it as a smart co-worker embedded right inside the tools companies use to run HR, finance, procurement, and everything else at enterprise scale. And this week, Joule is having something of a quiet victory lap following a blockbuster Sapphire 2026 conference, with several previously announced features actually landing in customers' hands.

The biggest practical news is that a fresh Joule Capabilities document hit SAP's help portal on June 10, detailing what's available right now across the product suite. On the feature side, SAP's Q1 2026 release brought some genuinely useful improvements: Joule's startup times are faster, and there's now a cross-thread search so you can dig back through your entire conversation history across all sessions — no more opening thread after thread trying to find that thing you asked about two weeks ago. Google Drive integration is also live, meaning Joule can now ground its answers in documents parked in Google Workspace alongside SAP's own knowledge. That's a big deal for teams who live in both worlds. Rounding it out, Joule now handles up to 8,000 documents per pipeline for organizations that want to feed it a huge internal knowledge base — policies, procedures, templates, the whole library.

Stepping back, the bigger picture here is that SAP is turning Joule from a single AI chatbot into an entire ecosystem. At Sapphire, CEO Christian Klein unveiled over 50 Joule Assistants spanning HR, payroll, recruiting, procurement, and more — essentially a specialized mini-Joule for every business function. General availability for many of those assistants is rolling out this month. For enterprise software, this is a genuinely ambitious transformation, and the June releases suggest SAP is making good on those promises.

If you're in sales or customer service and haven't looked at Agentforce lately, this release is a good reason to revisit it.

🔍 Tool Spotlight

Google Gemma 4

If you've ever wanted to run a powerful AI model on your own computer — or inside your company's private servers — rather than sending everything to a cloud, Google Gemma 4 is worth knowing about. Released in late May and refined for production use by early June 2026, Gemma 4 is Google's latest open-weight model family, meaning the actual model files are free to download and use for research or business. What makes Gemma 4 special is that it's genuinely multimodal: it can handle text, images, and audio all in one model, rather than needing separate tools for each. The largest version packs a 256,000-token context window — that's roughly 200,000 words of text it can "see" at once, which is extraordinary for a self-hosted model.

The family comes in five sizes, from the tiny 2-billion-parameter version that could run on a modern phone all the way up to a 31-billion-parameter version for serious compute environments. It supports over 140 languages and ships under an Apache 2.0 license, which means companies can actually build commercial products on top of it without licensing headaches. For developers, researchers, or privacy-conscious organizations that can't send sensitive data to OpenAI or Anthropic, Gemma 4 is arguably the most capable open option on the market right now. The surprise here is just how good a "free" model has gotten.

ChatGPT

This Week in ChatGPT

It's been a quietly significant week for ChatGPT — not a flashy model launch, but the kind of steady improvements that make a tool genuinely more useful to live with. The headline is memory: OpenAI overhauled how ChatGPT remembers things about you, doubling its capacity and making updates automatic. Think of it like upgrading from a sticky note on your fridge to an assistant who actually pays attention. The new system also gives you a reviewable summary page, so you can see exactly what ChatGPT thinks it knows about you. Plus and Pro users in the US are getting it first.

The other big quality-of-life addition is email. If you've connected Gmail or Outlook, you can now ask ChatGPT to write and send a message without ever leaving the chat — no copy-pasting, no switching tabs. It's a small thing that removes a surprising amount of friction. ChatGPT also got interactive charts, a table of contents for long documents, and a proper full-screen writing mode, nudging it closer to a real productivity app rather than just a chat window.

On the model side, OpenAI simplified the picker into four tiers — Instant, Medium, High, and Pro-only — which is genuinely less confusing than the alphabet soup of model names it replaced. And if you're using GPT-4.5 anywhere, mark your calendar: it's being retired on June 27th. Not dramatic, just housekeeping — but worth knowing before something breaks.

Mistral AI

This Week in Mistral AI

Mistral had a big week — arguably one of the most action-packed stretches the French startup has had since it burst onto the scene. The splashiest move was renaming its AI assistant from "Le Chat" to "Vibe" and turning it into something far more ambitious. Vibe now has two modes: Work Mode, designed for long, multi-step tasks that take time to think through, and Code Mode, which can write code, open pull requests, and work across your entire project through a new VS Code extension. In one move, Mistral is going after ChatGPT and GitHub Copilot simultaneously.

Underneath Vibe sits a new model: Mistral Medium 3.5. It's multimodal — meaning it understands both images and text — and it's built for "agentic" tasks, where the AI doesn't just answer a question but takes a series of actions to complete a goal. There's even a dial for how much reasoning effort it applies, which is a clever way to balance speed against depth depending on what you're asking it to do.

Then there's Voxtral TTS, a new voice model that can clone any voice from just a short sample, speak multiple languages, and stream audio in real time. The zero-shot cloning part — meaning it needs no training examples, just a clip — is genuinely impressive, and a little eerie. Finally, Mistral partnered with Emmi AI to bring physics simulation into its enterprise platform, letting engineers in aerospace, manufacturing, and energy run AI-powered digital twins instead of expensive real-world tests. For a company that started as a research lab, Mistral is moving very fast into the real world.
