SAP Joule

This Week in SAP Joule

If you've never heard of SAP Joule, that's fine — you're not supposed to have. It's the AI assistant baked into SAP, the software giant that runs the back-office plumbing (expenses, HR, supply chains, finance) for a huge chunk of the world's biggest companies. Joule's whole job is to sit inside that unglamorous machinery and let people talk to it in plain English instead of clicking through twelve menus.

This week Joule quietly took on a pile of very human chores. It can now handle business travel bookings inside Concur Travel, meaning someone can just type "book me a flight to Chicago Tuesday and a hotel near downtown" and Joule handles the search, comparison, and checkout. Right alongside that, it picked up full conversational control of expense reports too — creating, editing, submitting, and even fixing a receipt that doesn't match the amount on the card statement, which is the kind of fiddly task nobody enjoys. On the more technical side, developers got a version of Joule that lives inside SAP's Kyma cloud dashboard, able to pull pod logs, inspect clusters, and explain what's going wrong without someone digging through documentation. SAP also cleaned up the interface itself, tidying the response buttons and letting the "what's new" panel show a full history of updates instead of just the latest one, plus added access controls so companies can lock down exactly which documents Joule is allowed to read from.

None of this is flashy, and that's sort of the point. Joule isn't trying to write poetry or generate images — it's trying to become the assistant that quietly does the paperwork nobody wants to do, at the exact companies where millions of office workers spend their day. That's a much bigger bet on how AI actually changes daily work than a flashy chatbot demo.

Einstein AI

This Week in Einstein AI (Salesforce)

Einstein is Salesforce's AI brain, built into the world's most widely used customer-relationship software. It started years ago as a feature that predicted things like which deals might close. Lately it's evolved into Agentforce, a set of AI agents that don't just predict or chat — they actually go do the work themselves inside a company's sales, service, and shopping systems.

The big news this week is that Agentforce Commerce moved from "coming soon" to fully available, and it ships with three distinct agents: one that helps shoppers find and buy things, one that helps business buyers place orders, and one that helps merchants manage their store. What makes this a real step up rather than just another chatbot is that these agents can act, not just answer. A shopper agent can check whether an item is actually in stock, confirm it'll arrive before a shipping cutoff, and complete the purchase, all in one conversational flow, instead of just suggesting the shopper go look for themselves. Salesforce is also opening the door for these agents to show up natively inside ChatGPT and Google's Gemini apps, meaning the agent could eventually meet you where you already are instead of requiring you to visit a Salesforce-powered site. With over 12,000 customers already live on the platform, this isn't a pilot program anymore.

The throughline for Einstein this year is the shift from "AI that tells you something" to "AI that finishes the task for you." For regular people, that increasingly means the retailer's chat window you're typing into might not just answer your question about a return policy — it might actually process the return while you're still typing.

🔍 Tool Spotlight

Cursor

Cursor is a code editor built around AI, essentially a supercharged version of the familiar VS Code that programmers have used for years, except now you can describe what you want in plain English and an AI agent will actually write, edit, and run the code across your project's real files. It's become one of the most popular tools among professional developers precisely because it treats AI not as an autocomplete gimmick but as a coworker that can take on entire chunks of a task.

This week's version, 3.11, released July 10, adds something called side chats: little parallel conversations you can spin off from your main AI session using a quick "/side" command, without derailing whatever the main agent is busy building. You might ask a side chat to research an alternative approach or sanity-check a decision, then pull that answer back into the main thread later, or just leave it running like a separate open tab. The update also makes it easier to search back through old agent conversations and simplifies picking which project or repository you're working in.

What's genuinely interesting here is what it says about where AI coding tools are headed: instead of one AI doing one thing at a time, Cursor is now managing multiple simultaneous AI conversations the way a person juggles browser tabs. It's a small feature, but it hints at coding agents evolving from single-threaded assistants into something closer to a small team you're directing at once. Anyone who writes code for a living, from students to professional engineers, is a plausible user here.

ChatGPT

This Week in ChatGPT

ChatGPT is the chatbot that basically kicked off this whole AI moment, and OpenAI just had one of its biggest weeks in a while. If you've mostly used it to answer questions or write essays, that's about to feel like the tip of the iceberg, because the company just pushed it hard toward actually doing things for you instead of just talking about them.

The headline move is something called ChatGPT Work, which launched on July 9th. Instead of chatting back and forth until you've pieced together an answer yourself, you give it a goal, it makes a step-by-step plan you get to approve first, and then it goes off and builds the actual finished thing: a spreadsheet, a slide deck, a document, even a working web app. Think of it like handing a task to a capable intern who shows you their plan before starting, then comes back with the finished folder instead of a pile of notes. It runs on the new GPT-5.6 model family (three flavors this time, nicknamed Sol, Terra, and Luna, priced differently depending on how much power you need) and has OpenAI's coding tool Codex baked in. Right alongside that, ChatGPT's voice mode got a real upgrade called GPT-Live, which can listen and talk at the same time instead of waiting politely for its turn, so conversations feel less like a walkie-talkie and more like actually talking to someone. OpenAI also merged its separate Codex app into the main ChatGPT desktop app and started winding down its Atlas browser, tidying up what had become a slightly cluttered family of products.

Put together, this week is OpenAI betting that people don't just want smarter answers, they want finished work. If that sticks, "asking ChatGPT for help" starts to mean less typing and more approving plans while it does the typing for you.

Mistral AI

This Week in Mistral AI

Mistral AI is the French challenger in the AI race, known for building strong, often open-source models while everyone else keeps their code locked up. This week showed off just how many directions the company is pulling in at once, from math-proving software to actual robots.

The most eye-catching release was Leanstral 1.5, a model built specifically to write formal mathematical proofs, the kind of rigorous, no-room-for-error logic used in advanced math and safety-critical software. It solved 587 out of 672 problems from a notoriously hard math benchmark and is free to use, which is a big deal for researchers who'd otherwise pay serious money for that kind of specialized reasoning power. Mistral also shipped Robostral Navigate, a model that helps robots find their way around real-world spaces using nothing but a regular camera and plain-language instructions, no special sensors required, and it's designed to work on basically any robot. On the document side, a new version of Mistral's OCR tool (the software that turns scanned pages into usable text) got smarter about reading document structure, not just words. And behind the scenes, CEO Arthur Mensch confirmed a new open-weight model is coming this summer, described as "fat but sparse," meaning it's big in total size but efficient in how much of itself it actually uses per task, with early access opening this month.

The throughline here is Mistral spreading its bets: not just chasing a bigger chatbot, but building specialized tools for math, robotics, and documents while it works on a bigger model in the background. It's a reminder that the AI race isn't just about who has the flashiest assistant, it's also about who quietly wins the boring-but-critical use cases.

Claude Code

This Week in Claude Code

If you haven't met it yet, Claude Code is Anthropic's coding assistant that lives right in your terminal or editor, reading your files, writing code, and running commands like a very fast, very patient pair programmer. The other notable move is availability: Claude Code and Claude Cowork are now in public beta for Claude for Government Desktop, meaning FedRAMP High authorized agencies can use these same tools with tighter admin controls, audit logs, and spending limits. This Week in Claude Cowork

Anthropic even shared usage data showing the single biggest thing people use Cowork for — about a third of sessions — is exactly the boring-but-essential stuff: pulling scattered updates into one report, building onboarding checklists, and fixing up spreadsheets.

The takeaway is that Anthropic is betting Cowork becomes something you check in on throughout the day rather than something you sit in front of, more like a project manager than a program. Anthropic also extended its doubled usage limits through August 5 to cushion the rollout, so if you're on a paid plan, now's a good window to actually try it.

Microsoft Copilot

This Week in Microsoft Copilot

If you use Windows, Word, or Outlook, you've probably bumped into Copilot — Microsoft's AI assistant that's been popping up in basically every corner of its software. This week it had one of those "spring cleaning meets big renovation" moments, with Microsoft admitting some things haven't worked while announcing an ambitious plan for what comes next.

The headline news: Microsoft is planning to merge every version of Copilot — the one in Windows, the one in Office apps, the consumer app, the works — into a single unified app by August, alongside a new paid tier of "AutoPilot" agents. On the smaller-but-useful side, Copilot now adds watermarks to AI-generated videos and audio so people can tell what's real, and long-running agent tasks now show live progress icons right in the Windows taskbar, so you're not left wondering if the AI is still working or just stuck.

The big picture here is Microsoft betting big on autonomous agents while quietly acknowledging that most people still aren't sold on paying for AI features yet. The taskbar progress indicators and watermarks are small trust-building moves — they're Microsoft's way of making an increasingly autonomous assistant feel less like a black box. Whether the August merger actually gets more people to stick around, or just consolidates confusion into one app instead of five, is the thing to watch next.

🔍 Tool Spotlight

MolmoMotion (Ai2)

MolmoMotion is a new open-source AI model from the Allen Institute for AI (Ai2) and the University of Washington that does something wonderfully specific: you show it a few seconds of video plus a plain-English instruction, and it predicts exactly where objects will move next in 3D space. Think of it like a physics-savvy fortune teller for pixels — tell it "the cup will slide left" and it plots out the actual trajectory, point by point, in real-world coordinates. Built on Ai2's Molmo 2 vision-language model, it comes in two flavors: one that predicts motion step-by-step like writing out coordinates, and one that handles uncertainty better by blending possible futures together.

Why it matters: this is exactly the kind of skill robots need to grab objects reliably, and it already boosted robot manipulation success rates by over 20 percentage points in testing, plus made AI-generated video look more physically believable. Researchers, robotics teams, and video-generation startups are the obvious audience, but the surprising part is that Ai2 released the whole thing openly — model weights, training code, and training data — at a time when most labs keep their best tricks locked up.
