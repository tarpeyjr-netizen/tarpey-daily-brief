Anthropic Claude

This Week in Anthropic Claude

Claude is the AI chatbot and coding assistant made by Anthropic, and it just had one of its biggest weeks in a while. If you've used Claude for anything from homework help to writing code, the version running under the hood changed this week, and a couple of restrictions that had been in place got lifted too.

The headline news is Claude Sonnet 5, which is now the default model for anyone on Claude's free and paid plans. Think of Sonnet as the "everyday driver" model, the one most people actually talk to, and this new version is built to be far more independent. Anthropic says it can plan out multi-step tasks, use tools like a web browser or a computer terminal on its own, and even double-check its own work without being told to. That's a big deal because it means Claude can now handle longer, messier jobs, like researching a topic and building something with the results, in one continuous stretch instead of needing constant hand-holding. Right alongside that, Anthropic restored two other models, Claude Fable 5 and Mythos 5, after the U.S. government lifted export restrictions that had paused them a few weeks earlier; Anthropic also said it built a new safety filter that catches the exact issue that triggered the pause over 99% of the time now. And in a move that shows Claude isn't just for writing emails and code, Anthropic launched Claude Science, a version aimed at biology and chemistry research that can do things like predict protein structures and taps into more than 60 scientific databases.

On top of all that, Anthropic rolled out beefed-up admin tools for business customers, giving IT teams better visibility into who's using Claude, how much it costs, and tighter controls to avoid surprise bills. Put together, this week was Anthropic saying "Claude can do more, for more people, in more fields," from a smarter everyday assistant to a lab research partner. For regular users, the practical upshot is that the assistant you talk to every day just got noticeably more capable of running with a task and finishing it.

Perplexity AI

This Week in Perplexity AI

Perplexity started out as a search engine that answers your questions in plain sentences instead of just handing you ten blue links, but lately it's been turning into something closer to a full-blown digital assistant. This week's news makes that shift even clearer, with several updates aimed at making Perplexity work for you even when you're not actively typing into it.

The biggest idea is something called Personal Computer, an always-on AI that lives on a dedicated Mac mini and blends your local files and apps with Perplexity's cloud brain, acting almost like a digital assistant that never clocks out. It watches for triggers and quietly carries tasks forward around the clock, which is a pretty different pitch than "ask a question, get an answer." That fits with Perplexity's push to make Comet, its AI-powered web browser, available to entire companies now, complete with an assistant that can summarize pages, do research inline, and handle multi-step chores like booking flights or managing email on its own. Meanwhile, Perplexity's Deep Research feature, the mode built for digging deep into a topic and producing a thorough writeup, now runs on the more powerful Opus 4.6 model, which Anthropic makes, and that upgrade is rolling out from Max subscribers down to Pro users. Perplexity also announced a clever trick for its Personal Computer hardware: a system that automatically decides whether to run a task on your own device or send it to the cloud, keeping sensitive stuff like financial or health data local while shipping heavier computing jobs out to more powerful servers.

Zoom out and the pattern is clear: Perplexity wants to stop being just a smarter search bar and become the thing quietly working in the background of your digital life. Whether that lands well with users will depend on how comfortable people get with an AI that's always half-listening and acting on its own, but it's a notable bet on where assistants are headed next.

Microsoft Copilot

This Week in Microsoft Copilot

If you use Windows, Word, or Outlook, you've probably bumped into Copilot — Microsoft's AI assistant that's been popping up in basically every corner of its software. This week it had one of those "spring cleaning meets big renovation" moments, with Microsoft admitting some things haven't worked while announcing an ambitious plan for what comes next.

The headline news: Microsoft is planning to merge every version of Copilot — the one in Windows, the one in Office apps, the consumer app, the works — into a single unified app by August, alongside a new paid tier of "AutoPilot" agents. These are always-on AI helpers that work in the background without you asking, quietly checking your email and calendar and taking action on their own, sometimes making 10 to 20 behind-the-scenes AI calls just to handle one task. Think of it less like a chatbot you talk to and more like a assistant who shows up to work while you're asleep and leaves a note about what got done. At the same time, Microsoft is cutting the fat: Copilot Podcasts and Copilot Labs are both getting shut down after failing to catch on. That honesty matters, because it came alongside a pretty striking admission — fewer than 4.5% of Microsoft's 450 million business customers actually pay for Copilot, and even among those who do, only 20 to 30% use it weekly. On the smaller-but-useful side, Copilot now adds watermarks to AI-generated videos and audio so people can tell what's real, and long-running agent tasks now show live progress icons right in the Windows taskbar, so you're not left wondering if the AI is still working or just stuck.

The big picture here is Microsoft betting big on autonomous agents while quietly acknowledging that most people still aren't sold on paying for AI features yet. The taskbar progress indicators and watermarks are small trust-building moves — they're Microsoft's way of making an increasingly autonomous assistant feel less like a black box. Whether the August merger actually gets more people to stick around, or just consolidates confusion into one app instead of five, is the thing to watch next.

🔍 Tool Spotlight

MolmoMotion (Ai2)

MolmoMotion is a new open-source AI model from the Allen Institute for AI (Ai2) and the University of Washington that does something wonderfully specific: you show it a few seconds of video plus a plain-English instruction, and it predicts exactly where objects will move next in 3D space. Think of it like a physics-savvy fortune teller for pixels — tell it "the cup will slide left" and it plots out the actual trajectory, point by point, in real-world coordinates. Built on Ai2's Molmo 2 vision-language model, it comes in two flavors: one that predicts motion step-by-step like writing out coordinates, and one that handles uncertainty better by blending possible futures together.

Why it matters: this is exactly the kind of skill robots need to grab objects reliably, and it already boosted robot manipulation success rates by over 20 percentage points in testing, plus made AI-generated video look more physically believable. Researchers, robotics teams, and video-generation startups are the obvious audience, but the surprising part is that Ai2 released the whole thing openly — model weights, training code, and training data — at a time when most labs keep their best tricks locked up.

This Week in ChatGPT

ChatGPT is OpenAI's flagship AI assistant — the one that kicked off the modern AI boom back in late 2022 and now has hundreds of millions of users worldwide. This week was a big one, with a new model dropping, memory getting smarter, and a long-awaited coding tool landing for everyone.

The headline story is GPT-5.6, which OpenAI quietly launched on June 26 in a restricted preview. Here's the twist: access was initially limited to a group vetted and approved by the Trump administration, which raised plenty of eyebrows across the tech world. On the memory front, OpenAI rebuilt the whole system from scratch. The old approach basically hoarded every note about you indefinitely; the new one periodically re-synthesizes what it knows, so outdated information doesn't linger forever. Think of it like a friend who actually updates their mental notes about you rather than remembering forever that you "love sushi" from a conversation three years ago. Plus and Pro users also get double the memory capacity, and — for the first time — the full memory system is coming to free users too, made possible by a roughly 5x cut in compute costs. Meanwhile, Codex Remote went generally available on all plans, meaning you can now kick off coding tasks from your phone and have ChatGPT work on your actual Mac or Windows machine in the background, checking in with you when it needs approval.

Rounding things out, OpenAI rolled out a much-improved dictation model with better accuracy across languages and accents — particularly strong gains for Japanese, Korean, Vietnamese, and multilingual speakers who mix languages mid-sentence. And GPT-4.5 officially retired this week, with conversations seamlessly continuing on GPT-5.5. With GPT-5.6 on the horizon and free users finally getting memory, the platform is pushing hard to make the free tier feel genuinely powerful, not just a teaser for paid plans.

ElevenLabs

This Week in ElevenLabs

ElevenLabs is the company behind some of the most eerily realistic AI-generated voices on the internet — it's the tool podcasters, game developers, and app builders reach for when they want audio that doesn't sound like a robot from 2003. This week brought a mix of exciting new features and some housekeeping that developers will want to pay attention to.

The housekeeping: the older eleven_monolingual_v1, eleven_multilingual_v1, and scribe_v1 models are all being retired on July 9. If you're building anything that calls those endpoints, now is the time to migrate — you have about a week. On the business side, the government of Poland has taken a stake in ElevenLabs, which is an unusual move for a startup and signals just how seriously some governments are treating AI voice technology as strategic infrastructure.
