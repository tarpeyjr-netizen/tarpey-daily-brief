Microsoft Copilot

This Week in Microsoft Copilot

Microsoft Copilot is the AI assistant baked into the tools millions of people use every day — Word, Excel, Teams, Outlook — and it's been on a tear lately. This week, the biggest story is one that's been building all month: the official rollout of a fundamentally new way Copilot works.

The most significant shift is the arrival of Copilot Cowork, which reached general availability on June 16 and is already being used by more than half of the Fortune 500. Here's the key idea: regular Copilot Chat answers your questions, but Cowork actually does the work — it can handle long, multi-step tasks that jump between apps, and it keeps running in a secure cloud environment even after you close your laptop. Think of it less like a chatbot and more like an always-on assistant who never needs a coffee break. Alongside that, Microsoft quietly slipped in something eye-catching: Anthropic's Claude is now available as a model option inside Copilot Chat, meaning users can pick the AI brain they want for different jobs. And Microsoft added GPT 5.5 Instant to the mix as well, optimized for fast, everyday questions and image-based tasks.

There were also practical changes worth noting. Copilot features inside Word, Excel, PowerPoint, and OneNote are now locked to users with a paid Microsoft 365 Copilot license — which will matter to anyone on a team where not everyone has that plan. And in Teams, meeting organizers can now delete AI-generated recaps, summaries, and transcripts right from the Recap page, giving people more control over what gets stored. Between the Cowork launch, the new model options, and the licensing shift, this has genuinely been one of the bigger weeks in Copilot's history — and it's a sign of how quickly Microsoft is moving to make AI feel like a core part of office life rather than an optional add-on.

Artlist

This Week in Artlist

Artlist is a creative platform that started as a place to license music for videos — think YouTube creators, filmmakers, and marketers — but has spent the last couple of years reinventing itself as a full AI-powered video production suite. It's one of the more interesting transformations happening in the creative tools world right now.

The big news this week is a major workforce restructuring: Artlist announced it's cutting around 200 jobs, roughly 40% of its 500-person team, as it pushes hard into an AI-first operating model. CEO Ira Belsky was blunt about why — competitors are building the same kinds of products with 120 employees, and Artlist had five times that headcount. What makes this story unusual is that the layoffs aren't coming from a company in trouble. Artlist just crossed $300 million in annual recurring revenue with 50% year-over-year growth, so this is a bet on the future, not a rescue mission. The company is betting that an AI-native team structure can move faster and compete with startups that were built lean from day one.

On the product side, there's been a lot happening this year that provides context for that strategic pivot. Artlist Studio — their new layer-based video creation platform — lets you pick characters, backgrounds, and settings independently and swap them out without starting from scratch, which is a genuinely different approach from the "one prompt controls everything" style of most AI video tools. The platform now aggregates 38 generative video models in one place, from Veo 3.1 to Kling 3.0 to Sora 2. And in a move that signals just how seriously they're taking the AI content space, Artlist TV — a streaming channel made entirely of AI-generated shows — launched on June 1. The restructuring this week is painful for the people affected, but it tells you where the company thinks the creative industry is heading.

Mistral AI

This Week in Mistral AI

Mistral AI is the French startup that's become Europe's most credible answer to OpenAI and Anthropic. They're known for releasing powerful open-weight models — ones you can actually download and run yourself — alongside a growing suite of enterprise products. This week they had one of their busiest weeks in recent memory, dropping a major new product, expanding their flagship agent, and announcing some serious infrastructure moves.

The headline release is OCR 4, which shipped on June 23rd and is genuinely impressive. OCR stands for "Optical Character Recognition" — basically, it's software that reads text out of images and documents. But Mistral's version goes way beyond just reading words. OCR 4 understands the structure of a document: it knows the difference between a table and a paragraph, marks exactly where each chunk of text lives on the page, and even flags how confident it is about each extraction. It covers 170 languages, processes up to 2,000 pages per minute on a single GPU, and — crucially for regulated industries like banking, healthcare, and law — can run entirely inside your own servers, so sensitive documents never have to leave your building. That self-hosted angle is a big deal; lots of enterprises have been waiting for document AI that doesn't require them to upload their files to someone else's cloud.

Alongside OCR 4, Mistral had a packed AI Now Summit where they unveiled a partnership with Airbus that will weave Mistral's models into aircraft design and manufacturing, expanded their Vibe agent to handle everything from inbox triage to coding pull requests in one unified tool, and announced a new 10 MW data center in Les Ulis, France, opening in Q3. That last one matters more than it sounds — owning compute infrastructure means Mistral is less dependent on big US cloud providers, which is a strategic priority for a European company trying to compete at the frontier. All told, it was a week that showed Mistral is serious about growing up from "scrappy open-source favorite" to genuine enterprise AI platform.

For a curious teenager learning to code their first AI app, this is a genuinely big deal — the barrier just dropped significantly.
