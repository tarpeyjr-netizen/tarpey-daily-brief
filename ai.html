Claude Cowork

This Week in Claude Cowork

Claude Cowork is Anthropic's AI-powered workspace — think of it as having an extremely capable AI colleague living inside your computer, helping you research, write, analyze, and build things, all from a single app. This week was a busy one, with a handful of meaningful upgrades that make Cowork feel more like a real workplace tool than ever before.

The biggest quality-of-life addition is Effort Control, which lets you dial up or down how hard Claude works on any given task. Choose from Low, Medium, High, or Max — High is the default, which means most everyday requests are already handled thoughtfully, but you can push to Max when you need Claude to really dig in. On the connectivity front, Anthropic quietly expanded the list of apps Cowork can send work to, adding Adobe, Canva, Gamma, Lovable, Miro, Replit, Vercel, and Wix alongside the existing lineup. That means you can now take a brainstorming session in Cowork and push the results directly into a Canva design or a live Replit project without copy-pasting anything. For companies using Okta to manage employee access, there's also a new enterprise connector feature that lets IT admins provision Claude tools once, and every employee gets automatic access on first login — no more per-person setup tickets. The week wasn't entirely smooth though: on June 23rd Anthropic experienced an elevated error rate across Claude.ai, Cowork, and the API, though the incident was resolved the same day.

Put it all together and you can see the direction Cowork is heading — less "AI chatbot you open separately" and more "AI that's woven into wherever your work already lives." The connector expansion in particular signals that Anthropic wants Cowork to be the starting point of workflows, not just a standalone tool.

ElevenLabs

This Week in ElevenLabs

ElevenLabs is the company behind some of the most realistic AI-generated voices on the internet — the kind that can clone a voice from a short audio clip and speak in dozens of languages with natural-sounding emotion. This week they shipped a wave of updates that push the platform further into enterprise territory while also making individual developers' lives easier.

The headline technical release is Music v2, now available through the API. Where the original music generator worked a bit like a simple prompt box — you describe a vibe and it produces something — Music v2 introduces a more structured, chunk-based approach. You build a composition out of discrete segments, controlling structure, pacing, and arrangement much more precisely. It's the difference between asking someone to "write you a song" versus handing them sheet music and saying "build on this." On the speed side, ElevenLabs introduced Flash, its fastest-ever text-to-speech model, which can generate speech in just 75 milliseconds via the API. To put that in context, a normal human blink takes about 150ms — Flash is literally faster than a blink. That kind of latency opens the door for real-time voice applications that previously weren't possible. Meanwhile, companies with stricter data requirements got a meaningful gift: ElevenLabs now supports on-premise and on-device deployment, meaning your audio generation never has to leave your own servers. And pricing just got friendlier too, with cuts announced for both ElevenAPI and ElevenAgents for self-serve developers.

ElevenLabs also made global news this week when Poland announced an $11 million investment to build an AI tech hub in partnership with the company — a sign of how seriously governments are starting to treat voice AI as critical infrastructure. For regular users, the clearest takeaway from this week's updates is that ElevenLabs is moving fast on speed, control, and reach all at once.

Apple Foundation Models — Free Tier for Developers

If you've ever built an app and gulped at the API bill from OpenAI or Anthropic, Apple just gave a lot of small developers something to cheer about. At WWDC 2026, Apple announced that its Foundation Models — the AI brains that power on-device features across iPhone, iPad, and Mac — are now free to use via Private Cloud Compute for any developer whose app has fewer than two million first-time App Store downloads. In practice, that covers the vast majority of indie developers and small studios. Instead of paying per token to run AI features, those developers can now call Apple's models through a clean Swift API at no cost, keeping user data private in the process since computation happens on Apple's infrastructure rather than a third-party cloud. What makes this even more interesting is the added flexibility: the same API now lets developers call third-party models like Claude or Gemini through the exact same interface, so you can swap between Apple's model and an external one without rewriting your code. Apple also unveiled a new Dynamic Profiles system for building multi-agent workflows, and confirmed the Foundation Models framework will go open source later this summer. For a curious teenager learning to code their first AI app, this is a genuinely big deal — the barrier just dropped significantly.

Meta Llama

This Week in Meta Llama

Meta Llama is Meta's family of open-weight AI models — "open-weight" meaning Meta publishes the model files publicly so anyone can download them, run them on their own hardware, and modify them however they like. That's the big differentiator from ChatGPT or Gemini, where you can only access the model through an API and never touch the underlying weights. Llama has become the backbone of an enormous open-source AI ecosystem, powering everything from local chatbots to enterprise apps that need more privacy than a cloud service allows.

It was a quieter week for fresh Llama announcements, but the longer-term storyline is genuinely worth following. The current headline family — Llama 4 — launched earlier this year with two standout models. Llama 4 Scout packs 17 billion active parameters with a stunning 10-million-token context window (imagine feeding it a stack of novels and asking it to find contradictions), while Llama 4 Maverick runs the same active parameter count but routes through 128 different "expert" sub-networks — a mixture-of-experts architecture that scored above GPT-4o on several leaderboards at launch. Looking ahead, reports this week pointed to Meta actively developing its next models, codenamed "Avocado" (an LLM) and "Mango" (a multimedia generation model), both expected to ship later in 2026. There's also talk of a Llama 4.5 release as a stepping stone before the next major generation arrives.

The more fascinating undercurrent is a strategic tension Meta is navigating. Some reports suggest the company is weighing how much to stay purely open-source versus building more closed, proprietary products, as the compute costs of frontier AI keep climbing. For now, Llama remains open and beloved by developers worldwide — but the decisions Meta makes about Avocado and Mango over the next six months could reshape whether Llama stays the champion of open AI or starts feeling like a carefully managed product line with a marketing team attached.

For years, Microsoft's AI strategy basically meant "use OpenAI." That changed dramatically at Microsoft Build 2026 in early June, when the company unveiled seven brand-new AI models built entirely in-house under a family called MAI — short for Microsoft AI. The headline of the bunch is MAI-Thinking-1, a 35-billion-parameter reasoning model with a massive 256,000-token context window, which means it can hold an enormous amount of information in mind while working through a complex problem. Think of it like the difference between a student who can only keep one textbook open versus one who has an entire library memorized. Microsoft trained it from scratch on clean, commercially licensed data — no recycled outputs from other companies' models, which matters a lot for businesses worried about legal liability.

The full family covers a lot of ground: there's a fast coding model called MAI-Code-1-Flash, image generation models under MAI-Image-2.5, and voice and transcription models too. All of them are available through Azure AI Foundry, and they also landed on third-party platforms like Fireworks AI and OpenRouter, meaning developers don't need a full Microsoft relationship to start experimenting. The real story here isn't any single model — it's that Microsoft is explicitly building toward what they called "long-term self-sufficiency," a polite way of saying they no longer want to be entirely dependent on OpenAI. For enterprises already deep in the Microsoft ecosystem, having homegrown models optimized for Azure is a genuinely big deal.
