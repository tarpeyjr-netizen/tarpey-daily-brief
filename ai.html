Claude Code

This Week in Claude Code

If you haven't met it yet, Claude Code is Anthropic's coding assistant that lives right in your terminal or editor, reading your files, writing code, and running commands like a very fast, very patient pair programmer. This week was less about flashy new powers and more about Anthropic quietly oiling the machine so it breaks less and tells you more about what it's doing.

The biggest practical change is a new login-expiry warning: if you've got a long background session running and your login is about to time out, Claude Code now gives you a heads-up so you can re-authenticate before the session gets yanked out from under you. Alongside that, the footer now shows clearer status badges, including a little grey pause icon when you're in manual permission mode, so you always know whether the agent is waiting on you or working on its own. Anthropic also went after a pile of background-session bugs that had been annoying developers on macOS and Windows, things like sessions freezing when reopened, or going permanently unresponsive when the daemon's session token went stale. Those now recover automatically instead of forcing a restart. On top of that, streaming responses feel snappier, startup uses less memory, and there's a new setting for remote-controlling Claude Code from inside VS Code.

The other notable move is availability: Claude Code and Claude Cowork are now in public beta for Claude for Government Desktop, meaning FedRAMP High authorized agencies can use these same tools with tighter admin controls, audit logs, and spending limits. Put together, this was a "make it sturdier" week rather than a "make it smarter" week, which matters if you're the kind of person who's been burned by a background session vanishing mid-task.

Xai Grok

This Week in Xai Grok

Grok is Elon Musk's AI model from xAI, built into X (formerly Twitter) and available as its own app, and it's had a genuinely stacked week. Layer on top of that a newly generally-available Speech to Text API covering 25 languages, and xAI is clearly making a serious push into voice as a whole category, not just a Grok feature.

There was also a smaller but symbolic moment: Musk posted a cryptic two-word update suggesting Grok Imagine, the image and video generation tool, has reached a completion milestone, pushing Grok further into multimodal territory. Between a new flagship model, a voice platform launch, and multimodal progress, this was one of the busier weeks xAI has had in a while, and it signals the company is racing to compete on breadth as much as raw model quality.

Anthropic Claude Cowork

This Week in Claude Cowork

Claude Cowork is the part of Anthropic's Claude that acts less like a chatbot and more like a coworker you can hand actual office tasks to — things like building a report, reconciling a spreadsheet, or putting together a slide deck while you go do something else. This week it got a lot less tied to your laptop.

On July 7, Anthropic announced that Cowork is leaving the desktop and heading to the web, iPhone, and Android, starting with a beta for Max subscribers that will widen to more plans over the coming weeks. The big idea is that a Cowork session now lives in the cloud instead of on your machine, so you can kick off a task at your desk, close your laptop, and check its progress from your phone later — it just keeps working and pings you if it needs a decision only a human can make. Anthropic also merged Chat and Cowork into a single home screen, so there's one sidebar and one search bar instead of two separate tools competing for your attention. On top of that, Pro and Max users can now hand Cowork actual computer control, letting it click around, open files, and run dev tools on its own, and it picked up write access to Microsoft 365 so it can draft emails, manage your calendar, and drop files into OneDrive or SharePoint. Anthropic even shared usage data showing the single biggest thing people use Cowork for — about a third of sessions — is exactly the boring-but-essential stuff: pulling scattered updates into one report, building onboarding checklists, and fixing up spreadsheets.

The takeaway is that Anthropic is betting Cowork becomes something you check in on throughout the day rather than something you sit in front of, more like a project manager than a program. Anthropic also extended its doubled usage limits through August 5 to cushion the rollout, so if you're on a paid plan, now's a good window to actually try it.

This Week in Microsoft Copilot

If you use Windows, Word, or Outlook, you've probably bumped into Copilot — Microsoft's AI assistant that's been popping up in basically every corner of its software. This week it had one of those "spring cleaning meets big renovation" moments, with Microsoft admitting some things haven't worked while announcing an ambitious plan for what comes next.

The headline news: Microsoft is planning to merge every version of Copilot — the one in Windows, the one in Office apps, the consumer app, the works — into a single unified app by August, alongside a new paid tier of "AutoPilot" agents. The big picture here is Microsoft betting big on autonomous agents while quietly acknowledging that most people still aren't sold on paying for AI features yet. The taskbar progress indicators and watermarks are small trust-building moves — they're Microsoft's way of making an increasingly autonomous assistant feel less like a black box. 🔍 Tool Spotlight

MolmoMotion (Ai2)

MolmoMotion is a new open-source AI model from the Allen Institute for AI (Ai2) and the University of Washington that does something wonderfully specific: you show it a few seconds of video plus a plain-English instruction, and it predicts exactly where objects will move next in 3D space. Think of it like a physics-savvy fortune teller for pixels — tell it "the cup will slide left" and it plots out the actual trajectory, point by point, in real-world coordinates. Built on Ai2's Molmo 2 vision-language model, it comes in two flavors: one that predicts motion step-by-step like writing out coordinates, and one that handles uncertainty better by blending possible futures together.

Why it matters: this is exactly the kind of skill robots need to grab objects reliably, and it already boosted robot manipulation success rates by over 20 percentage points in testing, plus made AI-generated video look more physically believable. Researchers, robotics teams, and video-generation startups are the obvious audience, but the surprising part is that Ai2 released the whole thing openly — model weights, training code, and training data — at a time when most labs keep their best tricks locked up.
