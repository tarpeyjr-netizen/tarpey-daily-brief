Claude CoWork

This Week in Claude CoWork

Claude CoWork is Anthropic's desktop app that lets regular office workers hand off long, multi-step tasks to an AI — things like drafting reports, organizing files, or researching topics — without needing to know a single line of code. It runs on both Mac and Windows, and this week it's been unusually busy with news.

The biggest thing to land is a temporary usage boost that Anthropic quietly rolled out at the start of June and that runs through July 5: they've doubled CoWork's five-hour session limits at no extra charge for Pro, Max, and Team plan subscribers. That means you get roughly twice the runway on long autonomous tasks before hitting a cap. It's framed as a promotional window, but it's also a pretty transparent nudge for people to actually try the heavier workflows CoWork was designed for. Alongside that, Anthropic has been on a connector roll — they just released a bundle of 20-plus legal MCP connectors and 12 practice-area plugins, which essentially turns CoWork into a reasonably capable research assistant for lawyers. Contracts, case law lookups, document drafting — the legal vertical is getting real attention.

On the enterprise side, Anthropic launched a beta for centralized MCP connector access, starting with Okta integration. What that means in plain English: a company's IT admin can now set up a connector once, and every employee gets it automatically the first time they log in — no individual setup required. That's a meaningful quality-of-life improvement for large teams. There's also a mobile companion feature called Dispatch in beta, which lets Pro and Max users control long-running CoWork tasks from their phone. So if you've got CoWork grinding away on your laptop at home, you can check in and approve steps from wherever you are. Taken together, these updates suggest Anthropic is pushing CoWork hard into the enterprise mainstream.

ElevenLabs

This Week in ElevenLabs

ElevenLabs is the company behind some of the most eerily realistic AI-generated voices on the internet — it's the tool podcasters, game developers, and app builders reach for when they want audio that doesn't sound like a robot from 2003. This week brought a mix of exciting new features and some housekeeping that developers will want to pay attention to.

The headline creative release is Music v2, now available through the ElevenLabs API. The original music generation was prompt-based — you'd describe what you wanted and hope for the best. Music v2 takes a more structured approach, letting you build a composition from chunks, mixing AI-generated sections with audio reference clips you supply yourself. Think of it like having a rough cut of a song and asking the AI to compose around the parts you already like. That kind of fine-grained control over arrangement and pacing is a big step up. Meanwhile, their transcription engine got smarter too: the Scribe realtime WebSocket now accepts a "keyterms" parameter that nudges the model toward domain-specific vocabulary — useful if you're transcribing medical conversations or legal depositions where jargon matters — and a no_verbatim mode that strips out filler words and false starts for cleaner output. Their conversational AI agents also gained first-class tagging for organizing conversation history, and the turn model can now be set to v2 or v3 depending on how much latency you're willing to trade for quality.

The housekeeping: the older eleven_monolingual_v1, eleven_multilingual_v1, and scribe_v1 models are all being retired on July 9. If you're building anything that calls those endpoints, now is the time to migrate — you have about a week. On the business side, the government of Poland has taken a stake in ElevenLabs, which is an unusual move for a startup and signals just how seriously some governments are treating AI voice technology as strategic infrastructure.

Meta Llama

This Week in Meta Llama

Meta Llama was, for years, the world's most popular open-source AI model family — the go-to toolkit for developers who wanted to build smart apps without paying per API call. But 2026 has brought a dramatic plot twist: Meta has quietly retired the Llama brand in favor of something much more ambitious, and much less open.

The new player is Muse Spark, built by Meta's newly formed Superintelligence Labs and launched in April. Unlike Llama — which anyone could download and run on their own hardware — Muse Spark is proprietary, and Meta's messaging has been careful: they "hope" to open-source future versions, but there are no firm promises. What Muse Spark does offer is genuinely impressive: it's a natively multimodal reasoning model, meaning it can think across text, images, and video all at once. Its standout trick is Contemplating mode, which spins up multiple AI agents working in parallel on the same hard problem simultaneously — similar in spirit to Gemini's Deep Think, but executing it in a more distributed way. Benchmarks put it at 58% on Humanity's Last Exam, a notoriously brutal test designed to stump even frontier models. As of June 8, Muse Spark now powers Meta AI on the company's smart glasses, replacing Llama 4 and meaningfully narrowing the gap to competitors like Google and OpenAI in real-world, on-device performance.

The shift from Llama to Muse Spark is one of the more significant strategic pivots in AI this year. Meta spent years building developer goodwill through open-source releases, and now it's pulling back — at least for the cutting-edge stuff. For anyone who's been building with Llama, the ground is shifting. The open-source AI community is watching closely to see whether Meta's "hope to open-source" ever becomes a real commitment, or whether Muse Spark stays locked behind the meta.ai walls for good.
