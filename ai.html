ChatGPT

This Week in ChatGPT

ChatGPT is OpenAI's flagship conversational AI — the tool that most people think of when someone says "AI chatbot." It's used by hundreds of millions of people to write, think, code, and figure things out. This week, OpenAI went on a quiet but meaningful sprint of improvements, touching everything from how you pick a model to how you control your privacy.

The biggest change you'll notice is a cleaned-up model picker. Instead of a confusing dropdown of version numbers, ChatGPT now offers four tiers — Instant, Medium, High, and Pro — making it much easier to decide whether you want a fast answer or a deeply reasoned one. Think of it like choosing between a quick text reply and a long, thought-out email. That update landed June 10 and rolled out to Plus and Pro users on web and mobile. At the same time, the old GPT-5.2 models were officially retired as of June 12; if you had a conversation running on one of them, it quietly switched over to the equivalent GPT-5.5 version without breaking anything. Two more retirements are on the calendar: GPT-4.5 goes away June 27, and o3 follows on August 26.

Beyond model housekeeping, two features got meaningful upgrades. Scheduled Tasks — the ability to tell ChatGPT to do something at a specific time, like "check the weather every morning" — got a dedicated Scheduled page with better notifications and the ability to pause or edit tasks. And memory controls got more granular: you can now highlight text in your memory summary and delete it, or just flip the whole thing off. For anyone who's ever felt a little uneasy about what ChatGPT "remembers" about them, that's a genuinely useful addition. ChatGPT Business also quietly added Active Sessions, letting users see every device logged in and sign out of anything suspicious. Taken together, this week feels like OpenAI tidying up the house — making the product feel more polished and trustworthy as the GPT-5.5 era fully kicks in.

Mistral AI

This Week in Mistral AI

Mistral AI is a French startup that's become one of the most interesting AI companies in the world — not because it's the biggest, but because it keeps making powerful models and releasing them as open source, which means anyone can download and use them freely. This week, Mistral continued its ambitious transformation from "AI model company" into something much bigger.

The headline story is the continued rollout of Vibe, the newly rebranded version of Mistral's Le Chat app. Vibe isn't just a name change — it's a whole new concept. It's a single AI agent that can handle long-running work tasks (catching up on email, running deep research, drafting documents) and software development tasks (building features, fixing bugs, shipping pull requests) all in one place, with integrations for VS Code and the command line. It's Mistral's answer to the question: "What if your AI assistant could actually do things, not just talk about them?" Alongside Vibe, Mistral released Mistral Medium 3.5, a 128-billion parameter model that blends instruction-following, reasoning, and coding into one — and released it under a modified MIT license, meaning developers can build on top of it with relatively few restrictions. That's a big deal in a world where many powerful models are locked behind APIs.

Meanwhile, Mistral is also thinking big about infrastructure and money. The company is reportedly in early talks to raise around €3 billion, which would push its valuation to roughly €20 billion — nearly double what it was valued at less than a year ago. And to back up that ambition with real hardware, Mistral announced a new 10-megawatt inference data center near Paris, set to open in Q3 2026. The company is also going after industrial customers — Airbus, BMW, and chip-maker ASML are already signed on to use Mistral's new engineering AI stack, which gained physics simulation capabilities through the acquisition of a startup called Emmi. It's a fascinating week that shows Mistral is serious about being a full-stack AI company, not just a model shop.

Anthropic Claude Code

This Week in Anthropic Claude Code

Claude Code is Anthropic's command-line tool that lets developers hand off coding tasks directly to Claude — think of it as a tireless coding partner you chat with in your terminal. This week was anything but quiet, starting with the bombshell that Anthropic's newest model, Claude Fable 5 (also called Mythos-class), was released on June 9th and then pulled offline within three days. The culprit? A U.S. government export directive that forced the model back off the shelf just as developers were starting to get their hands on it. The model had been billed as Anthropic's most capable widely-released model yet, purpose-built for long, complex, autonomous tasks — and it launched simultaneously across the Claude API, AWS, Microsoft Foundry, and more. The abrupt suspension left many in the developer community scratching their heads and watching closely for its return.

While Fable 5's saga played out, the Claude Code team kept shipping practical improvements at a steady clip. Auto mode — which lets Claude pick the best model automatically, handling permission checks quietly in the background — arrived on Bedrock, Vertex, and Foundry for Opus 4.7 and 4.8. That's a big deal for enterprise teams running Claude on those platforms, since it removes the friction of manually choosing model configurations. On the safety side, Claude Code now stops to ask before writing to files that could run code behind the scenes, like shell startup files (.zshenv, .bash_login) and build configs (.npmrc, .bazelrc). It's a small change but a meaningful one — a bit like your assistant double-checking before touching the engine under the hood.

The reliability fixes rounded out a strong week. Mid-stream connection drops, scrolling and focus bugs, remote session task states, and background session reliability all got patches. And for teams running Claude inside their own infrastructure, Managed Agents can now operate in a sandboxed environment and connect to private MCP servers — meaning enterprises can keep their sensitive data and tools behind their own walls while still getting the full power of Claude's agentic capabilities. It all adds up to a week that showed the Claude Code team is pushing on every front simultaneously, from frontier models to the little bugs that quietly drive developers crazy.

Google Gemma 4

If you've ever wanted to run a powerful AI model on your own computer — or inside your company's private servers — rather than sending everything to a cloud, Google Gemma 4 is worth knowing about.
