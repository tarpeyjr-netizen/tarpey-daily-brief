Google Gemini

This Week in Google Gemini

Google Gemini is Google's family of AI models — think of it as the brains behind everything from the Gemini chat app to the smart features baked into your Pixel phone and Google Workspace. If you've used Google Docs lately and had an AI suggest an edit, that was Gemini. This past week, Google kept stacking updates at a pace that's hard to keep up with, and the most interesting thread running through all of them is the same idea: Gemini is becoming less of a chatbot and more of a co-pilot that lives inside your whole digital world.

The biggest developer news was the launch of Managed Agents in the Gemini API, now in public preview. This is a big deal for anyone building apps with AI — instead of setting up your own servers and sandboxes to run AI agents, Google now hosts them for you in secure, isolated Linux environments. The Antigravity Agent dropped alongside this: a managed agent that can autonomously plan tasks, write and run code, manage files, and browse the web inside its own container. On the model front, Gemini 3.5 Flash graduated from preview to generally available, making it the new default for Enterprise users as of June 8th. It's aimed squarely at the kind of sustained, multi-step reasoning that coding and agentic tasks require — and that's a space where every AI company is racing right now. Gemini 3.1 Flash Image and Gemini 3 Pro Image also went GA, bringing native visual understanding to more production workflows.

For regular Pixel users, the June Pixel Drop (published June 16th) was the week's splashiest moment. Gemini Omni 2 arrived on Pixel devices with a fun pitch: just type what you want, and Gemini blends your text, photos, and video clips into a polished video. Music generation joined the party too — describe a vibe or upload a photo, and Gemini writes and produces an original song with lyrics. A new Bubbles feature turns any app into a floating window you can drag around the screen. There's one notable loss this week, though: Gemini CLI and the Code Assist IDE extensions are being deprecated as of June 18th, signaling Google is consolidating its developer story around the Gemini API and Managed Agents platform instead.

The big picture is that Google is betting on Gemini as the connective tissue of its entire product lineup. Shipping updates to Pixel phones, Workspace, Google Photos, enterprise integrations, and autonomous developer agents in a single week is a statement: they want Gemini to be the AI layer you don't have to think about, because it's already everywhere you look.

Meta Llama

This Week in Meta Llama

Meta Llama is Meta's family of open-weight AI models — "open-weight" meaning Meta publishes the model files publicly so anyone can download them, run them on their own hardware, and modify them however they like. That's the big differentiator from ChatGPT or Gemini, where you can only access the model through an API and never touch the underlying weights. Llama has become the backbone of an enormous open-source AI ecosystem, powering everything from local chatbots to enterprise apps that need more privacy than a cloud service allows.

It was a quieter week for fresh Llama announcements, but the longer-term storyline is genuinely worth following. The current headline family — Llama 4 — launched earlier this year with two standout models. Llama 4 Scout packs 17 billion active parameters with a stunning 10-million-token context window (imagine feeding it a stack of novels and asking it to find contradictions), while Llama 4 Maverick runs the same active parameter count but routes through 128 different "expert" sub-networks — a mixture-of-experts architecture that scored above GPT-4o on several leaderboards at launch. Looking ahead, reports this week pointed to Meta actively developing its next models, codenamed "Avocado" (an LLM) and "Mango" (a multimedia generation model), both expected to ship later in 2026. There's also talk of a Llama 4.5 release as a stepping stone before the next major generation arrives.

The more fascinating undercurrent is a strategic tension Meta is navigating. Some reports suggest the company is weighing how much to stay purely open-source versus building more closed, proprietary products, as the compute costs of frontier AI keep climbing. For now, Llama remains open and beloved by developers worldwide — but the decisions Meta makes about Avocado and Mango over the next six months could reshape whether Llama stays the champion of open AI or starts feeling like a carefully managed product line with a marketing team attached.

Muse Spark is now rolling out across Meta AI, Instagram, Facebook, WhatsApp, and even Ray-Ban Meta smart glasses, which means it's reaching a massive everyday audience that probably has no idea they're using a frontier AI model. On the developer side, Meta held its first-ever LlamaCon event, where it announced a limited preview of the Llama API — a developer-friendly way to access Llama 4 Scout and Llama 4 Maverick with one-click API keys and interactive playgrounds. Meta also introduced new Llama Protection Tools and a Llama Defenders Program, giving trusted partners security evaluation tools.

The strategic picture here is fascinating and a little bittersweet for the open-source community that rallied around Llama. Meta is increasingly betting on closed, integrated AI products embedded in its app ecosystem, rather than releasing models for the world to run freely. Muse Spark is proprietary. The Avocado project that preceded it was designed closed-source from the start. The Llama brand still lives, but it increasingly describes a developer API and a legacy of open weights rather than Meta's actual cutting-edge work. For the millions of developers and researchers who built on Llama's openness, this week is a reminder to watch carefully where Meta's true attention now lies.
