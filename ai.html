Microsoft Copilot

This Week in Microsoft Copilot

Microsoft Copilot is the AI assistant Microsoft has been stitching into basically everything it makes — Word, Excel, Outlook, Windows, you name it. Think of it as a helpful coworker who lives inside your software and can draft, summarize, and organize things for you. This week Microsoft kept expanding who actually gets to use it and what it can do once it's there.

The biggest practical change: on July 14, Microsoft shipped an update that quietly removes a technical roadblock for big companies. A lot of large organizations run an older, slower-updating version of Microsoft 365 called the Semi-Annual Enterprise Channel, and until now that version couldn't run Copilot properly. The new update makes that older channel work just like the regular fast-updating one, so employees at those companies can finally see the Copilot button show up in Word and Excel without IT having to do a full system migration first. That's a big deal for adoption — it's the difference between Copilot being a perk for a few teams and being available company-wide. Alongside that, Microsoft also rolled out watermarking for AI-generated video and audio inside Microsoft 365, so when Copilot creates or edits a clip, there's now a visible or audible marker showing it was touched by AI — a nod to the growing worry about not being able to tell real footage from generated footage.

The most surprising move, though, came out of Copilot Studio, the tool businesses use to build their own custom AI agents. Microsoft now lets those agents run on Anthropic's Claude models — Sonnet 4.5, Sonnet 4.6, and Opus — alongside OpenAI's models, giving developers a straight-up choice between rival AI systems inside Microsoft's own product. It's a little like a car company letting you swap in a competitor's engine. Microsoft also added multi-turn conversation testing, so builders can check how an agent handles a whole back-and-forth conversation instead of just one question at a time, and opened up an admin-reviewed path for employees to publish their homemade agents to a company-wide store.

Put together, this week is less about flashy new tricks and more about Copilot becoming sturdier plumbing — easier to roll out at scale, safer around AI-made media, and now genuinely multi-brand under the hood. For everyday users, the SAEC fix in particular means a lot more coworkers are about to see Copilot appear in their apps for the first time.

SAP Joule

This Week in SAP Joule

If you've never heard of SAP Joule, think of it as the AI assistant baked directly into the giant business software that runs payroll, expenses, and supply chains for huge chunks of the corporate world. It doesn't do viral chatbot tricks — it does spreadsheets, approvals, and IT dashboards, which sounds boring until you realize millions of office workers touch it every day.

This week Joule quietly stretched into a few more corners of that world. It picked up AI help inside SAP's Kyma Runtime dashboard, so engineers can now ask it to check cluster health or pull pod logs instead of digging through raw Kubernetes data themselves. Over in Concur Expense, Joule can now create, edit, submit, or even recall an expense report through plain conversation, and it'll flag a missing receipt or a mismatched total before you hit submit. Concur Travel got the same treatment, letting people search and book flights, hotels, and rental cars just by chatting instead of clicking through forms. SAP also tidied up the assistant's interface itself, expanding the "What's New" log so past updates don't vanish, and cleaning up the response panel so actions like retry, copy, and feedback all sit on one tidy row.

None of this is flashy, but that's kind of the point. Joule isn't trying to win a headline war — it's trying to become the default way employees talk to enterprise software, one expense report and one travel booking at a time. For regular office workers, it means fewer tedious forms and more "just ask" moments creeping into daily routines.

Einstein AI

This Week in Einstein AI

Einstein is Salesforce's original AI brain — the part that scores leads, predicts which deals will close, and suggests what a sales rep should say next. Lately Salesforce has been drawing a sharper line between Einstein, which predicts things, and its newer sibling Agentforce, which actually goes and does things autonomously inside your CRM.

This week's real story is less about a shiny new feature and more about a reality check. Adoption numbers for Agentforce are still modest, with only around 5% of eligible customers using it and roughly seven in ten early deployments running into data-quality headaches serious enough to stall them out. Salesforce is responding by shoring up the plumbing rather than adding flash: Setup Powered by Agentforce, still in beta, lets admins configure the system using natural language instead of digging through settings menus, and Agentforce 3 added a proper observability dashboard so IT teams can actually see whether their AI agents are healthy, stuck, or quietly failing. Pricing has also settled into a clearer $2-per-conversation model, which at least gives companies a number to budget against.

The takeaway is that autonomous AI agents inside business software are still in the "measure twice, cut once" phase. Salesforce is spending this stretch building the dashboards and guardrails that make trusting an AI agent with real customer data feel less like a leap of faith.

Kimi K3 (Moonshot AI)

Kimi K3 is a new open-source AI model from the Chinese startup Moonshot AI, and it just became the biggest openly released AI model on the planet at 2.8 trillion parameters. That number sounds abstract, but the practical version is this: it's a free-to-download model that reportedly goes toe-to-toe with top systems from OpenAI and Anthropic on hard reasoning and coding tests, while only "waking up" a fraction of its brain for any single question, which keeps it cheaper to run than its size implies. It can also read a full 1-million-token document in one go and reason with images natively, not just text.

Why it matters: this is the kind of release that rattles markets, the same way DeepSeek's debut did back in 2025, because it shows frontier-level AI is no longer locked behind a handful of expensive American labs. Researchers, startups, and hobbyists who want a top-tier model they can actually inspect, modify, or self-host now have a genuinely serious option, with full weights due out by July 27. It's a big deal for anyone tracking who controls the cutting edge of AI.

ChatGPT

This Week in ChatGPT

If you've ever used ChatGPT to ask a quick question, you already know the basics — it's OpenAI's chatbot, the one that kicked off this whole AI wave. But this week it stopped acting like a chatbot and started acting like a coworker who never sleeps.

The headline news is GPT-5.6, which OpenAI split into three flavors: Sol, the flagship brain for hard problems; Terra, a balanced middle option; and Luna, a lightweight version built for speed. Riding on top of that new engine is ChatGPT Work, a brand-new agent that can dig through your connected apps and files and actually produce documents, spreadsheets, and presentations for you — less "answer my question" and more "do my homework." At the same time, OpenAI quietly folded its separate coding tool, Codex, right into the ChatGPT desktop app, so instead of juggling two apps you now get Chat, Work, and Codex living under one roof. There's a bittersweet note too: the Atlas web browser OpenAI launched not long ago is being shut down, with the plug pulled on August 9.

Smaller but genuinely useful upgrades landed alongside the big stuff. Custom instructions — the notes you give ChatGPT about how you like it to behave — got a huge length boost, from 1,500 characters up to 5,000, so power users can finally write a real instruction manual instead of a haiku. Search also got a serious glow-up, letting you dig through past chats, projects, images, and documents all in one place on web, iPhone, and Android, plus a new dictation model that's noticeably better at understanding accents and different languages.

Put together, the message is clear: OpenAI wants ChatGPT to stop being a place you visit for answers and start being a tool that finishes tasks while you do something else. For everyday users, that mostly means less app-switching and more "just ask it to handle it" moments — though as with any new agent, it's worth double-checking its work before you hit send on that spreadsheet.

Mistral AI

This Week in Mistral AI

Mistral is the scrappy French AI lab that's built a name for itself by making powerful, often open-source models without the enormous marketing budget of its American rivals. This week it proved that reputation isn't just about chatbots — it's branching into robots, documents, and even its own hardware.

The most eye-catching release is Robostral Navigate, Mistral's first model built specifically to steer robots. Feed it a single camera feed and a plain-English instruction like "go to the kitchen," and the 8-billion-parameter model figures out how to get there, having learned entirely in simulation before being handed real-world tasks. Right alongside that, Mistral shipped Mistral OCR 4, a document-reading model that doesn't just pull out text but also draws boxes around it, classifies what kind of content it found, and tells you how confident it is — and it does this in 170 languages, all inside a single self-hosted container. Independent testers reportedly preferred it over every other OCR tool they tried, which is a strong showing for a company often overshadowed by bigger names.

On the infrastructure side, Mistral CEO Arthur Mensch confirmed a new "sparse" mixture-of-experts model is already in early access with partners, hinting at a bigger open-weight release down the line. The company is also building its own dedicated 10-megawatt inference facility outside Paris, set to open later this year, which is basically Mistral saying "we don't want to depend on renting someone else's computers anymore." Meanwhile, its Le Chat assistant picked up a new "Vibe" agent that can handle long, multi-step work — catching up your inbox, running research, drafting documents, and even writing and shipping code changes on its own.

Taken together, this week shows Mistral spreading well beyond text chat into robotics, document processing, and self-owned data centers. It's a reminder that the AI race isn't only about who has the smartest chatbot — it's increasingly about who controls the widest range of tools and the infrastructure underneath them.

None of this is flashy by itself, but stacked together it shows a tool maturing past the "cool demo" phase into something built for people who need it to just work, without surprises, day after day.

Anthropic Claude Cowork

This Week in Claude Cowork

Cowork is Anthropic's mode for Claude that goes beyond chatting and actually does file and task work for you — think of it less like texting a smart friend and more like handing a capable assistant the keys to your desktop. This week Cowork officially broke out of its cage. It started life as a desktop-only feature, but Anthropic just announced it's spreading to the web and to phones, with the rollout beginning July 7 for Claude Max subscribers before it trickles down to other plans.

The headline change is that Cowork tasks no longer need your laptop to stay open and awake. You can kick off a task on your desktop, close the lid, and Claude keeps working in the background — even scheduled tasks can run with zero personal devices online. Then you can check progress or redirect the work from your phone later, like leaving a contractor working on your house and just checking in through a doorbell camera instead of standing over their shoulder all day. Anthropic also merged Chat and Cowork into a single home tab, so there's now one sidebar, one search bar, and one shared home for Projects and Artifacts instead of two separate worlds. On top of that, Claude picked up write access to Microsoft 365 tools, meaning it can now draft and send emails, manage calendar events, and create or update OneDrive and SharePoint files directly.

To celebrate the expansion, Anthropic is keeping its doubled Cowork usage limits in place through August 5. The bigger picture here is that Anthropic is betting Cowork becomes less like a chat window and more like an always-on coworker who never needs you to babysit it — a meaningful shift for anyone using Claude to manage real work instead of just answering questions.

🔍 Tool Spotlight

ZML / LLMD

ZML is a Paris-based AI infrastructure startup, and this week it launched LLMD, a free server that lets AI models run efficiently across almost any chip — Nvidia, AMD, Google's TPUs, Apple's Metal chips, and Intel Arc included. Normally, getting an AI model to run fast means being locked into whichever chip it was built for, kind of like a video game that only works on one console. LLMD acts as a universal adapter, letting popular open-source models like Llama, Mistral, Gemma, and Qwen run at full speed no matter what hardware is underneath, with an interface that's compatible with the same API standard OpenAI uses.

Why it matters: AI companies are currently fighting over scarce, expensive GPU capacity, and LLMD gives developers more freedom to shop around for cheaper or more available chips instead of being stuck. It's aimed at developers and companies running their own AI models rather than everyday users, but its ripple effects — cheaper, faster AI services — could reach everyone. What's surprising is the backing: ZML counts Turing Award winner Yann LeCun, and the founders of Hugging Face and Docker among its investors, a serious vote of confidence for a 20-person startup barely a year old.

Google Gemini

This Week in Google Gemini

Gemini is Google's family of AI models, the brain behind everything from the Gemini app on your phone to the "Fill" button quietly working inside Google Sheets. It's Google's answer to the question "what if AI was baked into every product we make," and this week it got a little more baked in.

Alongside that, Google opened up public preview access to Managed Agents in the Gemini API, including a general-purpose one called the Antigravity Agent that can plan, reason, write and run code, manage files, and browse the web, all inside its own secure sandbox. That's a meaningful shift: instead of just answering questions, Gemini can now be handed a task and left to actually go do it, safely boxed away from your real computer. On the more everyday side, "Fill with Gemini" in Google Sheets, the feature that auto-completes spreadsheet data using AI, expanded into eleven more languages including Mandarin, Dutch, Hebrew, and Swedish, so a lot more people around the world just got a genuinely useful shortcut.

Google also nudged Gemini deeper into classrooms and offices this week. Students and teachers can now attach interactive Gemini Canvas creations, like quizzes and infographics, directly to Google Classroom assignments, and Gemini Enterprise added a public preview connector for Asana, letting business users search and manage projects without leaving the Gemini interface. Meta Llama

This Week in Meta Llama

Llama is Meta's line of AI models, famous for being "open" in a way most big AI companies' models aren't, meaning developers and researchers can download and tinker with them directly instead of only accessing them through an API. That openness built Llama a huge following, but this week's news says more about where Meta's attention has drifted than about Llama itself.

The standout release was Muse Image, a new image-generation tool that launched inside the Meta AI app on July 7 and is now showing up across Instagram, WhatsApp, and Meta's ad tools. Here's the catch: Muse Image isn't a Llama product. That's a real change in direction for a company that spent years positioning itself as the champion of open-source AI. Meanwhile, on the Llama side specifically, Meta has been focused less on flashy new models and more on infrastructure and goodwill: rolling out a developer-facing Llama API in limited preview, launching Llama Protection Tools and a "Llama Defenders Program" to help partners test their systems for security holes, and handing out over $1.5 million across ten international Llama Impact Grant winners.

So it was a fairly quiet week for Llama the model itself, but a telling one for Llama the strategy. If Meta keeps leaning on proprietary models like Muse Spark for its flashiest features while Llama becomes more of a developer-and-partner ecosystem play, that's worth watching for anyone who picked Llama specifically because it was free and open.
