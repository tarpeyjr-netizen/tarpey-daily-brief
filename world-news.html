Sunday, June 28, 2026 · Asia

South Korea

Asia · population ~52M

Politics & Government

South Korea's ruling Democratic Party scored a landslide victory in the June 3 local elections, winning 12 of 16 metropolitan mayor and governor races while the opposition People Power Party held only 4 seats, including the Seoul mayorship. The vote was marred by a severe ballot shortage — the National Election Commission had printed only ~50% of required ballots, causing depletion in 91 districts and some polling stations running until 10 PM; NEC Chairman Rho Tae-ak subsequently resigned. President Lee Jae Myung's political honeymoon is fading after one year in office, with Democratic Party chairman Jung Chung-rae stepping down ahead of an intra-party leadership contest and polls showing declining public approval.

Economy & Business

South Korea's GDP surged 1.7% quarter-on-quarter in Q1 2026 — its strongest quarterly gain since 2020 — prompting the OECD to raise its full-year growth forecast to 2.6%, the only upward revision among G20 economies. Annual exports are projected to reach an all-time high of $924.4 billion, with semiconductors now accounting for 42% of total exports after a 169% year-on-year surge driven by global AI infrastructure investment. In a landmark shift, SK Hynix dethroned Samsung Electronics as the country's most valuable listed company for the first time in over 25 years, while the KOSPI benchmark index has risen roughly 90% since January — the best-performing major index in the world.

Military & Security

The 2026 U.S. National Defense Strategy formally assessed that South Korea is now capable of taking primary responsibility for deterring North Korea, signalling a significant shift in alliance burden-sharing; Seoul has pledged to raise defence spending to 3.5% of GDP and purchase $25 billion in U.S. military equipment. North Korea, meanwhile, is expanding port facilities at Nampo and Chongjin to accelerate construction of a green-water navy, including a reported 10,000-ton warship. Domestic security analysts flagged heightened alertness along the DMZ corridor in North Chungcheong province following rhetorical escalation from Pyongyang toward Seoul.

Society & Culture

Young Seoulites are finding unexpected community in the ongoing election-integrity protests sparked by the June 3 ballot shortage, with politically active youth describing the demonstrations as a rare space for civic belonging. Education chiefs beginning four-year terms on July 1 face urgent calls to address severe learning and behavioural deficits among the "pandemic generation" — students who experienced COVID-19 lockdowns during critical developmental years. The long-running 2024–2026 medical crisis, triggered by government quotas expanding medical school admissions, continues to strain hospitals as thousands of resident doctors who resigned have yet to fully return.

Science & Technology

South Korea's Ministry of Science and ICT launched a ₩22.5 billion AI+S&T innovation programme targeting bio, materials, earth science, nuclear fusion, and batteries over four years, while overall government R&D in strategic technologies will rise 30% to ₩8.6 trillion in 2026. The "K-On-Device AI Semiconductor" project will invest $664 million through 2030 to develop 10 indigenous on-device AI chips. Semiconductor clusters in Yongin and Pyeongtaek — anchored by Samsung and SK Hynix fabs — are on track to attract $413 billion in private investment by 2047, cementing Korea's position at the centre of the global AI hardware supply chain.

Saturday, June 27, 2026 · Europe

Germany

Europe · population ~84M

Politics & Government

Germany's grand coalition of CDU/CSU and SPD is facing mounting instability, with nearly 70% of Germans rating the federal government's performance as poor in May 2026 and Chancellor Friedrich Merz hitting a 71% disapproval rating. Internal coalition disputes — including a standoff over funding for student financial aid (BAföG) — have reignited fears of an early collapse, with state elections in Saxony-Anhalt and other Länder in September set to be a critical test. Meanwhile, the far-right AfD leads national polls at 28%, ahead of the CDU at 24%, as the window for meaningful policy action before autumn narrows.

Economy & Business

Economic forecasters have sharply downgraded Germany's growth outlook to just 0.5% for 2026, as high energy costs, stagnating exports, and rising social contributions continue to weigh on industry and households. The ifo Business Climate Index inched up to 84.9 in May, suggesting companies are slightly less pessimistic, though exports are projected to broadly stagnate after three consecutive years of contraction. A new government fiscal package focused on defense and infrastructure is providing some demand support, but structural challenges in key export-oriented sectors — including the automotive and chemicals industries — remain unresolved.

Military & Security

Germany has unveiled its first-ever comprehensive military concept, positioning the Bundeswehr as the strongest conventional army in Europe, with a record €108 billion defence budget for 2026 approved by the Bundestag — €82.7 billion in core funding plus €25.5 billion from the Bundeswehr special fund. Troop numbers are set to grow by up to 10,000 next year, and €9 billion annually has been earmarked for strengthening partner nations including Ukraine. Germany's dramatically expanded NATO role — contributing the alliance's second-largest capability package — reflects the government's response to ongoing Russian aggression in Ukraine and incursions into European airspace.

Society & Culture

Germany's Gen Z is pushing back against the country's historic rearmament drive: in March 2026, over 50,000 students marched in 130 cities under the slogan "The rich want war — the youth, a future," protesting the likely reintroduction of military conscription. Separately, environmental groups including Greenpeace and Fridays for Future staged demonstrations in May against the government's plans to expand gas power infrastructure, with rallies in Hamm and other cities. Human rights observers have also flagged growing restrictions on Palestine solidarity protests, raising concerns about freedom of assembly in Germany.

Science & Technology

Germany's Federal Agency for Disruptive Innovation (SPRIND) launched the Quantum Sensing Initiative on June 24, combining quantum sensor data with AI for real-world actionable insights, as part of a broader €18 billion High-Tech Agenda targeting AI, quantum technologies, microelectronics, biotechnology, nuclear fusion, and climate-neutral transport. The government has also unveiled a new national technology transfer agency and plans to recruit international scientists, while making it easier for universities to collaborate on defence research. India and Germany concluded a high-level meeting in early June to expand cooperation in quantum communication and space technologies.

Friday, June 26, 2026 · Asia

Iraq

Asia · population ~42M

Politics & Government

Iraq's new Prime Minister Ali al-Zaidi, whose government secured a confidence vote in May, is navigating intense pressure from both Washington and Tehran as he works to stabilize a fractured political landscape. The top U.S. diplomat in Baghdad warned that Washington would not partner with any cabinet including U.S.-designated terrorist factions, prompting Zaidi to exclude Sadiqoon bloc members from ministerial posts despite it being the third-largest Shia party. In a landmark move, powerful cleric Muqtada al-Sadr announced the dissolution of his Saraya al-Salam militia and its integration into Iraq's national command structure — a potential turning point in the long push to consolidate state control over armed groups. However, Kataib Hezbollah and Harakat Hezbollah al-Nujaba have rejected disarmament, underscoring the limits of the new government's authority over the broader militia ecosystem.

Economy & Business

The IMF projects Iraq's GDP will contract by 6.8% in 2026, the sharpest decline in the MENA region, as the U.S.–Iran conflict has slashed oil export revenues by an estimated 70%. Oil accounts for over 90% of government income, leaving the state budget severely exposed to regional shocks even as a ceasefire has been in place since April 8. Earlier in the year, Iraq unveiled $450 billion in investment opportunities at the Baghdad Investment Forum, signaling its longer-term ambitions for economic diversification. The World Bank is also providing $900 million to upgrade Iraq's road infrastructure, aiming to create jobs and improve connectivity as the country braces for post-conflict reconstruction.

Military & Security

The 2026 Iran war — which ran from late February through a ceasefire on April 8 — drew Iraq into the crossfire of U.S.–Israeli strikes targeting Iranian proxies on Iraqi soil, triggering airport closures in Baghdad and Erbil and unprecedented strain on the country's sovereignty. While the ceasefire holds, IOM's June 1 displacement update documents continued cross-border mobility disruptions and fragile humanitarian access in multiple governorates. Asaib Ahl al-Haq and Kata'ib al-Imam Ali have pledged to place weapons under state authority, but hardline factions including Kataib Hezbollah remain in open defiance, leaving Iraq's security landscape deeply uncertain as it manages the post-OIR transition in U.S.–Iraq security relations.

Society & Culture

Thousands of unemployed graduates staged protests in Baghdad in May demanding government job guarantees in the 2026 budget, reflecting deep frustration with a public sector stretched by fiscal contraction. Medical interns planned a strike beginning June 1 over chronic salary delays and the replacement of permanent positions with short-term contracts — part of a broader pattern of health-worker stoppages that have reduced public hospitals to emergency-only care. In the Kurdistan Region, unpaid teacher salaries continue to fuel school closures and threaten educational continuity for hundreds of thousands of students. Iraq still hosts over one million internally displaced persons, the majority in urban informal settlements, though the pace of return to Ninewa governorate has slowed amid renewed regional tensions.

Music · Baghdad & diaspora

The viral spread has given a global platform to grassroots Iraqi creativity that thrived in local coffee shops and street weddings long before any algorithm noticed it — and is now reshaping how Iraqi identity travels across borders.

Thursday, June 25, 2026 · Africa

Ethiopia

Africa · population ~130M

Politics & Government

Ethiopia held its 7th General Election on June 1, with Prime Minister Abiy Ahmed's Prosperity Party securing an overwhelming majority in the federal parliament and regional councils; the National Election Board announced final results on June 21. The vote was marred by significant exclusions — polling was not held in Tigray or in 30 Amhara constituencies due to militia activity and conflict, and voting was interrupted at over 143 stations across Oromia and Amhara. Analysts and observers had warned in advance that the election could not be free or fair without deeper political reforms. Ethiopia's foreign minister also reiterated the country's push for maritime access to the sea, framing it as a structural necessity for long-term trade and energy security.

Economy & Business

The IMF projects Ethiopia's economy will expand at 7.1% in 2026, making it one of sub-Saharan Africa's fastest-growing economies, driven by resilient agricultural production, gold mining gains, and sustained domestic demand. Ethiopia showcased its investment credentials at a London diplomatic networking event in June, with Ambassador Biruk Mekonnen highlighting the Grand Ethiopian Renaissance Dam, new industrial parks, and transport corridors as pillars of the country's growth story. However, a persistent gap between the official and parallel foreign exchange markets — banks selling birr near 151 per dollar while the parallel rate tops 180 — remains a structural challenge, as does an unresolved $1 billion Eurobond restructuring.

Military & Security

Renewed fighting between Ethiopian federal troops and the Tigray People's Liberation Front (TPLF) has reignited concerns about a return to full-scale war. In May, the TPLF ousted regional leader Tadesse Werede and reinstated hardliner Debretsion Gebremichael, who led the region during the 2020–2022 conflict. Central to the standoff are Amhara militia forces still occupying parts of western Tigray in violation of the 2022 Pretoria Agreement. Clashes erupted in January and have continued sporadically, while in June, internally displaced persons in Tigray staged a three-day protest outside regional government offices demanding safe and voluntary returns.

Science & Technology

Ethiopia's national grid has surged to a record 9.7 gigawatts of installed capacity following the full integration of the Grand Ethiopian Renaissance Dam and the inauguration of the Aysha II wind farm, one of the largest in the region. Over 96% of Ethiopia's energy now comes from renewables, positioning it among global leaders in green power. The country has also won the bid to host the 2027 UN Climate Summit, reflecting growing international recognition of its environmental leadership and its role as a potential regional energy exporter.

Wednesday, June 24, 2026 · South America

Chile

South America · population ~19M

Politics & Government

President José Antonio Kast, who took office on March 11, 2026, has moved quickly to stamp the new administration's hard-right agenda on Chilean governance. His government launched Chile Sale Adelante, a seven-measure economic package that includes freezing public transport fares in Santiago through year-end and providing regional price-support funds. In May, Kast reshuffled his cabinet, replacing the Minister of Public Security and the Government Spokesperson. He also signed a Cancer Health Alert decree on March 23, empowering health agencies to fast-track treatment waiting lists and collaborate with the private sector. The new administration represents the most right-wing government Chile has seen since the end of the Pinochet era, ending four years under Gabriel Boric's center-left coalition.

Economy & Business

Chile's GDP contracted 0.3% in Q1 2026 quarter-on-quarter, though analysts project full-year growth of around 2.4%, underpinned by robust copper exports and gradually recovering domestic demand. The country remains the world's largest copper producer, accounting for roughly a quarter of global output, and thirteen major copper projects worth $14.8 billion are expected to hit key milestones this year alone. GDP per capita reached an estimated $20,240 in 2026, up sharply from $17,735 in 2025, partly reflecting currency movements alongside real economic gains. Trade led growth in Q4 2025 at 7.2%, with manufacturing, transportation, and personal services also contributing positively.

Military & Security

President Kast has dramatically escalated Chile's border security posture, ordering the military to begin digging a trench along the northern border with Peru as part of a hardline crackdown on irregular migration. The administration has deployed a joint task force of 3,000 army and Carabineros personnel with authority to detain and deport undocumented migrants within a 10-kilometre exclusion zone. Chile's foreign-born population doubled over the past five years to nearly 9%, with much of the influx arriving from Venezuela, and crime linked to migration became a central issue in the 2025 election. Security analysts at CSIS note that Kast's moves signal a far deeper role for the armed forces in domestic public security than any government since the return to democracy.

Society & Culture

Student protests swept high schools and universities across Chile in June 2026, with demonstrators opposing austerity cuts to education funding, the criminalization of youth, and rising violence in schools. Youth unemployment (ages 15–24) sits at approximately 21.6%, compounding social tensions in a country still grappling with the aftermath of pandemic-era school closures and inadequate mental health support. In a positive cultural development, Chile moved forward with new heritage legislation recognizing memorial sites — particularly those tied to the Pinochet dictatorship — as legally protected sites of living memory, a long-standing demand from civil society groups. Applications for the Global Teacher Prize Chile 2026 also opened in early June, spotlighting educators working to reverse pandemic learning losses.

Science & Technology

Chile's copper mining sector is accelerating a wave of technological innovation in 2026, with autonomous haul truck fleets and remotely operated drilling reducing fuel consumption by 27% at major operations like Escondida. Water scarcity in the Atacama Desert has driven the industry to expand desalination capacity — 20 of Chile's 24 desalination plants already serve mining operations, and by 2033, 71% of mining water is projected to come from seawater. Thirteen megaprojects filed simultaneously earlier this year signal a structural shift in how the industry plans and coordinates large-scale capital investment. These advances respond to surging global copper demand driven by electrification, power grid buildout, and the clean energy transition.

Tuesday, June 23, 2026 · South America

Colombia

South America · population ~52M

Politics & Government

Colombia's political landscape was upended on June 21 when far-right candidate Abelardo de la Espriella narrowly won the presidential runoff with 49.65% of the vote, defeating left-wing senator Iván Cepeda of President Gustavo Petro's ruling Historic Pact by less than a percentage point. De la Espriella — a criminal defence attorney and political outsider who ran on a promise of 90-day military offensives against armed groups — received the endorsement of President Trump and blamed Petro for Colombia's security and economic deterioration. International observers deemed the vote orderly and transparent, though Petro had alleged fraud after the first round; the winner is set to take office on August 7. The result leaves Colombia deeply divided, with opposition parties well-positioned to block de la Espriella's agenda in congress.

Economy & Business

Colombia's economy expanded 2.6% in 2025 and is forecast to hold above 2% in 2026, but a 23.7% minimum wage hike this year has stoked inflation fears, with core inflation re-accelerating to 5.5% and the central bank raising its policy rate to 11.25%. The fiscal deficit reached 6.2% of GDP after the fiscal rule was suspended, pushing public debt above 61% of GDP, while foreign direct investment plummeted by a third to US$11.5 billion in 2025 compared to US$17.2 billion when Petro took office in 2022. Markets responded cautiously to the election result, with the Colcap index slipping on oil price drag; investors are watching whether de la Espriella will reverse Petro-era energy and tax policies. Financial and insurance services are projected to be the top-performing sector in 2026, with growth forecast at 6.7%.

Military & Security

President-elect de la Espriella has vowed to launch immediate military action against guerrilla groups on taking office, stating he will "give the order to bomb all camps holding narco-terrorists" and authorise the military to shoot down aircraft and sink boats used in cocaine trafficking. Security analysts warn that armed groups — including dissident FARC factions, the ELN, and the Gaitanist Gulf Clan — have nearly doubled in size to roughly 27,000 fighters under Petro, with recent bombings and mass-casualty incidents interpreted as demonstrations of strength ahead of anticipated negotiations. Colombia is also bracing for cross-border instability from Venezuela following a major external intervention and the reported capture of President Nicolás Maduro, with analysts warning of potential migration waves and spillover violence at the frontier. The UN Security Council has urged Colombia to protect former combatants and accelerate rural reforms as the 2016 peace agreement marks its tenth anniversary.

Society & Culture

Thousands of leftist Colombians took to the streets of Bogotá and Cali on election night to protest de la Espriella's victory, with demonstrators vowing "resistance" to right-wing rule; protesters in Cali burned US flags while riot police deployed tear gas. Indigenous music provided a soundtrack to the marches through Cali's Puerto Resistencia neighbourhood, a flashpoint from the 2021 national strike. Young demonstrators gathered outside Bogotá's national university, with one protester declaring: "For every injustice we see or every right they take away from us, there will be a new protest." The election result has deepened Colombia's generational and geographic divides, with the left drawing heavily from urban youth and the Pacific coast while de la Espriella's base spans rural regions and the Caribbean coast.
