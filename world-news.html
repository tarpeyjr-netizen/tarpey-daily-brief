Saturday, July 4, 2026 · Asia

China

Asia · population ~1,410M

Politics & Government

President Xi Jinping marked the Communist Party's 105th founding anniversary on July 1 with a 40-minute speech at the Great Hall of the People urging cadres to adapt to changing circumstances while "safeguarding advances," a nod to headwinds from slowing growth and demographic decline. The same week, the Ministry of Public Security began piloting a national digital identification system for online verification, drawing concern from rights defenders over expanded tracking capability. On the diplomatic front, Foreign Minister Wang Yi opened a tour of Nordic nations on July 2, part of Beijing's continued outreach to Europe.

Economy & Business

China's economy picked up pace in June, with manufacturing and retail sales improving on the back of rebounding U.S.-bound exports, which analysts say were up around 15% over the year's first four months. Beijing has set a 2026 GDP growth target of 4.5-5%, slightly below last year's goal, while the 10% Section 122 tariff on goods from major trading partners is set to expire July 24, prompting exporters to rush shipments in case rates surge again. Regulators have meanwhile elevated a slate of "future industries" — semiconductors, advanced displays, aerospace, and the low-altitude drone economy — as long-term growth engines, with new CAAC certification rules for drone manufacturing taking effect this month.

Military & Security

A new Pentagon assessment released last week described China's military expansion as "historic," projecting Beijing will field six additional aircraft carriers by 2035 — nine total, just behind the U.S. fleet of eleven — and that its nuclear arsenal will exceed 1,000 warheads by 2030. Separately, Reuters reported that covert Chinese military training of Russian forces was personally approved by Russia's defence minister, with generals from both countries directly involved — a revelation that has alarmed European officials. On the diplomatic side, U.S. President Trump has accepted an invitation to visit China and invited Xi for a reciprocal state visit, describing the relationship as "extremely strong."

Society & Culture

A controversial "Law on Ethnic Unity and Progress" took effect July 1, which human rights groups say enshrines forced assimilation of Tibetans, Uyghurs, and Mongolians; parliamentarians from Estonia, Latvia, and Lithuania have called for Europe-wide condemnation. Freedom House's 2026 country report notes that civic and labor protests have risen at the local level despite police repression, with economic grievances like unpaid wages and land seizures driving roughly 85% of unrest, even as overtly political dissent remains rare given the risk of severe penalties.

Science & Technology

China launched a new marine satellite on July 2 and dispatched research vessels on their 16th Arctic expedition on July 3, continuing a build-out of polar and ocean-monitoring capability noted by state science outlets. Longer-running efforts to close the country's gap with the U.S. in drug discovery and biotech continue to draw fresh funding, part of a five-year push to make biotech and alternative energy new pillars of the economy.

🔥 Trending
Sileme ("Are You Dead?")
Tech · Nationwide (urban China)

A bluntly named check-in app called Sileme, or "Are You Dead?", has held onto grassroots buzz across China's app-store charts after climbing to the top with zero ad spend. Users living alone tap a daily button to confirm they're okay; two missed check-ins trigger an alert to an emergency contact. Its creators are Gen-Z developers who say they built it out of the isolation of urban solo living rather than for the elderly market many assumed it targeted, and its organic spread has surfaced a wider conversation about loneliness and unmet eldercare needs. The app's continued attention has even prompted talk of a rebrand as its creators respond to the scrutiny.

Thursday, July 3, 2026 · Europe

United Kingdom

Europe · population ~68M

Politics & Government

Prime Minister Keir Starmer announced his resignation this week after a mounting Labour Party rebellion, triggered in part by Greater Manchester Mayor Andy Burnham's decisive parliamentary by-election victory in Makerfield. The UK is set to appoint its seventh prime minister in ten years, with a formal Labour leadership contest beginning July 9 and concluding before the summer recess. Meanwhile, the House of Lords wrapped its June 29–July 3 business schedule covering debates on burial plots, lobbying transparency, and nature's rights. Upcoming elections include a Norfolk Police and Crime Commissioner by-election on July 16 and a Greater Manchester mayoral by-election on July 30.

Economy & Business

The UK economy showed mixed signals heading into July: GDP grew 0.6% in Q1 2026 but fell 0.1% in April, while inflation remains above target at 2.8% and real household disposable income dropped 0.8% in the first quarter. In a significant trade development, the UK-India Comprehensive Economic and Trade Agreement is set to enter into force on July 15, and a tightened steel safeguard effective July 1 cut tariff-free import quotas by 60%. Business confidence weakened sharply in June, with the IoD sentiment index falling to minus 61, and 23% of hospitality businesses now operating at a loss—up from 15% three months ago.

Military & Security

In one of the biggest defence announcements in years, Prime Minister Starmer unveiled a nearly £300 billion defence investment plan to modernise Britain's armed forces, raising spending to 2.7% of GDP by 2029 and targeting 3.5% by 2035. The plan prioritises self-flying fighter jets, uncrewed submarines, and drones, with Special Forces receiving a 12% funding uplift. British forces boarded a sanctioned shadow-fleet oil tanker in the Channel last month, and the UK pledged 150,000 Ukrainian-produced drones and over 350 air defence missiles to Kyiv. Starmer is expected to present the plan at the NATO summit in Ankara on July 7–8.

Society & Culture

Anti-immigration protests flared in Belfast and Southampton in early June after a Sudanese man was arrested for a stabbing in Belfast, with UK leaders calling for calm amid demonstrations under the banner "Enough is Enough." On the healthcare front, over 100 medical workers and Amnesty International supporters gathered on Brighton Beach on June 26 to protest the NHS's contract with data firm Palantir, pressing paint-covered hands onto a giant Palantir logo to highlight the company's ties to Israel's military. A cross-party House of Commons committee had previously called for the contract to be terminated.

Science & Technology

The UK government is doubling down on AI with a £1.6 billion strategy covering 2026–2030, including two new research labs at Oxford (SOFAIR, focused on open-source AI) and UCL (BOLD, focused on rethinking machine learning) sharing £60 million in government funding. Google DeepMind will establish its first automated laboratory in the UK this year, dedicated to materials science research. UK Research and Innovation (UKRI) is channelling investment across six priority areas, from advancing AI development to building world-class data infrastructure, with healthcare and clean energy as headline applications.

🔥 Trending

Music · London

London alt-rock four-piece Keo have had a rapid and genuinely grassroots ascent through the UK's indie circuit, with their debut single I Lied, Amber surpassing 1 million Spotify streams shortly after release and the band graduating from a packed 200-capacity EBGB's set at Liverpool Sound City 2025—fans spilling into the street—to headlining the festival's main stage in May 2026. Thursday, July 2, 2026 · South America

Argentina

South America · population ~46M

Politics & Government

President Javier Milei's government underwent a significant cabinet shake-up as Diego Santilli replaced Manuel Adorni as Cabinet chief after Adorni resigned amid scandal over concealing $500,000 from his financial disclosures. Despite the turbulence, Milei's La Libertad Avanza party emerged from last year's midterms with more than double its previous congressional seats, giving the president increased legislative leverage — though still short of a majority. The Senate approved the 2026 budget 46 to 25, projecting 5% economic growth, annual inflation of 10.1%, and a peso exchange rate near AR$1,423 by December. Meanwhile, Human Rights Watch flagged that nearly 35% of federal judicial positions remain vacant, raising concerns about the independence of the judiciary under Milei's administration.

Economy & Business

Argentina's inflation stabilisation effort showed further progress as June monthly inflation came in at just 1.6%, a remarkable decline from the hyperinflationary crisis Milei inherited. Annual inflation still runs at roughly 33%, but the government points to a poverty rate falling to 28.2% — the lowest since 2018 — and a primary fiscal surplus of 1.4% of GDP. The economy is forecast to grow 3–4% in 2026, buoyed by a $20 billion currency swap deal with the Trump administration. A major tech investment also headlined the week: OpenAI and Argentine-U.S. firm Sur Energy announced Stargate Argentina, a $25 billion, 500MW AI data centre project in Patagonia — the first Stargate initiative in Latin America, with construction expected to begin this year and operations in early 2027.

Military & Security

Argentina deepened its security partnership with the United States this week on two fronts. The U.S. and Argentina co-hosted the 2026 Proliferation Security Initiative Western Hemisphere Engagement in Buenos Aires (June 23–25), drawing more than 80 officials from 20 nations to discuss regional proliferation challenges and interdiction best practices. Separately, Argentina's Navy signed a five-year cooperation agreement with the U.S. Southern Command to boost maritime surveillance in the South Atlantic, targeting illegal fishing and other threats under the "Protecting Global Commons Program." The deal drew criticism from opposition lawmakers who warned that the framing of Argentine waters as part of a "global commons" encroaches on national sovereignty.

Society & Culture

Argentina was rocked by nationwide protests after the body of 14-year-old Agostina Vega was found sexually assaulted, dismembered, and hanged in Córdoba — echoing the 2015 killing that sparked the landmark "Ni Una Menos" (Not One Woman Less) movement. Lawyers counted at least 63 registered femicides so far this year, with advocates warning the real toll exceeds 100. The case intensified public anger at President Milei, who has called the feminist movement "ridiculous and unnatural" and defunded programmes supporting gender violence victims. Argentina's social fabric also remains strained by austerity: the National Statistics Institute reports 31.6% of the population living in poverty as of June, with older people and those with disabilities hit hardest.

Science & Technology

The headline technology story is Stargate Argentina — the OpenAI and Sur Energy partnership for a $25 billion AI data centre in Patagonia — which positions Argentina as the first Latin American node in OpenAI's global infrastructure network and a regional hub for AI computing powered by renewable energy. The announcement stands in sharp contrast to deep cuts the Milei government has made to domestic research institutions: CONICET (the national scientific research council), INTA (agricultural technology), and ARSAT (satellites) have all seen budgets slashed and entire programmes eliminated. Scientists warn the hollowing out of public research capacity could undermine the very workforce needed to support Argentina's AI ambitions over the long term.

🔥 Trending
The Therian Movement
Culture / Identity · Buenos Aires

A striking grassroots phenomenon has taken hold among Argentine teenagers: the "therian" movement, in which young people identify mentally, spiritually, or psychologically with non-human animals. Buenos Aires plazas became gathering spaces for teens wearing animal masks, running on all fours, and inhabiting animal personas — beagles, cats, foxes, wolves — in scenes that went viral across TikTok, where #therian surpassed 2 million posts with Argentina leading all Latin American countries in engagement. The movement, which originated in niche online communities in the U.S. and Europe, found particularly fertile ground in Argentina's youth culture, blending identity exploration with community belonging during a period of intense economic anxiety. Critics have dismissed it as a fad, while youth advocates note that the trend reflects a broader search for connection and self-expression among a generation navigating austerity, social pressure, and a rapidly changing digital landscape.

Wednesday, July 1, 2026 · North America

Canada

North America · population ~40M

Politics & Government

Prime Minister Mark Carney capped a busy parliamentary session with a June 25 press conference outlining his government's legislative achievements, including multiple spending and defence bills. On the same day, Carney announced a design-and-build competition open to all Canadian firms to restore 24 Sussex Drive, the official prime ministerial residence. Foreign Affairs Minister Anita Anand held a joint press conference in Ottawa with Turkish counterpart Hakan Fidan, signalling deepening bilateral ties. Meanwhile, the government confirmed a plan to reduce the federal public service by roughly 12,600 positions as part of a drive to cut C$13 billion in annual spending by 2028–29.

Economy & Business

With the CUSMA trade-agreement review deadline falling on July 1, Canada and the United States remained far apart on a new deal, with the Canadian envoy noting that sectoral tariffs on steel, aluminum, lumber, and automotive products are the real sticking point. Deloitte warned that Canada's economy is effectively "on pause," forecasting only 0.7% annualised GDP growth for 2026 after a technical recession spanning October 2025 through March 2026. Canada has given formal notice that it wants CUSMA renewed, but analysts say a full tariff-scrapping deal is unlikely before the U.S. midterms, prolonging economic uncertainty into 2027.

Military & Security

Canada reached the long-sought NATO 2% of GDP defence-spending benchmark in the 2025–26 fiscal year, committing more than C$63 billion across the Department of National Defence, the Canadian Armed Forces, and allied government partners. The Carney government has pledged to reach 3.5% of GDP on core defence by the mid-2030s and has set a target of half a trillion dollars in defence investment over the next decade. Investments are focused on military personnel readiness, new equipment procurement, cyber security, and the domestic defence industrial base, with spending treated increasingly as industrial policy to boost Canadian manufacturing and high-skill jobs.

Society & Culture

The 2026 FIFA World Cup officially kicked off on June 12 at BMO Field in Toronto, with Canada's men's national team opening with a draw against Bosnia and Herzegovina before a star-studded ceremony featuring Alanis Morissette and Michael Bublé. Days later, a national campaign by the Canadian Medical Association urged Parliament to pass the Safe Social Media Act, with physicians reporting rising mental-health impacts from harmful digital environments on youth. A Toronto court also convicted Magna International founder Frank Stronach of sexual assault and indecent assault for incidents spanning the 1970s to 1990s, a verdict that drew wide public attention.

Science & Technology

Prime Minister Carney launched AI for All, Canada's new national artificial-intelligence strategy, pledging AI literacy training for one million post-secondary students, kits for 3,000 educators, and a Sovereign Technology Alliance built on 12 international partnerships. Ontario Tech University simultaneously opened Canada's first School of Ethical Artificial Intelligence, aimed at governance frameworks and responsible AI deployment. A new report from the Blockchain Research Institute argued that Canada must redesign its economic architecture around data, networks, and digital infrastructure to translate its world-class research talent into sustained commercial scale.

🔥 Trending

Music · Toronto, Ontario

Orbital Ensemble is a Brazilian psychedelic jazz collective based in Toronto that burst onto the Canadian music scene in 2025 and has been gathering momentum ever since. The group blends Afro-Brazilian rhythms, free jazz improvisation, and psychedelic textures in a sound that feels genuinely unlike anything else in the Toronto live-music circuit. Their debut full-length Contínua, released June 19, has earned praise from Exclaim! as one of the most compelling Canadian releases of mid-2026, with critics noting the band's ability to make sprawling, 10-minute compositions feel urgent and danceable. Their rise reflects a broader wave of diasporic artists reshaping what Toronto sounds like to the world.

Tuesday, June 30, 2026 · South America

Peru

South America · population ~34M

Politics & Government

Peru's razor-thin runoff election on June 7 ended with Keiko Fujimori — daughter of former authoritarian president Alberto Fujimori and leader of the Fuerza Popular party — defeating left-wing challenger Roberto Sánchez by fewer than 43,000 votes out of roughly 25 million cast, a margin of just 0.12 percent. Overseas ballots proved decisive, arriving late to tip the count in Fujimori's favor after Sánchez had led on domestic votes. On her fourth presidential attempt, Fujimori is now set to be inaugurated on July 28, replacing caretaker President José María Balcázar, though queried ballots are still under review by the National Jury of Elections. The vote crystallized a deep national divide between those seeking a return to hard-right economic and security policies and those backing a renewed left-wing project after Pedro Castillo's turbulent abbreviated presidency.

Economy & Business

Peru's economy expanded 3.5% in Q1 2026, outpacing expectations on the back of strong private consumption, rising employment, and subdued inflation, with the central bank holding its policy rate at 4.25% in May. The fiscal deficit has narrowed sharply from 3.5% of GDP in 2024 to a projected 1.9% in 2026, reflecting disciplined consolidation. Attention is now turning to the incoming Fujimori administration's stance on major infrastructure projects: a long-stalled Southern Gas Pipeline revival is under discussion, and the new Chinchero international airport near Cusco is on track to open in 2026–27. Meanwhile, critical minerals featured prominently at the World Mining Congress, with speakers urging Peru to leverage its copper, lithium, and zinc reserves as strategic assets in the global energy transition.

Military & Security

A state of emergency covering Lima and Callao remained in effect through late June, allowing armed forces to support police operations, yet security analysts noted that homicide rates have not declined despite the deployment. Organized crime networks continued to expand their footprint across the country, driving extortion, territorial violence, and trafficking. Crime was a defining theme of the 2026 presidential campaign, with both candidates pitching divergent security visions — Fujimori emphasizing an iron-fist approach and Sánchez prioritizing social investment. Non-violent offenses such as vehicle theft and snatch-and-run robberies account for roughly 68% of reported incidents, while homicides make up about 9%, underlining the breadth of the security challenge the incoming government inherits.

Society & Culture

Planned demonstrations near Lima's National Jury of Elections headquarters in mid-June reflected ongoing public tension over the contested vote count. Human Rights Watch's 2026 Peru chapter documented a continued backsliding on democratic norms, with Congress passing measures that weakened judicial independence and fostered impunity. In health policy, Peru's National Maternal Perinatal Institute revised its therapeutic abortion guidelines in ways that rights groups say effectively restrict access for women and girls whose lives or health are at risk. The OECD separately urged Peru to address persistently high informal employment and expand access to quality early childhood education, particularly for Indigenous and rural communities.

Science & Technology

Peru's state oil company Petroperu remains under an emergency restructuring framework approved earlier this year. On the infrastructure front, the June 2026 investment newsletter from law firm Echecopar highlighted ProInversión bids for major Lima water supply works (estimated at $779 million) and the Choquequirao cable car public-private partnership, both expected to be awarded in Q4 2026. At the World Mining Congress, delegates stressed that integrating critical minerals into Peru's long-term development agenda is essential to capitalize on the global AI- and EV-driven demand surge. The IAEA also reiterated the importance of nuclear-derived science and technology for development across Latin America, a message that landed in Lima amid growing interest in advanced energy options.

🔥 Trending
Lita Pezo & "Cumbia Amazónica"
Music · Iquitos, Loreto

Lita Pezo, a 27-year-old singer from Iquitos who rose to fame winning The Voice Peru in 2022, has teamed up with Grammy Latino-nominated producer Kayfex and the legendary Los Wembler's de Iquitos to release "Mejor Sin Ti", a cumbia amazónica track filmed entirely in the lush landscapes of the Peruvian jungle. Cumbia amazónica — the psychedelic, reverb-drenched tropical genre born in Amazonian cities like Iquitos and Pucallpa in the 1970s — has long been a cult favourite globally, but the collaboration is generating renewed domestic pride and TikTok traction as younger listeners discover the genre. International music writers have flagged the project as part of a broader Q-Pop and Amazonian revival reshaping Peru's pop landscape. Pezo has said the song fulfilled a lifelong dream: "Siempre tuve ganas de homenajear a mi ciudad cantando una cumbia" — I always wanted to pay tribute to my city by singing a cumbia.

Monday, June 29, 2026 · Oceania

Australia

Oceania · population ~27M

Politics & Government

Prime Minister Anthony Albanese has championed a significant budget shift, with the 2026–27 Federal Budget introducing long-deferred taxation reforms including changes to negative gearing and capital gains tax on housing — positions the government had previously ruled out. Albanese framed these as "hard decisions" needed to boost productivity, and the reforms have triggered intense debate in parliament. The budget also included measures aimed at addressing cost-of-living pressures, with economists describing it as a major policy pivot from Labor's earlier platform. The government pointed to a strong employment record — 1.2 million jobs created over four years and the lowest average unemployment in 50 years — as justification for the bolder reform agenda.

Economy & Business

Australia's economy continued to outpace most major advanced economies, according to the latest National Accounts, with growth driven overwhelmingly by business investment now at its highest share of GDP in a decade. However, inflation remains a concern: the ABS monthly CPI indicator showed inflation at 4.2% year-on-year, driven by fuel and services prices, with underlying measures staying uncomfortably elevated. The Reserve Bank held the cash rate at 4.35%, signalling caution despite positive growth signals. Wages growth has exceeded three per cent in every quarter, and the gender pay gap has hit an all-time low, though cost-of-living remains the dominant political pressure point heading into the second half of 2026.

Military & Security

Australia released its 2026 National Defence Strategy and Integrated Investment Program in April, with Defence Minister Richard Marles pledging an additional A$14 billion over four years and A$53 billion over the decade, targeting defence spending at 3% of GDP by 2033–34. The strategy prioritises self-reliance and deterrence, building on the "denial first" approach introduced in 2024. In June, Australia joined Exercise Valiant Shield 26, deploying 80 ADF personnel across Japan, Hawaii, Guam, and Australia to test Pacific security responses, including a P-8A Poseidon maritime patrol aircraft. The ASPI Defence Conference on June 25 brought together regional security experts under the theme "Securing the Region for Uncertainty."

Society & Culture

Australian educators are pushing back after the Australian Education Union reached a deal with the Labor government that teachers say locks in real pay cuts for four more years and removes the right to strike — a move that followed a major statewide strike action in March. June has also brought a wave of Pride Month events across major cities, with LGBTQIA+ community celebrations gaining official support from local councils and workplaces. The Australian Human Rights Commission's 2026 assessment highlighted rising concerns about racism, inequality, and polarisation as major social fault lines heading into the second half of the decade.

Science & Technology

A new hydrogen breakthrough published in June showed Australian researchers converting waste heat into clean hydrogen fuel, a development with potential implications for industrial decarbonisation. The Australian Academy of Science honoured six top scientists with civic awards spanning mathematics and immunology, while also launching its Restore Science campaign calling for urgent action to reverse declining research investment that risks driving talent overseas. Australia also expanded its association with Horizon Europe and launched a new Australia–Vietnam Science, Technology and Innovation Cooperation Initiative, offering up to A$1.3 million for collaborative research projects.
