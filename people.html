Week ending Friday May 29, 2026

This Week

Pope Leo XIV dominated the headlines with a historic one-two punch: publishing his first encyclical Magnifica Humanitas — a sweeping 42,000-word document on AI and human dignity — and then delivering an unprecedented papal apology for the Catholic Church's centuries-long role in legitimizing slavery. Meanwhile, Magnus Carlsen won the Chess.com Open and beat World Champion Gukesh at Norway Chess, cementing his grip on the game's top tier.

Magnus Carlsen — Chess Player

Update: A dominant week for Carlsen. He won the 2026 Chess.com Open Playoffs on May 20, defeating GM Jan-Krzysztof Duda in a Grand Final reset and earning $50,000. He then headed home for Norway Chess (May 25 – June 5), where in Round 4 on May 28 he delivered the only classical win of the round, beating World Champion Gukesh Dommaraju. Carlsen's tournament form looks resurgent on home turf.

His next novel, The French Illusion, is also set for September 29, 2026 (Doubleday).

Michael Lewis — Author

Sally Rooney — Author

Update: Major story. Rooney announced (May 19–20) that she will publish a Hebrew translation of her bestselling 2024 novel Intermezzo through November Books, an independent Israeli publisher that supports Palestinian rights and does not receive Israeli government funding. Week ending Friday May 22, 2026

This Week

Sally Rooney made international headlines by announcing a Hebrew translation of Intermezzo through a BDS-compliant Israeli publisher — a nuanced move drawing both praise and backlash — while Tyler Cowen published a high-profile piece arguing AI’s biggest threat isn’t unemployment but the psychological cost of adjustment, and Paige Bueckers continued her breakout WNBA sophomore season with 24 points in a Wings win over Chicago.

Updates

Montana Fouts — Softball Player

Fouts, currently playing for the Utah Talons in Athletes Unlimited, was named to the USA Softball Women’s National Team 2026 athlete pool — one of 36 players selected. The pool will compete at the WBSC World Cup Group Stage (September 12–16 in Oklahoma City) and the 2026 USA Softball International Cup, with all players also in consideration for the 2028 Olympic team.

Magnus Carlsen — Chess Player

Carlsen has been dominant across formats in 2026. He won the FIDE Freestyle Chess World Championship, defeating Fabiano Caruana 2.5–1.5 in Weissenhaus, Germany — his 21st world title across formats. He also won the TePe Sigeman tournament in Malmö, Sweden, and the Chess.com Open Playoffs (defeating GM Jan-Krzysztof Duda in the Grand Final for $50,000). He will defend his title at the 2026 Esports World Cup.

John Grisham — Author

Grisham has two major 2026 releases on deck. His nonfiction Shaken: The Rush to Execute an Innocent Man — about death-row inmate Robert Roberson in Texas — publishes June 9 from Doubleday. His espionage thriller The French Illusion, about a Paris honeymoon turned kidnapping, publishes September 29. Pre-publication buzz for Shaken is building with just weeks to go.

Michael Lewis — Author

Lewis has been promoting Who Is Government?: The Untold Story of Public Service, appearing on PBS NewsHour and CBS Evening News to discuss the crucial work of federal employees. He also revealed in February that he has a verbal deal to write a Sam Altman biography — but only once ChatGPT is capable of writing a competing draft. “I want to challenge it to a contest,” Lewis said. No major news in the past week specifically.

Frederik Backman — Author

Sally Rooney — Author

The biggest literary news of the week: Rooney announced that a Hebrew translation of Intermezzo will be published by November Books, an Israeli publisher that supports Palestinian rights, does not operate in West Bank settlements, and receives no Israeli government funding — making it BDS-compliant in her view. The deal was announced alongside a Guardian interview with Palestinian-Irish activist Samir Eskanda. “I was, of course, never boycotting the Hebrew language or any language,” Rooney said. The move generated significant coverage in the Irish Times, Jerusalem Post, Haaretz, and Middle East Eye.
