🏛️ State audit exposes lavish spending, VISA card abuse in Cary

A scathing state audit released Thursday found the Town of Cary handed out an outsized number of government credit cards and racked up tens of thousands of dollars in questionable purchases, including $37,301 for a councilmember's graduate tuition, $65,653 for a ghostwriter to pen a book about former Town Manager Sean Stegall, and $121,314 on video production for a 2024 staff retreat. More than 62% of Cary employees carried a town VISA card — nearly four times the average among North Carolina's largest municipalities — and the 828 cardholders combined for $24.2 million across 60,000-plus transactions between January 2024 and December 2025. State Auditor Dave Boliek said the findings should "result in much-needed financial restraint and discipline," and a separate criminal investigation tied to Stegall's November resignation is continuing.

🌡️ Drought eases only slightly despite recent Triangle rain

The latest classifications from the North Carolina Drought Management Advisory Council, released Thursday based on conditions through July 14, show eight counties in the Triangle and eastern Triad still locked in exceptional drought, the most severe category. "Raleigh still has a 16-inch deficit since August, even with decent rain in the last week," said Linwood Peele of the state Division of Water Resources, noting that "one or two nice rains doesn't fix 10 months of little to no rainfall." Another 38 counties remain in extreme drought and 37 more in severe drought, with reservoirs and streamflow still running below normal across the state.

🚨 Driver charged in July 4 crash that killed RDU shuttle passengers

Raleigh police announced charges Wednesday against Barry Hayes, 70, in connection with a July 4 crash near Raleigh-Durham International Airport that killed Scott and Jennifer Christofferson. Hayes faces two counts of involuntary manslaughter, two counts of misdemeanor death by vehicle, reckless driving, a red-light violation, unsafe tires and driving without a license after police say his car ran a red light at Glenwood Avenue and Lumley Road, striking a Fast Park shuttle and flipping it onto its side. The couple's child, also a passenger, was hospitalized; the shuttle driver was not injured. Scott Christofferson worked as an emergency department pharmacist at UNC Health Rex Hospital in Raleigh.

🏒 Hurricanes to raise second Stanley Cup banner Sept. 29, open season vs. Panthers

The NHL announced Wednesday that Carolina will raise its second Stanley Cup championship banner on Sept. 29 before hosting the Florida Panthers to open the 2026-27 season, pitting the reigning champions against the two-time defending champs from the prior seasons. The Hurricanes lost just one game on their way through the Eastern Conference before beating the Vegas Golden Knights in the Final to win their first title since 2006. The opener kicks off an expanded, 84-game slate — the most games in league history — with the full schedule set for release Thursday.

🏈 ACC Kickoff rolls into Charlotte; Belichick and Tar Heels up Friday

The three-day ACC Football Kickoff is underway at the Hilton Charlotte Uptown, with NC State coach Dave Doeren and quarterback CJ Bailey holding court Wednesday alongside Miami's Mario Cristobal and 2025 ACC Championship MVP Darian Mensah. UNC coach Bill Belichick headlines Friday's session, appearing with defensive lineman Melkart Abou Jaoude, offensive lineman Christo Kelly and receiver Jordan Shipp for a full day of national media hits, while Duke also takes the stage. Wednesday, July 15, 2026

🎓 Wake schools race to finish emergency repairs before fall

The Wake County Board of Education voted 9-0 Monday to approve emergency construction contracts at three elementary schools — Creech Road, Davis Drive and Durant Road — after structural engineers found significant wall cracking and settling tied to a shared 1990s prototype design. The timeline is tight: students at Durant Road are scheduled to return to class July 30, leaving crews little margin for delay. Board members pressed staff during the meeting on whether repairs could realistically be finished in time for the start of the school year. The structural issues add to a summer already dominated by back-to-back Triangle headlines about strained public infrastructure.

🚨 Cyclosporiasis outbreak keeps climbing in Wake County

Wake County launched a new online dashboard to track a fast-growing outbreak of cyclosporiasis, a foodborne parasite that causes explosive diarrhea, after confirmed cases jumped from 154 to more than 200 in just a few days. Wake County accounts for the large majority of North Carolina's cases, and state health officials are still investigating the source. At least 14 people have been hospitalized statewide, five of them in Wake County, though no deaths have been reported. Testing and case tracking continue as officials try to pin down a common source.

🏒 Hurricanes to raise second Cup banner Sept. 29 against Panthers

The NHL released its 2026-27 schedule Wednesday, confirming the Carolina Hurricanes will open the season at home on Sept. 29 against the Florida Panthers, the club that beat them in back-to-back conference finals before Carolina finally broke through this spring. The pregame ceremony will raise the franchise's second championship banner and mark its first Stanley Cup in 20 years. Carolina lost only one game across the entire playoff run, sweeping past Vegas in the Final. Ticket and celebration details for the opener are expected in the coming weeks.

🌡️ Drought eases slightly, but Triangle isn't in the clear

Recent storms nudged Wake and Franklin counties from exceptional to extreme drought, but much of Orange County — including Chapel Hill — remains in the most severe category. Raleigh has picked up 7.87 inches of rain this summer compared with just 2.26 inches in Fayetteville, underscoring how unevenly rainfall has landed across central North Carolina. Raleigh remains under Stage 1 water restrictions while Durham's tighter Stage 2 rules bar most landscape watering and limit restaurants to serving water only on request. Forecasters say scattered weekend storms could bring further relief, though officials estimate nearly three feet of rain is still needed by year's end to fully end the drought.

Tuesday, July 14, 2026

🚨 Retaliatory shooting outside Wilson courthouse wounds three

A shooting Monday morning outside the Wilson County Courthouse left a 19-year-old woman in critical condition and wounded a sheriff's deputy and a Wilson police officer. Investigators say a 21-year-old suspect, now in custody, opened fire on the woman as she walked toward the courthouse around 10:40 a.m., and the officers were struck while confronting him. The Wilson County Sheriff's Office says the attack appears to be retaliation tied to a teenager's murder several weeks earlier, with the wounded woman related to that case. The suspect faces attempted murder and weapons charges after a shootout with officers not far from the scene; the deputy and officer are expected to recover.

🎓 Three Wake elementary schools rushed into emergency repairs before opening

The Wake County Board of Education voted 9-0 Monday to approve emergency construction contracts after structural engineers found unsafe conditions at Durant Road, Davis Drive and Creech Road elementary schools. Officials described the issue as accelerating "settling," with a wall crack at Creech Road that has grown far faster than expected since first appearing in 2024; engineers also found walls at Creech Road weren't built to the original plans. The timeline is tight — Durant Road is a year-round school due back July 30, while Davis Drive and Creech Road start Aug. 24 — and Superintendent Robert Taylor is expected to finalize emergency contracts this week so work can begin as soon as possible.

🏗️ Durham weighs $15M incentive for AbbVie's $1.4B campus

Durham County commissioners held a public hearing Monday night on a proposed $15 million incentive package for AbbVie as the pharmaceutical giant's planned $1.4 billion campus moves toward construction, expected to wrap by the end of 2028. County Commission Chair Mike Lee called the incentive "small" relative to the investment and said the project is "a huge deal for Durham." Durham Tech President JB Buxton said the school and regional community colleges are positioning themselves to supply the skilled workforce pharmaceutical employers are seeking, part of a broader wave of life-sciences investment in the area that includes Novartis' Research Triangle expansion.

📊 Frito-Lay to close Raleigh warehouse, cut nearly 70 jobs

Frito-Lay notified employees it will shut down its distribution warehouse on Midway West Road in Raleigh on Sept. 6, laying off roughly 68 workers. The company said WARN notices went out to all affected staff and that it will share other job openings and try to place some employees at nearby facilities. The closure is part of a broader round of Frito-Lay and parent PepsiCo facility consolidations underway across the country amid softer snack demand.

🚨 Teen charged as adult over July 4 Brier Creek shooting appears in court

Justin Brown, 16, was held without bond Monday after appearing before a Wake County judge on charges including assault with a deadly weapon with intent to kill and inciting a riot, stemming from the Fourth of July "teen takeover" violence in Brier Creek. Police say Brown fired a gun into two occupied cars during a fight among teens — one vehicle held two adults, the other an adult and four children — though no one was seriously hurt. He was appointed a public defender and is due back in court Aug. 3, one of several arrests still working through the system after the holiday weekend drew thousands of teens to Glenwood South and Brier Creek.

🚨 Former WakeMed campus officer charged with child sex crimes

Chad Roger Penland, 47, a former WakeMed campus police officer who also served with the Morrisville Police Department, turned himself in to Johnston County deputies on three felony charges alleging sex crimes against a 15-year-old girl in 2022. He's held on a $350,000 bond. WakeMed said it suspended Penland immediately upon learning of the allegations and that the conduct alleged did not occur on hospital property.

🚨 Cyclospora cases surge in Wake County, four hospitalized

North Carolina health officials are tracking a growing cyclospora outbreak centered in Wake County, which has logged more than 180 of the state's 307 confirmed cases since May 1, with four Wake County patients hospitalized. Public Health Deputy Director Jennifer Brown said the county is working with the CDC and state agriculture officials to trace a common source, though investigators haven't yet linked the illness to a specific ingredient. At WakeMed, providers have seen a 200-300% jump in patients reporting diarrhea and other GI symptoms, and restaurants ahead of Downtown Raleigh Restaurant Week say they're tightening produce-handling practices as a precaution. Health officials recommend thoroughly washing fresh produce and seeking care for prolonged GI symptoms.

🌡️ Falls Lake gains a foot after rain, still 10 feet below last summer

Falls Lake rose to 246.19 feet as of Monday, a full foot higher than the week before thanks to recent rainfall, but the reservoir remains roughly 10 feet below where it stood in July 2025, according to WRAL's weekly drought tracker. The lake — Raleigh's primary drinking-water source for more than 650,000 residents — was on pace for its driest June on record before the rain arrived. Raleigh remains under Stage 1 water restrictions, and city officials have asked council for authority to move to Stage 2 restrictions faster than the lake's automatic 45%-of-supply trigger if conservation efforts continue to lag.

Sunday, July 12, 2026

🚨 Bomb threat clears Kenan Stadium, all clear issued

UNC Police evacuated Kenan Stadium on Thursday afternoon after a 911 call reported a bomb threat, prompting a swarm of officers, K-9 units and a stadium-wide search. The Alert Carolina notice went out at 3:55 p.m. telling people to avoid the area while investigators worked the scene. Hours later, UNC Police confirmed no credible threat was found and lifted the all-clear. It's the second high-profile safety scare in Chapel Hill this summer, and comes as campus police face pressure to respond fast to any threat near university facilities.

🌡️ Falls Lake keeps dropping as Raleigh eyes Stage 2 restrictions

Falls Lake sat at 245.27 feet as of Thursday — roughly six feet below normal and still falling 2-3% a week despite recent rain, according to WRAL's drought tracking. Raleigh City Council has already given the city manager authority to move into Stage 2 restrictions early if compliance doesn't improve, and irrigation-meter violations climbed to 46% in the most recent enforcement sweep. Central NC remains in severe drought, with forecasters estimating the region needs 10 to 15 inches of rain to fully recover — a process expected to take months. Falls Lake automatically triggers Stage 2 if it falls to 45% of supply pool.

🏛️ Raleigh mayor stays on standby as curfew ordinance takes shape

Mayor Janet Cowell said there are no plans for an emergency curfew this weekend, but she's staying in the city in case a threat emerges, citing "no credible information" of anything else planned after the July 4 teen takeovers. That follows City Council's first formal step toward a youth curfew, directing the city attorney and police to draft an ordinance covering those 17 and under, with a council vote expected Aug. 18. Police Chief Rico Boyce has also floated backpack restrictions and expanded youth programming as part of the response to the Glenwood South and Brier Creek violence.

📊 Raleigh's Q2 crime data: violent crime down, traffic deaths way up

Raleigh police's second-quarter numbers show total violent and property crime down 1% year over year, with homicides falling 33% (four versus six) and robberies down 14%. But the report flagged a sharp rise in traffic fatalities — up 67%, from nine deaths to 15 — along with a 5% jump in motor vehicle theft, per ABC11's breakdown. The data lands just as the department faces scrutiny over the Fourth of July disorder that drew an estimated 8,000 people to Glenwood South.

🎓 New NCAA eligibility rules reshape rosters at UNC, Duke, NC State

Division I's new age-based eligibility model — giving athletes up to five years to play if they enroll within a year of turning 19 — is drawing fresh scrutiny as programs plan ahead, with a Chapelboro analysis this week weighing how it could simplify roster building for Triangle schools. The rule eliminates season-of-competition limits and redshirt tracking, running the eligibility clock continuously regardless of transfers or sit-outs. It takes full effect for athletes enrolling in fall 2027, with current UNC, Duke and NC State athletes able to use whichever rule set benefits them most.
