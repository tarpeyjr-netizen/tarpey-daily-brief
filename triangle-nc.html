Wednesday, July 22, 2026

🚨 Man shot outside Raleigh gas station overnight

A man walked into a Speedway on Jones Sausage Road around 3 a.m. Wednesday with gunshot wounds to his head and side, telling police he'd been shot with a shotgun. A store employee called 911 and the man was taken to a hospital, where he is expected to survive. Raleigh police are still searching for a suspect, and the investigation remains active. The shooting is the latest in a string of gun violence incidents that WRAL has tracked across the city this month.

🚨 Durham raid shuts down business tied to homicides, shootings

NC Alcohol Law Enforcement agents and Durham police raided a business at 603 Ellis Road over the weekend and shut it down for selling alcohol without a permit, capping years of violent crime tied to the property. Agents seized three firearms — one held by a convicted felon, another reported stolen — along with opioids and liquor, and charged owner Travis Davon Miller and an employee with illegally selling alcohol. The location has been linked to homicides, shootings, aggravated assaults and weapons violations since 2024, with law enforcement executing multiple warrants there. ABC11 reports the broader weekend operation led to more than 30 arrests and the seizure of fentanyl, methamphetamine and cocaine across the city.

🌡️ Rain eases Triangle drought, but Falls Lake still deeply depleted

Recent storms have brought welcome relief to the Triangle's drought — Spectrum News 1 reports Raleigh-Durham International Airport has logged about 6.75 inches of rain in July, more than double the typical 3 inches. But Falls Lake, the primary drinking water source for Raleigh and six surrounding communities, remains 5.2 feet below full and at just 66% capacity. Raleigh Water Assistant Director Ed Buchan said the reservoir has stabilized and even gained ground after losing 2–3% a week earlier this summer, but cautioned "it's obviously still in a very serious situation." Stage 2 water restrictions would kick in if Falls Lake drops to 45% capacity, a threshold officials are watching closely as the drought lingers across central North Carolina.

🤝 Wake, Durham schools pull salads as cyclospora cases top 500

North Carolina has logged 561 cases of cyclosporiasis since May, according to the state health department, with the majority in Wake County and nearly half reported in just the past week. At least 16 people have been hospitalized statewide, including five in Wake County. As a precaution, Durham Public Schools has pulled all fresh produce from cafeteria menus until September, while Wake County Public Schools has removed salads, parsley and cilantro and is monitoring CDC guidance. The FDA is still tracing the source of the outbreak, which officials say is linked to parsley, cilantro and lettuce — after a test implicating Taylor Farms iceberg lettuce used in Taco Bell meals turned out to be a false positive.

TUESDAY, July 21, 2026

🚨 Woman killed in I-40 hit-and-run near downtown Raleigh

Raleigh police are investigating a fatal hit-and-run after a woman was struck and killed early Sunday on eastbound Interstate 40 near South Saunders Street. Officers found 33-year-old Jena Shively in the roadway around 12:15 a.m.; investigators say she was first struck by an unidentified vehicle whose driver fled, then hit by several more cars traveling the interstate before crews could close the lanes. Eastbound I-40 stayed shut for nearly five hours while investigators worked the scene, according to CBS17. Police have not said what led Shively into the travel lane, and the search for the vehicle that first hit her continues, ABC11 reports.

🌡️ Heat advisory and storm risk squeeze the Triangle

Wake and Durham counties spent the weekend under a heat advisory as heat index values climbed to 106-107 degrees, with forecasters also flagging a risk for severe thunderstorms moving through Sunday afternoon into Monday morning. The extreme heat strained the power grid: more than 1,100 Duke Energy customers near East Geer Street and Midland Terrace in Durham lost power Saturday after equipment failed, with crews working through the afternoon to restore service. Duke Energy listed the cause simply as "equipment going offline" amid the heat wave. The advisory lingered into the start of the week as temperatures stayed stubbornly high across central North Carolina, per First Alert 4.

🤝 Cary hosts USA's rugby win over Spain in Nations Cup debut

The U.S. Eagles beat Spain 29-22 at First Horizon Stadium inside WakeMed Soccer Park in Cary on Saturday night, moving to 3-0 in the inaugural World Rugby Nations Cup after earlier wins over Zimbabwe and Portugal. The new competition brings together the 12 unions already qualified for the 2027 Men's Rugby World Cup in a structured annual test series, with Cary serving as a host site for group-stage matches. The win drew a strong crowd to WakeMed Soccer Park and marked another high-profile international sporting event for the Triangle. The Eagles' unbeaten run through group play adds to Cary's growing reputation as a stop for major rugby and soccer events, according to World Rugby.

🎓 NC State's Isaiah Shirley nominated for national community-service honor

NC State senior defensive tackle Isaiah Shirley was named a nominee for the 2026 Allstate AFCA Good Works Team on Monday, recognizing his community service work alongside his standing as a two-time ACC All-Academic performer. Shirley serves as NC State's representative on the ACC Student-Athlete Advisory Council and sits on the football team's leadership council. He's one of just four student-athletes nationally, and the only football player, still in contention for the Arthur Ashe Jr. Sports Scholars Award, the ACC noted in its own nominee announcement. The full 22-member Good Works Team, plus one honorary head coach, will be announced in September.

SUNDAY, JULY 19, 2026

🏛️ State auditor calls out "extravagant spending" in Cary

North Carolina State Auditor Dave Boliek released a 2,603-page report on the Town of Cary's finances Thursday, seven months after town manager Sean Stegall resigned amid allegations of financial misconduct. Investigators found nearly two-thirds of Cary's 828 employees carried a town-issued procurement card — compared with an average of 16% among North Carolina's other most populous municipalities — and flagged more than $1 million in questionable purchases, including $48 glasses of wine, $1,600 in Ray-Ban sunglasses for the town council, and $35,000 spent producing a documentary of a staff retreat in Wilmington. Boliek said the findings point to "a cultural problem in terms of extravagant spending of taxpayer dollars." Cary held its own news conference Thursday evening to outline changes made since Stegall's departure.

🚨 Overnight shootings in Raleigh and Durham leave two hurt

Raleigh police charged 33-year-old Eddie Thomasson after a shooting on Magnolia Court near Lynn Road early Thursday left a man with life-threatening but stable injuries; he faces charges including discharging a weapon into an occupied dwelling and assault with intent to kill. In Durham, an 11-year-old boy was grazed by a bullet early Friday when gunfire — reportedly around 20 shots — struck multiple apartment buildings at Rochelle Manor in the Joyland community. The boy was treated at the scene and released; no arrests had been made as of Friday morning, and Durham CrimeStoppers is offering a reward for information.

🌡️ Drought-weakened tree topples onto Glenwood Avenue

A large oak tree came down across Glenwood Avenue near Carolina Country Club Friday afternoon, taking out power lines and cutting electricity to more than 600 Duke Energy customers. Crews worked through the night to clear debris and restore power, and the road reopened by 6:30 a.m. Saturday. An arborist told WRAL the culprit likely wasn't wind or rain but weeks of relentless heat and one of the region's worst droughts in years, which has left trees stressed and more prone to failure across the Triangle.

🏗️ Downtown Durham weighs closures against new openings

A wave of recent business closures in downtown Durham has stirred concern, but city leaders say the district is in transition rather than decline. Downtown Durham Inc. CEO Nicole Thompson pointed to inflation, tariffs, and rising costs as pressures squeezing existing businesses, even as roughly ten new ventures — including a classical Pilates studio called Boomerang — prepare to open in the coming months. Regionally, demand for owner-operated businesses across Raleigh, Durham, and Chapel Hill remains strong, with the Triangle ranking among the most active acquisition markets in the Southeast.

Saturday, July 18, 2026

🚨 Glenwood South Defendants Ordered Out of Entertainment District

Two men charged in the July 4 mob violence that overwhelmed Raleigh's Glenwood South appeared in court Thursday, where a judge ordered both to stay out of the entertainment district as a condition of release. David Elijah Lane, 20, and Amir Chance McClain, 22, face felony rioting charges tied to the night roughly 8,000 people swarmed the street and a Raleigh police officer suffered an elbow injury breaking up a fight. Lane was released on a $10,000 bond and McClain on $6,000; both are due back in court July 27. The hearing is the latest fallout from the holiday chaos that has since pushed Mayor Janet Cowell and Police Chief Rico Boyce to back a citywide curfew for minors.

🏛️ Duke Health Edges Out WakeMed in State Health Plan Shake-Up

The N.C. State Health Plan will make Duke Health its preferred access-tier provider in the Triangle starting in 2027, a deal that pushes WakeMed into the plan's costlier nonpreferred tier for its roughly 550,000 members. Duke Health reached out with a steep rate discount that state health plan administrator Tom Friedman said was too good to pass up, even as he called WakeMed "an excellent facility." Members who still seek non-emergency care at WakeMed's three hospitals will face $5,000 deductibles and $12,000 out-of-pocket maximums under the new standard plan, compared with $1,500 and $4,000 at preferred systems like Duke, Novant and UNC Health. WakeMed, which is pursuing a merger with Atrium Health, has not yet responded publicly to the change.

🏗️ Research Triangle Park Opens a New Chapter With RTP 3.0

The Research Triangle Foundation this week marked the formal adoption of RTP 3.0, opening the door for property owners to pursue mixed-use development and residential neighborhoods alongside the park's traditional research campuses for the first time in its history. The framework caps nearly five years of collaboration among companies, universities and local governments, and companies can now formally apply under the new rules. RTP spans 7,000 acres and supports more than 55,000 jobs across 385-plus companies, and foundation president Scott Levitan says the shift toward more dining, retail and housing reflects the growth the park needs to stay competitive.

🤝 Downtown Durham Business Owners Push Back on Decline Narrative

A string of recent closures has stirred worry about downtown Durham's health, but Downtown Durham Inc. CEO Nicole Thompson says the district is in transition, not trouble. Four businesses recently announced plans to close, citing inflation, tariffs and rising costs, while roughly ten new restaurants, bars and retail shops are set to open in the coming months. Among them is Boomerang, a classical Pilates studio holding its grand opening Saturday, and longtime tenant Exotique Boutique and Gallery, whose owners say adapting to customer feedback has kept them going for two decades.

🏛️ Chapel Hill Unveils First LUMO Rewrite Since 2003

Chapel Hill's long-awaited rewrite of its Land Use Management Ordinance officially went live this week, Mayor Jess Anderson told 97.9 The Hill in a Thursday interview. The draft is the ordinance's first overhaul since 2003 and is meant to better align the town's rules on transportation, affordable housing, climate resilience and transit-oriented development with its Complete Community goals. Anderson previewed a round of community outreach events and a timeline for public feedback before the council takes up formal adoption.

🏒 Hurricanes to Raise Second Stanley Cup Banner Against Panthers

The Carolina Hurricanes will kick off the 2026-27 season at Lenovo Center on September 29 by raising their Stanley Cup championship banner before hosting the Florida Panthers. The matchup was announced this week and doubles as a rematch of sorts against a Panthers team that has given Carolina trouble in past playoff runs. The Hurricanes beat the Vegas Golden Knights in six games in June to capture their first Stanley Cup since 2006, and NHL insider Elliotte Friedman called the Panthers pairing "the right choice for the banner raising," with both teams at "peak confidence and power."
