A Sister to Scheherazade

AuthorAssia Djebar
StylePostcolonial Feminist Fiction
Year1987
Pages176
Plot Synopsis

Isma and Hajila are both wives of the same man in postcolonial Algeria — not rivals, but bound together in a strange sisterhood. Narrated in two voices, Isma recounts her own story in the first person while telling Hajila's in the second, weaving between memory, exile, and the cloistered spaces women inhabit. When Hajila begins slipping beyond the veil — literally and metaphorically — it is Isma who quietly hands her the key to freedom. Djebar's prose is lyrical and fractured, as intimate as a whisper and as charged as an act of quiet revolution.

Why You Should Read It

This is a compact, devastating novel — under 200 pages — that earns its weight through strangeness and beauty rather than plot. If you've read anything in the Latin American or African literary tradition, Djebar will feel both familiar and surprising: she has García Márquez's musicality, but her subject is more immediate — Muslim women in postcolonial Algeria navigating the body, language, and the idea of freedom. It's the kind of book that sits with you the way Camus does at his best, not because of what happens but because of a feeling it leaves behind. For a reader drawn to international literary fiction with moral weight, this is exactly the kind of discovery that makes the canon feel alive.
