An American Tragedy

AuthorTheodore Dreiser
StyleAmerican Naturalism
Year1925
Pages~870
Plot Synopsis

Clyde Griffiths claws his way out of a threadbare, sermon-heavy childhood toward the glittering promise of American success, landing a factory job with his wealthy uncle in upstate New York. He falls into an affair with a poor coworker, Roberta, even as he's drawn toward the wealthy, socially connected Sondra Finchley, who represents everything he's been denied. When Roberta turns up pregnant and threatens to expose him just as Sondra's world opens up, Clyde takes her out on a lake with a plan half-formed in his mind — and what happens next becomes a courtroom spectacle that consumes the rest of the novel. It's less a whodunit than a slow, suffocating study of how ambition, class, and self-deception can curdle into catastrophe.

Why You Should Read It

This is the book The Great Gatsby gets compared to when people want to talk about the dark side of the American Dream with real teeth — Dreiser has none of Fitzgerald's lyricism, but he'll bury you in accumulated, damning detail instead. It's long and its prose is famously unpolished, but that bluntness is the point: Dreiser wants you to feel the machinery of class and desire grinding Clyde down rather than admire a well-turned sentence. If you liked how Native Son or An American Tragedy's spiritual descendants use a crime to indict an entire social order, this is the ur-text. Go in patient — the courtroom section alone runs a couple hundred pages — and you'll come out with a portrait of ambition curdling into disaster that's stuck with readers for a century.
