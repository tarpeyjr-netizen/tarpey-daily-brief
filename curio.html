Science & Technology

Physics

The Casimir effect — first predicted by Dutch physicist Hendrik Casimir in 1948 — describes a tiny attractive force between two uncharged metal plates placed extremely close together in a vacuum. It arises from quantum fluctuations in the electromagnetic field and was not experimentally confirmed until 1997, nearly fifty years after its prediction.

Chemistry

Gallium is one of the few metals that melts in the palm of your hand, with a melting point of just 29.76°C (85.6°F). Despite this, it has a boiling point of 2,400°C — giving it one of the widest liquid ranges of any element, a property that makes it valuable in high-temperature thermometers.

Astronomy

The Voyager 1 spacecraft, launched in 1977, is the most distant human-made object in existence. It crossed into interstellar space in 2012 and continues to transmit data back to Earth, with signals now taking more than 22 hours to travel the approximately 24 billion kilometers between the probe and NASA's receivers.

Technology

The first computer mouse was invented by Douglas Engelbart and demonstrated publicly on December 9, 1968 — an event now called "The Mother of All Demos." The device was a wooden block with two metal wheels; Engelbart's team also demonstrated hypertext linking, video conferencing, and collaborative real-time editing at the same presentation.

Engineering

The Panama Canal's Miraflores Locks use over 26 million gallons of fresh water for every ship that passes through — water that drains entirely by gravity from Gatún Lake without a single pump. When the expanded locks opened in 2016, engineers incorporated water-saving basins that recycle roughly 60% of that water per transit.

Aerospace

The SR-71 Blackbird, operated by the U.S. Air Force from 1966 to 1999, flew so fast at cruising altitude that its titanium skin would heat to over 300°C from air friction alone. The aircraft was designed with gaps between its panels when cold; only at full operating temperature did the metal expand enough to seal the airframe completely — meaning it routinely leaked fuel on the ground.

History & Geography

American History

The Northwest Ordinance of 1787, passed by the Congress of the Confederation, established the framework for admitting new states to the union from the territory north of the Ohio River. Crucially, it banned slavery in those territories — making it one of the first federal acts to limit the institution's expansion and a direct precursor to debates that would define the next century.

World History

The Byzantine Empire, commonly thought to have fallen in 1453 with the Ottoman conquest of Constantinople, had actually been shrinking for centuries before its end. By 1450, the empire's effective territory consisted of little more than Constantinople itself and a handful of outposts — ruling barely 50,000 people in a city that had once held 500,000.

Geography

Lake Baikal in Siberia contains approximately 23% of the world's unfrozen surface fresh water — more than all five North American Great Lakes combined. It is also the world's deepest lake at 1,642 meters and is estimated to be between 25 and 30 million years old, making it the oldest lake on Earth.

Japan

Japan's tradition of nemawashi — literally "going around the roots" — describes the practice of building consensus before a formal decision is made by quietly consulting all stakeholders in advance. Rooted in the agricultural technique of carefully preparing a tree's root system before transplanting it, the concept is considered central to Japanese business and organizational culture.

Ireland

The Irish language, known as Gaeilge, is the first official language of the Republic of Ireland under its 1937 constitution, though English is far more widely spoken in daily life. The Gaeltacht regions — primarily along the western seaboard — are government-designated areas where Irish remains the community language, and they receive special state support to preserve the tongue.

India

The city of Varanasi on the Ganges River is widely regarded as one of the oldest continuously inhabited cities in the world, with human settlement dating back at least 3,000 years. It holds profound significance in Hinduism, Buddhism, and Jainism — the Buddha is said to have delivered his first sermon nearby at Sarnath after attaining enlightenment.

South America

The Amazon River discharges approximately 20% of all the fresh water that flows into the world's oceans. Its daily output is so vast that Christopher Columbus, sailing in the Atlantic hundreds of miles offshore, detected fresh water in the sea — and correctly deduced he was near a continent of extraordinary size.

Durham NC

Durham was known as the "City of Medicine" long before the Research Triangle's modern reputation took hold. By the 1970s, the Durham-Chapel Hill corridor had more PhDs per capita than almost any metropolitan area in the United States, a concentration driven by Duke University, UNC-Chapel Hill, and the early biomedical research firms that clustered around them.

Business & Society

Economics

Economist Gary Becker was awarded the 1992 Nobel Prize for extending economic analysis to human behavior beyond traditional markets — including crime, discrimination, marriage, and education. His "human capital" theory argued that investments in education and training should be analyzed with the same rigor as investments in physical capital, a framework now foundational to labor economics.

Finance

The Dutch tulip mania of 1636–37 is often cited as the first recorded speculative bubble. At its peak, a single tulip bulb of the prized "Semper Augustus" variety reportedly sold for the equivalent of a canal house in Amsterdam. The crash was swift: within weeks of the peak, prices collapsed by more than 99%, leaving many speculators ruined.

Personal Finance

The "latte factor," popularized by financial author David Bach, refers to the cumulative cost of small daily discretionary purchases. While the concept is often debated by economists, the underlying principle — that small, automatic savings invested consistently over decades compound dramatically — is mathematically sound regardless of whether coffee is the vehicle.

Management

Peter Drucker coined the phrase "management by objectives" (MBO) in his 1954 book The Practice of Management, describing a system where managers and employees agree on specific, measurable goals together. The framework influenced virtually every subsequent goal-setting methodology, from Andy Grove's OKRs at Intel to the modern performance review processes used across corporate America.

Constitutional Law

In Gideon v. Wainwright (1963), the Supreme Court unanimously held that the Sixth Amendment's guarantee of counsel applied to state criminal proceedings, not just federal ones. The ruling meant that any defendant facing serious criminal charges who could not afford an attorney had the constitutional right to have one appointed — fundamentally reshaping how criminal justice operates at every level of government.

Coaching

Research in youth athletics consistently shows that early sport specialization — focusing exclusively on one sport before age 12 — is associated with higher rates of overuse injury and burnout compared to multi-sport participation. Studies from the American Academy of Pediatrics suggest that children who play multiple sports through adolescence are more likely to remain active athletes into adulthood.

Sports

Track & Field

The first sub-four-minute mile was run by Roger Bannister on May 6, 1954, in Oxford, England, with a time of 3:59.4. What is less remembered is that his record lasted only 46 days: Australian John Landy broke it on June 21, 1954, running 3:57.9 — proving that Bannister's feat had shattered not just a record but a psychological barrier that had held back an entire generation of milers.

Softball

Jennie Finch, widely regarded as the greatest softball pitcher of her era, went undefeated in international competition over a stretch that included the 2004 Athens Olympics gold medal. Her dominance extended to a famous exhibition series against Major League Baseball hitters — including Albert Pujols and Barry Bonds — whom she struck out or retired with regularity, bringing mainstream attention to the sport.

Mayo GAA

Mayo's senior football team has reached the All-Ireland final more times without winning than any other county in Gaelic football history, accumulating a string of final appearances since their last title in 1951. Despite this, the county's supporter base is regarded as among the most passionate and loyal in the GAA, with green-and-red crowds routinely filling Croke Park regardless of how many heartbreaks have come before.

Religion & Philosophy

Catholicism

The concept of subsidiarity — central to Catholic social teaching — holds that decisions should be made at the lowest competent level of authority, with higher institutions intervening only when local bodies genuinely cannot act effectively. First articulated formally in Pope Pius XI's 1931 encyclical Quadragesimo Anno, the principle has influenced not only Church governance but also the constitutional design of the European Union.

World Religion

The Sikh scripture, the Guru Granth Sahib, is treated as a living Guru — not merely a holy book — in Sikhism. It is given its own throne, clothed and fanned in gurdwaras, and put to "rest" each night in a ceremony called Sukhasan. Compiled in 1604 by the fifth Sikh Guru, Arjan Dev Ji, it contains hymns from multiple faith traditions including Islam and Hinduism, reflecting Sikhism's foundational belief in the unity of God across religions.

Science & Technology

Physics

When Einstein explained the photoelectric effect in 1905 — showing that light travels in discrete energy packets called photons — it was this paper, not his theory of relativity, that earned him the Nobel Prize in 1921. The discovery is the principle behind every modern solar cell and digital camera sensor.

Chemistry

Fluorine is so reactive it can ignite substances normally considered fireproof, including glass and water, under the right conditions. Chemists spent nearly 74 years attempting to isolate it — and several died in the process — before Henri Moissan finally succeeded in 1886 using electrolysis at extremely low temperatures.

Astronomy

Neutron stars are so dense that a teaspoon of their material would weigh roughly 10 million tons on Earth. Some spin up to 700 times per second; when their radiation beams sweep past Earth with clockwork regularity, we observe them as pulsars — some precise enough to rival atomic clocks.

Technology

The first computer mouse was invented by Douglas Engelbart in 1964 and was made of wood, with a single button and two metal wheels. It debuted publicly at what became known as "The Mother of All Demos" in 1968, the same presentation that also introduced video conferencing, collaborative editing, and hypertext links.

Engineering

The Panama Canal's lock system raises and lowers ships 85 feet above sea level to cross the Continental Divide. Each lockage uses roughly 26 million gallons of fresh water — drawn from Gatun Lake — which flows entirely by gravity with no pumps required.

Aerospace

Voyager 1, launched in 1977, is now over 23 billion kilometers from Earth — the most distant human-made object ever. It is still operational, but signals traveling at the speed of light now take more than 22 hours to make the one-way trip between the spacecraft and NASA's Deep Space Network.

History & Geography

American History

During the Lewis and Clark Expedition (1804–1806), the Corps of Discovery traveled over 8,000 miles round trip through largely uncharted terrain. Only one man died — likely from appendicitis — and the mission returned with detailed records of 178 previously undocumented plant species and 122 animal species.

World History

The Library of Alexandria was not destroyed in a single catastrophic fire. It declined gradually over centuries through neglect and reduced funding; the famous fire attributed to Julius Caesar in 48 BC destroyed only warehouses on the waterfront, not the library itself. The institution faded across several hundred years of political and economic instability.

Geography

The Amazon River discharges approximately 20% of all the fresh water that flows into the world's oceans — more than the next seven largest rivers combined. At its mouth near Marajó Island, the river stretches over 48 kilometers wide, far beyond the horizon from either bank.

Japan

The Japanese craft of kintsugi — literally "golden joinery" — repairs broken pottery with lacquer mixed with gold, silver, or platinum. Rather than hiding the damage, the technique honors a piece's history and embodies the philosophical ideal of wabi-sabi: finding beauty in imperfection and transience.

Ireland

The Cliffs of Moher on Ireland's Atlantic coast rise up to 214 meters (702 feet) and stretch for 14 kilometers along County Clare. They were formed approximately 320 million years ago from compacted layers of shale, siltstone, and sandstone — making them far older than the dinosaurs.

India

Varanasi, on the banks of the Ganges River, is considered one of the world's oldest continuously inhabited cities, with settlements dating back more than 3,000 years. Mark Twain, who visited in 1896, called it "older than history, older than tradition, older even than legend."

South America

The Atacama Desert in Chile and Peru is the driest non-polar desert on Earth — some weather stations there have never recorded rainfall. Despite this, the desert blooms spectacularly every several years when El Niño brings just enough moisture to trigger the germination of dormant seeds in an event called the "desierto florido."

Durham NC

Duke University's iconic Gothic West Campus was designed by Julian Abele, one of America's first prominent Black architects. Abele completed the designs in the 1920s and 1930s while working for the firm of Horace Trumbauer in Philadelphia — but due to segregation laws, he was not permitted to visit the campus he created during his lifetime.

Business & Society

Economics

Gary Becker pioneered the application of economic logic to human behavior outside traditional markets — including crime, discrimination, and family decisions. His concept of "human capital," the idea that education and skills are investments yielding measurable returns, transformed how governments and economists evaluate spending on schooling and workforce development. Becker received the Nobel Prize in Economics in 1992.

Finance

The Dutch East India Company (VOC), founded in 1602, is widely regarded as the world's first publicly traded corporation. It issued shares to the general public, established the Amsterdam Stock Exchange, and at its peak was worth an estimated $8 trillion in today's dollars — larger than Apple, Microsoft, and Saudi Aramco combined.

Personal Finance

The "pay yourself first" principle — automatically directing a fixed percentage of income to savings before spending anything else — is backed by decades of behavioral economics research. Because the money is never "seen" in a checking account, individuals consistently save more than those who intend to save what's left over at month's end.

Management

Peter Drucker introduced "management by objectives" (MBO) in his 1954 book The Practice of Management, arguing that managers and employees should jointly define specific, measurable goals. The approach directly influenced modern performance review systems and was later adapted into OKRs (Objectives and Key Results) now used widely across the technology industry.

Constitutional Law

In McCulloch v. Maryland (1819), Chief Justice John Marshall established the doctrine of implied powers, ruling that Congress could charter a national bank even though the Constitution contained no explicit authorization. The ruling also established federal supremacy over conflicting state laws — one of the most consequential decisions in the Court's history, still cited in constitutional arguments today.

Coaching

Research by the Positive Coaching Alliance, founded at Stanford University in 1998, found that athletes coached with encouragement and effort-based feedback show significantly higher intrinsic motivation and are far more likely to continue playing sports into adulthood than those coached in win-at-all-costs environments. The effect is especially pronounced in girls' youth athletics.

Sports

Track & Field

Bob Beamon's long jump of 8.90 meters (29 ft 2½ in) at the 1968 Mexico City Olympics shattered the world record by 55 centimeters — the largest margin in the event's history. The jump was so far beyond the existing record that the optical measuring equipment couldn't read it; officials had to retrieve a steel tape measure to confirm the distance. The record stood for 23 years.

Softball

Jennie Finch of the University of Arizona went 32–0 during the 2001 NCAA season, posting a 0.54 ERA and striking out 11.2 batters per seven innings. Her combination of size, speed (pitching roughly 65–68 mph from 43 feet, equivalent to a 95-mph fastball from 60 feet in baseball), and deceptive rise ball made her one of the most dominant college pitchers of any era.

Mayo GAA

Mayo's record in Gaelic football All-Ireland Senior Championship finals is among the most remarkable in the sport — the county has appeared in more finals without winning the Sam Maguire Cup than any other county. Their last championship victory came in 1951, yet Mayo has continued reaching the final repeatedly, making their near-misses a defining chapter in GAA lore.

Religion & Philosophy

Catholicism

Michelangelo painted the Sistine Chapel ceiling — over 5,000 square feet containing more than 300 figures — between 1508 and 1512. Contrary to the popular image, he did not lie on his back; he stood on specially designed curved scaffolding and craned his neck upward. He complained in a letter to a friend that the work left him with a goiter and permanently damaged eyesight.

World Religion

Sikhism's Golden Temple (Harmandir Sahib) in Amritsar, India, has four entrances — one on each side — symbolizing that people of all castes and from all directions are equally welcome. The temple's community kitchen (langar) serves free vegetarian meals to over 100,000 people every single day, regardless of religion, caste, or socioeconomic status.

India

At its zenith, Chola rulers commissioned the Brihadeeswarar Temple at Thanjavur, whose 216-foot granite tower was the tallest structure in the world when completed around 1010 CE — built without mortar, with a 25-ton capstone hoisted to the summit using a miles-long earthen ramp.

Business & Society

Economics

Joseph Stiglitz, co-winner of the 2001 Nobel Memorial Prize in Economics, demonstrated that markets plagued by information asymmetry — where one party knows significantly more than another — cannot achieve efficient outcomes on their own. His work showed that this problem is pervasive: in insurance markets, employers' hiring decisions, and credit markets, the party with less information is systematically disadvantaged, often causing markets to collapse or ration goods in ways standard competitive models cannot predict.

El Guerrouj also holds the 1,500-meter world record (3:26.00), and at the 2004 Athens Olympics he became the first man since Peter Snell in 1964 to win both the 1,500m and 5,000m gold medals at the same Games.

Religion & Philosophy

Catholicism

World Religion

In Shinto, Japan's indigenous religion, kami are not simply deities but sacred presences capable of inhabiting natural objects, ancestors, forces of nature, and even abstract concepts. Japanese tradition holds there are eight million kami — a figure meaning "innumerable" — reflecting the faith's core conviction that the sacred permeates every dimension of existence, from mountain peaks and ancient trees to the forge of a master swordsmith.

Science & Technology

Physics

The Casimir effect, predicted by Dutch physicist Hendrik Casimir in 1948, demonstrates that two uncharged metal plates placed nanometers apart in a vacuum will attract each other due to quantum fluctuations in the electromagnetic field between them. It was one of the first experimental confirmations that the vacuum of space is not truly empty.

Astronomy

Magnetars are a type of neutron star with magnetic fields so powerful — roughly a quadrillion times stronger than Earth’s — that they can distort atomic orbitals and slow the speed of light passing through their vicinity. A magnetar located 10 astronomical units from Earth would erase the data on every credit card on the planet.

Engineering

The Falkirk Wheel in Scotland, opened in 2002, is the world’s only rotating boat lift. It connects two canals at different elevations by rotating a pair of gondolas filled with boats, using less energy than boiling eight kettles of water per full rotation — because the weight of the water displaced by the boats always balances perfectly.

History & Geography

World History

The Library of Ashurbanipal, assembled by the Assyrian king in the 7th century BC at Nineveh, is considered the world’s first systematically organized library. It held over 30,000 clay tablets covering astronomy, medicine, literature, and law — including the oldest surviving version of the Epic of Gilgamesh.

Japan

The Japanese concept of monozukuri (literally “the making of things”) is a philosophy that treats manufacturing as an art form requiring mastery, precision, and continuous improvement. It underpins the production culture at companies like Toyota, where factory workers are expected to stop the entire assembly line if they spot a defect.

Ireland

The Hill of Tara in County Meath served as the ceremonial seat of the High Kings of Ireland for over 4,000 years. At its center is the Stone of Destiny (Lia Fáil), which according to legend would cry out when the rightful king of Ireland stood upon it — it fell silent, tradition holds, at the death of Cú Chulainn.

Durham NC

Durham’s Hayti neighborhood, centered on Pettigrew Street, was once one of the wealthiest Black communities in the American South. By the early 20th century it boasted a thriving corridor of Black-owned businesses, banks, and professional offices — earning it comparisons to Tulsa’s Greenwood District — before urban renewal projects in the 1960s and 70s demolished much of it to build the Durham Freeway.

Business & Society

Economics

Joseph Schumpeter’s concept of “creative destruction” holds that capitalism advances not through gradual improvement but through periodic revolutionary upheaval, where new industries annihilate old ones. He argued in Capitalism, Socialism and Democracy (1942) that this process — not price competition — is the essential fact about capitalism, and that it ultimately makes the system both dynamic and destabilizing.

Constitutional Law

In Mapp v. Ohio (1961), the Supreme Court ruled 6–3 that evidence obtained through unconstitutional searches and seizures could not be used in state criminal trials, extending the federal “exclusionary rule” to all U.S. courts. The case arose when Cleveland police broke into Dollree Mapp’s home without a warrant, and it fundamentally reshaped the relationship between law enforcement and the Fourth Amendment.

Coaching

Research on youth athletics consistently shows that early specialization — focusing on a single sport before age 12 — increases injury rates and burnout while offering no long-term performance advantage over athletes who played multiple sports. The most decorated Olympic athletes, on average, didn’t specialize in their primary sport until around age 15.

Track & Field

What is less remembered is that his record stood for just 46 days before Australian John Landy broke it — and that Bannister himself was a full-time medical student who trained for barely 30 minutes a day.

Softball

The ball arrived at the plate in roughly the same reaction time as a 95 mph overhand pitch due to the shorter distance and unfamiliar release angle.

Mayo GAA

Mayo’s record of appearing in 12 All-Ireland Senior Football Championship finals since 1950 without winning is unmatched in Gaelic football history. The county’s last All-Ireland title came in 1951, making their 70-year pursuit one of the most storied and agonizing in Irish sport — a wait sustained by some of the most passionate supporters in the country.

Religion & Philosophy

Catholicism

The concept of sensus fidelium (“sense of the faithful”) holds that the entire body of baptized Catholics, guided by the Holy Spirit, cannot collectively err in matters of faith and morals. First formally articulated at Vatican I and elaborated at Vatican II, it serves as a theological check on top-down doctrine — suggesting that widespread dissent among the faithful is itself a signal worth theological attention.

World Religion

In Sikhism, the concept of seva (selfless service) is considered one of the three pillars of the faith alongside prayer and honest labor. Every Sikh gurdwara operates a langar — a community kitchen that serves free meals to all visitors regardless of religion, caste, or status. The Golden Temple in Amritsar feeds an estimated 100,000 people daily, making it one of the largest free kitchens on earth.

Science & Technology

Physics

The Casimir effect — a tiny attractive force between two uncharged metal plates placed extremely close together in a vacuum — was predicted by Dutch physicist Hendrik Casimir in 1948 and arises from quantum fluctuations in the electromagnetic field. It was the first measurable laboratory demonstration that the quantum vacuum is not truly empty.

Chemistry

Gallium is a soft, silvery metal with one of the widest liquid ranges of any element: it melts just above room temperature (29.76 °C) yet doesn't boil until 2,400 °C. This unusual property makes it valuable in high-temperature thermometers and semiconductor applications.

Astronomy

Titan, Saturn's largest moon, is the only moon in the solar system known to have a dense atmosphere and stable liquid on its surface — but the lakes and rivers are filled with liquid methane and ethane, not water. Cassini's radar data revealed drainage channels carved into icy bedrock much like river systems on Earth.

Technology

The first transistor radio, the Regency TR-1, was released in October 1954 and ran on a 22.5-volt battery the size of a deck of cards. It sold for $49.95 — about $560 in today's money — and popularised the idea of personal, portable electronics a generation before the Walkman.

Engineering

The Millau Viaduct in southern France, completed in 2004, reaches a maximum height of 343 metres to the top of its tallest mast — taller than the Eiffel Tower — making it the world's tallest cable-stayed bridge. Its deck was slid horizontally into place on temporary supports over eight months.

Aerospace

The Saturn V rocket's five F-1 engines burned a combined 13,000 kg of kerosene and liquid oxygen per second at liftoff, generating 34.5 million newtons of thrust. The combustion instability problem that nearly killed the program was solved in part by deliberately introducing small acoustic baffles inside the injector plate.

History & Geography

American History

The Homestead Act of 1862 granted 160 acres of federal land to any adult citizen willing to improve it over five years. World History

The Hanseatic League, a medieval commercial confederation of Northern European trading cities, functioned for nearly four centuries (c. 1241–1669) without a permanent army, navy, or shared currency. At its peak it encompassed nearly 200 towns stretching from London to Novgorod, enforcing trade agreements through collective embargoes rather than military force.

Geography

Lake Baikal in Siberia holds approximately 23,615 cubic kilometres of fresh water — about 20 percent of the world's unfrozen surface freshwater — in a rift valley that is still widening by roughly two centimetres each year. Japan

The Meiji government's 1873 land tax reform replaced payment in rice with a fixed cash tax based on assessed land value, forcing farmers to grow cash crops and sell produce at market. This single policy shift accelerated Japan's agrarian-to-industrial transition faster than almost any comparable reform in 19th-century Asia.

Ireland

The Irish language's traditional script, Gaelic type (Cló Gaelach), was used in printed texts from the 16th century until the mid-20th century. The Irish government standardised spelling and adopted the Roman alphabet for official Irish in 1948, partly to make the language easier to learn in schools.

India

The Konkan Railway, running 740 km along India's western coast between Mumbai and Mangaluru, required the construction of 92 tunnels and 2,000 bridges through the Western Ghats — an average of nearly one major structure every 300 metres. Completed in 1998, it cut the rail journey between the two cities by over 12 hours.

South America

The Atacama Desert in northern Chile receives less than 1 mm of rain per year in some areas, making it the driest non-polar desert on Earth. Despite this, the Atacama hosts unique flora that survives almost entirely on coastal fog, and its ultra-dry conditions have preserved pre-Columbian mummies for over 7,000 years.

Durham, NC

Durham's Hayti neighborhood was one of the most prosperous African American communities in the early 20th-century South, home to insurance companies, pharmacies, and the North Carolina Mutual Life Insurance Company — once called "the largest Negro business in America." Gary Becker extended economic analysis to non-market behaviour, arguing in his 1964 work Human Capital that education and training are investments people make in themselves with expected future returns — a framework that reshaped how governments measure the value of schooling and workforce development programs.

Finance

The "latte factor," popularised by financial author David Bach, illustrates how small daily expenditures compound dramatically over time — $5 per day invested at a 7% annual return grows to roughly $95,000 over 30 years. Peter Drucker coined the phrase "management by objectives" (MBO) in his 1954 book The Practice of Management, arguing that organisations perform best when managers and employees jointly set specific, measurable goals rather than relying on top-down directives. Constitutional Law

In Gideon v. Wainwright (1963), the Supreme Court unanimously ruled that the Sixth Amendment's guarantee of counsel applies to state criminal trials through the Fourteenth Amendment. The decision required states to provide attorneys to defendants who cannot afford one, fundamentally reshaping the American criminal justice system.

Coaching

Research on youth sports consistently shows that coaches who emphasise effort, improvement, and enjoyment — a "mastery motivational climate" — produce athletes with higher long-term sport participation rates and lower dropout rates than coaches who focus primarily on winning. The effect is strongest among children aged 8–12 during the critical window for sport socialisation.

The Rosary as a structured devotional prayer was systematized in the 15th century, with the Dominican Order playing a central role in its popularisation. In Sikhism, the concept of seva (selfless service) is one of the faith's foundational practices. Every Sikh gurdwara maintains a langar — a community kitchen that serves free meals to all visitors regardless of religion, caste, or background — an institution that dates to the time of Guru Nanak in the late 15th century and collectively feeds millions of people daily worldwide.

Science & Technology

Physics

The Casimir effect — first predicted by Dutch physicist Hendrik Casimir in 1948 — demonstrates that even a perfect vacuum is not truly empty. Two uncharged metal plates placed nanometers apart in a vacuum experience a measurable attractive force caused by quantum fluctuations in the electromagnetic field between them. The effect is so real it must now be accounted for in the design of microelectromechanical systems.

Chemistry

Phosphorus was discovered in 1669 by German alchemist Hennig Brand, who evaporated enormous quantities of urine in the hope of isolating gold. Instead, the residue glowed in the dark and burst into flame on contact with air. He had inadvertently isolated the first element discovered in the modern era, and its name — from the Greek for "light-bearer" — reflects that eerie first impression.

Astronomy

Saturn's rings are astonishingly thin relative to their breadth: they span roughly 282,000 kilometers across yet average only about 10 meters thick in most places. They are composed primarily of water ice particles ranging from specks to boulders. Cassini mission data suggests the rings may be geologically young — perhaps only 10 to 100 million years old — meaning dinosaurs roamed Earth before Saturn had its iconic rings.

Technology

The first commercially successful touchscreen phone was not the iPhone. Devices with touchscreens existed for years, but Apple's 2007 introduction of capacitive multi-touch — a screen that could sense multiple fingers simultaneously and required no stylus — was the genuine revolution. The enabling breakthrough was integrating the touch sensor directly into the display glass, eliminating the thick overlay that had made earlier touchscreens sluggish and imprecise.

Engineering

The Millau Viaduct in southern France, completed in 2004, is the tallest bridge in the world: one of its pylons reaches 343 meters — taller than the Eiffel Tower. The deck was assembled on both sides of the Tarn River valley and then "launched" outward on rails, inching forward over months until the two halves met in the middle. The bridge eliminated a notorious traffic bottleneck that had turned the A75 highway into a summer standstill for decades.

Aerospace

The Voyager 1 spacecraft, launched in September 1977, crossed into interstellar space in 2012 — becoming the first human-made object to leave the heliosphere. It now travels more than 23 billion kilometers from the Sun and still sends data back to Earth. The radio signal, traveling at the speed of light, takes over 22 hours to reach NASA's receivers, yet scientists can still command the probe and receive its replies.

History & Geography

American History

The Homestead Act of 1862 offered 160 acres of public land to any adult citizen willing to farm it for five years. Over the following decades it transferred roughly 270 million acres — about 10% of the total land area of the United States — to private ownership. The Act reshaped the Great Plains, drew waves of immigrants from Europe, and was the most sweeping land redistribution in American history.

World History

The Byzantine Empire, the eastern successor of Rome, outlasted its western counterpart by nearly a thousand years, finally falling when Ottoman Sultan Mehmed II captured Constantinople on May 29, 1453. The date is still observed as a day of mourning in Greece. The fall scattered Greek scholars westward into Italy, carrying classical manuscripts that helped ignite the Renaissance — making the empire's collapse a catalyst for Western civilization's next great flourishing.

Geography

The Caspian Sea is the world's largest landlocked body of water, covering about 371,000 square kilometers — roughly the size of Japan. Despite its name, it is technically a lake, cut off from the world's oceans for millions of years. It holds about 40% of the world's total lake water volume, produces roughly 90% of the world's wild sturgeon caviar, and is bordered by five countries whose competing claims over its resources took decades of negotiation to resolve.

Japan

The concept of mono no aware — roughly, "the pathos of things" — is central to Japanese aesthetics. Coined by 18th-century scholar Motoori Norinaga, it describes the bittersweet awareness of impermanence that makes beautiful things more poignant. It is the feeling evoked by cherry blossoms falling, or a season passing. The concept informs classical literature, film, and garden design and is considered one of the defining lenses through which Japanese culture interprets the world.

Ireland

The Brehon Laws were the ancient legal system of Gaelic Ireland, administered by a hereditary class of jurists called brehons. Remarkably progressive for their era, they recognized women's property rights, outlined procedures for divorce, and set detailed schedules for compensating injuries. In use for more than a thousand years, they were systematically suppressed by English colonial administration and finally abolished in the early 17th century following the Plantation of Ulster.

India

The game of chess originated in India, likely in the Gupta Empire around the 6th century CE, where it was called chaturanga — Sanskrit for "four divisions," referring to the army branches of infantry, cavalry, elephants, and chariots. It spread westward through Persia, where it became chatrang, then into the Arab world, and finally into medieval Europe, where the pieces and rules evolved into the modern game. The rook's resemblance to a war chariot is a relic of that original Indian design.

South America

The city of Potosí in present-day Bolivia, founded after the discovery of a vast silver mountain in 1545, was at its peak in the early 17th century one of the largest cities in the world — rivaling London and Paris in population. The silver extracted from Cerro Rico funded the Spanish Empire for over two centuries and circulated globally, triggering inflation across Europe and reshaping the economies of China, which absorbed an enormous share of the silver in exchange for goods. Historians estimate that roughly 8 million people, mostly indigenous laborers and enslaved Africans, died working the mines.

Durham, NC

Durham's Parrish Street, known as "Black Wall Street" long before the more famous Tulsa district claimed the title, was home to Mechanics and Farmers Bank, founded in 1908. It is one of the oldest continually operating African American-owned banks in the United States and survived the Great Depression, urban renewal, and multiple recessions. Today headquartered on the same street, it continues to provide community lending in Durham over a century after its founding.

Business & Society

Economics

Milton Friedman's 1968 presidential address to the American Economic Association introduced the concept of the "natural rate of unemployment" — the idea that there is a floor below which unemployment cannot be pushed without triggering accelerating inflation. He predicted that the Phillips Curve trade-off between unemployment and inflation was a short-run illusion, and that policies exploiting it would eventually produce stagflation. The stagflation of the 1970s appeared to vindicate him and fundamentally reoriented macroeconomic policymaking worldwide.

Finance

Charles Ponzi did not invent the scheme that bears his name, but his 1919–1920 operation in Boston became its archetype. He promised investors 50% returns in 45 days by arbitraging international postal reply coupons, which he claimed could be bought cheaply abroad and redeemed at face value in the U.S. He raised $15 million from 30,000 investors before it collapsed. The actual coupon arbitrage was never feasible at the scale required — the entire postal system of Europe could not have supplied enough coupons to cover even a fraction of his promises.

Personal Finance

The "latte factor" — popularized by David Bach in the early 2000s — argues that small daily discretionary purchases, if redirected into investments over decades, compound into meaningful wealth. Critics rightly note it oversimplifies structural financial inequality. Yet the core mathematical point stands: a consistent $5-a-day saved starting at age 25, invested at 7% annually, grows to roughly $250,000 by retirement — not a fortune, but a demonstration of how behavioral habits interact with compound interest over long timelines.

Management

Peter Drucker coined the term "management by objectives" (MBO) in his 1954 book The Practice of Management, arguing that organizations should define clear, measurable goals at every level and align individual performance to them. The concept was the intellectual ancestor of today's OKRs (Objectives and Key Results), adopted by Intel, Google, and thousands of others. Drucker also coined the phrase "culture eats strategy for breakfast" — a warning that the best-designed plans fail without matching organizational behavior.

Constitutional Law

New York Times Co. v. Sullivan (1964) transformed American defamation law by holding that public officials cannot sue for libel unless they prove "actual malice" — that the defendant knew the statement was false or acted with reckless disregard for the truth. The case arose from a civil rights-era advertisement and was decided unanimously by the Warren Court. It effectively ended Southern officials' use of defamation suits to suppress civil rights reporting and established the constitutional framework that governs press freedom in the United States today.

Coaching

John Wooden, who coached UCLA men's basketball to 10 NCAA championships in 12 years including seven consecutive, famously spent the first practice of every season teaching players how to put on their socks and lace their shoes. His reasoning was practical: blisters and ankle problems begin with ill-fitting footwear. But the real lesson was about mastering fundamentals so completely that they became automatic. He called this approach the "Pyramid of Success" and believed preparation, not inspiration, was the wellspring of performance.

Sports

Track & Field

Sergey Bubka of the Soviet Union (later Ukraine) set the outdoor pole vault world record 17 times between 1983 and 1994, incrementally raising it centimeter by centimeter — a strategy that maximized his appearance fees, since major meets paid bonuses for world records. He was the first person to clear 6 meters outdoors (in 1985) and his outdoor record of 6.14 meters set in 1994 stood for nearly 26 years, until Armand Duplantis cleared 6.15m in 2020.

Softball

Jennie Finch, who pitched for the University of Arizona and the U.S. national team, once struck out baseball All-Stars Albert Pujols, Mike Piazza, and Brian Giles in a promotional challenge. Her dominance stemmed not just from speed but from the geometry of softball pitching: the underhand release from 43 feet gives a batter roughly 0.35 seconds to react — less time than facing a 95 mph baseball, because the release point is so much closer and the ball's trajectory follows a different visual plane than batters train for.

Mayo GAA

The 2004 All-Ireland Senior Football Championship final replay between Mayo and Kerry — a gripping contest decided by a single point — drew an attendance of 82,300 at Croke Park, one of the largest crowds for a GAA match in that era. Mayo had led at half-time and appeared poised to end their 53-year wait, but Kerry's experience and depth told in the final quarter. The defeat added another painful chapter to Mayo's championship story and cemented Kerry's reputation as the most resilient county in Gaelic football.

Religion & Philosophy

Catholicism

The Catholic doctrine of Purgatory — a state of purification after death for souls destined for heaven but not yet fully cleansed of the effects of sin — is most fully articulated in the Catechism of the Catholic Church and has scriptural grounding in 2 Maccabees 12:46. Dante's Purgatorio, written in the early 14th century, provided Western culture with its most enduring and detailed imaginative rendering of the doctrine, structuring it as a mountain with terraces corresponding to the seven deadly sins, each purged through penitential experience.

World Religion

In Islam, the concept of Zakat — one of the Five Pillars — requires Muslims who meet a minimum wealth threshold to give 2.5% of their accumulated savings annually to those in need. Unlike voluntary charity (sadaqa), Zakat is an obligatory act of worship with a precise calculation. Islamic economists estimate that if Zakat were fully implemented globally among eligible Muslims, it could generate hundreds of billions of dollars annually — making it one of the largest potential redistributive mechanisms in the world.

Physics

Quantum tunneling — the phenomenon where a particle passes through an energy barrier that classical physics says it cannot surmount — is not just theoretical. It is the operating principle behind flash memory, nuclear fusion inside stars, and the scanning tunneling microscope, which can image individual atoms by detecting the tiny tunneling current between tip and surface.

The ring structure of benzene stumped chemists for decades until August Kekulé reportedly dreamed in 1865 of a snake biting its own tail, inspiring him to propose a cyclic hexagonal arrangement. The insight unlocked organic chemistry and explained why benzene, despite its hydrogen-deficient formula, resists the addition reactions typical of other unsaturated compounds.

Astronomy

Dark matter — invisible to every telescope — is estimated to make up about 27% of the universe's total mass-energy content. Its gravitational effects on the rotation curves of galaxies and the bending of light around galaxy clusters are so consistent that most cosmologists treat its existence as settled, even though no direct detection has yet succeeded.

The first fiber-optic transatlantic telephone cable, TAT-8, was laid in 1988 and could carry 40,000 simultaneous calls — more than all previous transatlantic cables combined. The Black Death swept Europe between 1347 and 1351, killing an estimated 30–60% of the continent's population in four years. Japan

Bashō spent years walking the roads of Japan, composing poems in direct response to what he encountered, blending Zen Buddhist sensibility with acute observation of nature.

Ireland

India

The Indus Valley Civilization, flourishing from roughly 3300 to 1300 BCE in present-day Pakistan and northwestern India, had cities with grid-planned streets, multi-story brick houses with indoor plumbing, elaborate drainage systems, and standardized weights and measures. These achievements matched or exceeded those of contemporary Egypt and Mesopotamia, yet the civilization's writing system has never been deciphered.

South America

The Nazca Lines, etched into the coastal desert plateau of southern Peru between 500 BCE and 500 CE, include hundreds of geometric shapes and giant stylized animals — a hummingbird spanning 93 meters, a condor with a 130-meter wingspan. Their full scale is only comprehensible from the air, which is why they went unnoticed by the outside world until commercial aircraft began flying over the region in the 1920s.

Durham, NC

Business & Society

Economics

Thomas Piketty's 2013 book Capital in the Twenty-First Century used two centuries of data from 20 countries to argue that the rate of return on capital consistently exceeds economic growth — meaning wealth naturally concentrates at the top unless actively redistributed. The book sold over 2.5 million copies and reignited global debate about inequality that orthodox economics had largely set aside since the postwar decades.

Finance

The Dutch Tulip Mania of 1636–37 is history's most famous speculative bubble. At the peak, a single rare tulip bulb could fetch more than the price of a canal-front Amsterdam townhouse. The market collapsed within weeks, leaving many investors ruined — though economists still debate whether it constituted a true financial crisis or a more contained frenzy among a small circle of traders.

Personal Finance

The concept of an "emergency fund" — three to six months of living expenses in accessible savings — became a cornerstone of personal finance education in the mid-20th century. Its logic is direct: without a liquid buffer, any unexpected expense (medical bill, car repair, job loss) forces people into high-interest debt, compounding financial instability at precisely the moment it is most dangerous.

Management

Frederick Winslow Taylor, whose 1911 book The Principles of Scientific Management launched the industrial efficiency movement, believed every task could be optimized through systematic time-and-motion study. His methods transformed factory productivity and shaped management thinking for generations — but drew fierce criticism for treating workers as interchangeable components and stripping them of craft, judgment, and autonomy.

Constitutional Law

In Loving v. The decision invalidated similar anti-miscegenation laws in 16 other states and is now considered one of the most consequential civil rights rulings in the Court's history.

Coaching

Vince Lombardi, head coach of the Green Bay Packers from 1959 to 1967, won five NFL championships and the first two Super Bowls with a playbook so simple that opponents knew what was coming and still couldn't stop it. Lombardi believed relentless execution of fundamentals outperformed complexity every time — a philosophy captured in his maxim: "Chase perfection, and you can catch excellence."

Softball

George Hancock invented softball on Thanksgiving Day 1887, improvising an indoor game when Harvard and Yale alumni gathered at a Chicago boat club to hear football scores. He rolled up a boxing glove as a ball and chalked out bases on the floor — and within the hour had drafted the first rules. The sport spread rapidly under names like "indoor baseball" and "mushball" before the name "softball" was standardized in 1926.

Religion & Philosophy

Catholicism

The process of canonization — formally declaring someone a saint — has been reserved to the papacy since Pope Alexander III standardized it in 1173. The modern process requires verification of two miracles performed after death, a rigorous examination of the candidate's writings and life for doctrinal soundness, and the Pope's formal decree. The intermediate step, beatification, declares a person "Blessed" and permits limited veneration before full sainthood is granted.

World Religion

In Taoism, the concept of wu wei — often translated as "non-action" or "effortless action" — holds that the highest wisdom lies in aligning oneself with the natural flow of the universe rather than forcing outcomes through striving. The Tao Te Ching, attributed to the sage Laozi around the 6th century BCE, uses water as its central image: yielding, unresisting, yet capable of wearing away stone.

Science & Technology

Physics

Superconductivity — the complete disappearance of electrical resistance in certain materials — was discovered by Dutch physicist Heike Kamerlingh Onnes in 1911, when he found that mercury cooled below 4.2 Kelvin conducted electricity with zero resistance. Today, superconducting magnets power MRI machines and the Large Hadron Collider, and scientists are actively pursuing room-temperature superconductors that could transform energy transmission worldwide.

Chemistry

Chirality describes molecules that are mirror images of each other — like left and right hands — yet can behave completely differently in the body. Thalidomide, prescribed in the late 1950s as a sedative, was a chiral compound: one mirror-image form was therapeutic, but the other caused devastating birth defects. The tragedy accelerated the development of stereospecific drug synthesis and rigorous pharmaceutical testing worldwide.

Astronomy

Johannes Kepler formulated his three laws of planetary motion between 1609 and 1619, proving that planets orbit the sun in ellipses — not perfect circles as Ptolemy and Copernicus had assumed. His third law revealed that a planet's orbital period is mathematically linked to its distance from the sun, laying the quantitative groundwork that Newton later used to derive universal gravitation.

Technology

GPS works because of atomic clocks synchronized to within 20–30 nanoseconds across 24+ satellites. Without corrections for both special relativity (satellites' clocks run faster in lower gravity) and general relativity (they slow due to speed), GPS systems would drift by several kilometers each day — making Einstein's theories not just abstract physics, but essential software running in every smartphone.

Engineering

The Hoover Dam, completed in 1936 after five years of construction in the Nevada desert, used more concrete than any structure ever built at the time — enough to pave a two-lane highway from San Francisco to New York. During construction, 21,000 men worked in 140°F summer heat in rotating shifts around the clock; the project came in ahead of schedule and under budget.

Aerospace

The Wright Brothers' first powered flight at Kitty Hawk on December 17, 1903, lasted 12 seconds and covered 120 feet — shorter than the wingspan of a Boeing 747. Orville made the inaugural flight; Wilbur made the longest of the day at 59 seconds. The brothers had taught themselves aeronautical engineering by studying birds and the published failure reports of their predecessors.

History & Geography

American History

The Constitutional Convention of 1787 was called only to revise the Articles of Confederation, not to draft an entirely new government. Delegates quickly abandoned the original mandate and worked in total secrecy for four months in a sweltering Philadelphia building. James Madison kept the only comprehensive record of the debates — notes he declined to publish until after his death in 1836, nearly 50 years after the convention.

World History

The Mongol Empire, at its peak in the 13th century, was the largest contiguous land empire in history — stretching from Korea to Hungary and encompassing roughly 24 million square kilometers. Genghis Khan united dozens of warring nomadic tribes through a combination of military genius and ruthless discipline, creating a communications and trade network that stretched across Eurasia and briefly enabled the movement of people and goods from China to Eastern Europe.

Geography

The Nile River, flowing northward some 6,650 kilometers from central Africa to the Mediterranean, is one of the few major rivers in the world to run south-to-north. Its annual flooding deposited rich silt across Egypt's delta for millennia, creating one of the ancient world's most fertile agricultural zones and making possible the civilization that built the pyramids — without a single drop of rain for most of the year.

Japan

Origami traces its origins to the Heian period (794–1185 CE), when paper was so precious in Japan that folded paper was used almost exclusively in Shinto ceremonies and formal gift presentations. Purely recreational paper folding only became widespread once paper production expanded in the Edo period. The word origami — combining oru (to fold) and kami (paper) — did not enter common use until the 20th century.

Ireland

Daniel O'Connell, known as "The Liberator," achieved Catholic Emancipation in 1829 by organizing millions of Irish Catholics into a peaceful mass political movement — the Catholic Association — that the British government ultimately could not ignore. His model of nonviolent agitation and "moral force" politics influenced reform movements around the world, including Mahatma Gandhi, who cited O'Connell as an inspiration decades before India's independence campaign.

India

Ashoka the Great, emperor of the Maurya dynasty in the 3rd century BCE, witnessed such catastrophic bloodshed at the Battle of Kalinga — perhaps 100,000 dead — that he renounced warfare entirely and converted to Buddhism. He then propagated Buddhist ethics across his empire via stone edicts carved into pillars and rock faces across the subcontinent, some of which still stand today and represent the oldest surviving written inscriptions in India.

South America

The Amazon rainforest covers approximately 5.5 million square kilometers — roughly the size of the continental United States — and contains an estimated 10% of all species on Earth. A single hectare of Amazon jungle can contain more tree species than all of North America combined. The forest also generates its own rainfall: trees release so much water vapor that they effectively create a "flying river" of moisture that travels westward and waters much of South America.

Durham, NC

Durham's Hayti neighborhood was once known as the "Black Wall Street of the South." In the early 20th century it was anchored by North Carolina Mutual Life Insurance Company — founded in 1898 and eventually the largest Black-owned business in the United States — along with banks, theaters, pharmacies, and professional offices that formed one of the most prosperous Black business districts in the nation before urban renewal decimated it in the 1960s.

Business & Society

Economics

Gary Becker, who won the Nobel Prize in Economics in 1992, pioneered "economic imperialism" — applying rational-choice analysis to fields far beyond markets, including crime, marriage, discrimination, and addiction. He argued that people in all walks of life consistently respond to incentives, and his work showed that stiffer criminal penalties reduce crime rates — a then-controversial claim that reshaped criminology and public policy.

Finance

On Black Monday, October 19, 1987, the Dow Jones Industrial Average plunged 22.6% in a single trading day — the largest one-day percentage decline in its history, surpassing even 1929. The crash was amplified by computerized "portfolio insurance" programs that automatically triggered sell orders as prices fell, creating a feedback loop. The Federal Reserve responded swiftly, and unlike 1929, the broader economy absorbed the shock without entering depression.

Personal Finance

The principle of "paying yourself first" — automatically setting aside savings before spending any income — was popularized by George Clason in his 1926 book The Richest Man in Babylon, framing timeless financial lessons as parables of ancient Mesopotamia. Clason's core rule: save at least one-tenth of all you earn, consistently, before anything else. The book remains in continuous print a century after its publication.

Management

Henri Fayol, a French mining engineer who ran a coal company for 30 years, published his 14 Principles of Management in 1916 — covering division of work, authority, discipline, unity of command, and esprit de corps. Writing simultaneously with Frederick Taylor's "scientific management," Fayol focused on the overall organization rather than individual tasks, and his administrative theory became the foundation of business school management curricula for most of the 20th century.

Constitutional Law

In Gideon v. Wainwright (1963), the Supreme Court unanimously held that states must provide lawyers to criminal defendants who cannot afford their own. Clarence Earl Gideon — a drifter charged with breaking into a Florida pool hall — handwrote his petition from a prison cell, arguing his Sixth Amendment rights had been violated. The ruling transformed the American criminal justice system and led to the creation of public defender offices nationwide.

Coaching

Herb Brooks, who coached the 1980 U.S. Olympic ice hockey team to their stunning upset of the Soviet Union — the "Miracle on Ice" — deliberately avoided selecting the most talented players available. He wanted athletes who could sacrifice individual glory for a collective system, running his candidates through a grueling psychological screening process. "I'm not looking for the best players," he reportedly said. "I'm looking for the right ones."

Sports

Track & Field

Dick Fosbury revolutionized the high jump at the 1968 Mexico City Olympics by clearing the bar backwards and head-first — a technique so unconventional that spectators laughed when he first attempted it. He won gold with a then-Olympic record of 2.24 meters. Initially derided by coaches who tried to discourage the "Fosbury Flop," the technique is now used universally at every level of competition.

Softball

Dot Richardson, the shortstop who hit the first home run in Olympic softball history at the 1996 Atlanta Games, was simultaneously completing her medical residency. She maintained an orthopedic surgery career throughout her athletic prime, attending medical school during her years on the U.S. national team — a combination of professional and athletic achievement so rare that she was awarded the Sullivan Award as America's top amateur athlete that year.

Mayo GAA

The Sam Maguire Cup — the trophy awarded to the All-Ireland Senior Football Championship winner — is named after a Cork man who played Gaelic football for the London GAA while working as a post office clerk, and who was a senior figure in the Irish Republican Brotherhood. When Sam Maguire died in 1927, his friends and colleagues from the London GAA commissioned the trophy in his memory; Mayo, ironically, has come closest to winning it more times than almost any county without success since 1951.

Religion & Philosophy

Catholicism

The Society of Jesus (Jesuits), founded by St. Ignatius of Loyola in 1540, became the Catholic Church's most intellectually ambitious religious order. Within decades of their founding, Jesuits had established universities across Europe and the Americas, mapped coastlines of Asia and the Pacific, and served as court astronomers in China and Japan. Their educational mission produced over 20 popes' advisors, multiple Nobel laureates, and nearly 200 universities that still operate today.

World Religion

In Hinduism, dharma — one of the tradition's most central concepts — has no precise English equivalent. It encompasses duty, cosmic order, righteousness, and the right way of living, all simultaneously. Crucially, one's dharma is not universal: it varies according to one's stage of life, social role, and circumstances. A student's dharma differs from a ruler's; a parent's from a renunciant's — making it a highly contextual moral philosophy.
